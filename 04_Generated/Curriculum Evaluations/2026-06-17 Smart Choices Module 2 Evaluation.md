## Verdict: ✅ REVISE — with one **safety** revision that outranks the rest

The teaching design here is genuinely strong (engagement, rigor, relevance beat Module 1). But two things must be addressed before this is "ready," and one is a life-safety issue.

## Top gaps (highest impact first)

1. **⚠️ No overdose-response / harm-reduction content.** This module teaches that one pill kills — but never says what to do when someone takes one: **naloxone (Narcan), call 911, never use alone, signs of an overdose.** For a fentanyl module, omitting "how to keep your people alive" is the biggest gap. *(Note: whether to include harm reduction is a program/funder decision — but at minimum, naloxone awareness + "call 911" is life-safety, not ideology.)*
2. **Trauma-informed gap.** Many justice-impacted youth have **lost people to overdose**. The module needs a moment to acknowledge that, a grounding option, and to lean on *skills* over *fear* (scare-based messaging has weak/negative evidence). *(dimension 6)*
3. **No measurable objectives or formalized check.** *(dimensions 1, 9)*
4. **Evidence uncited**; fear-based framing not balanced with skills-based prevention. *(dimension 3)*

## Red-team — where it fails in a real room

- A youth who **lost a parent/friend to fentanyl** is hit hard by "the Eraser / death sentence" with no processing — can shut down or escalate.
- Pure fear can trigger **reactance** ("they're exaggerating") — the strongest skeptics tune out.
- The emoji codebook risks reading as a **how-to** without the firm "this is protective, not instructional" framing (mostly handled — reinforce it).
- If a kid discloses current use or a friend who uses, the facilitator has **no scripted response** and no harm-reduction info to offer.
