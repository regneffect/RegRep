# Module 8: Problem-Solving and Goal Setting
**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify and apply a structured, step-by-step problem-solving process to real-life challenges.
2. Distinguish between vague intentions and SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).
3. Write at least one personal SMART goal with defined action steps.
4. Practice peer coaching skills by helping a partner refine and strengthen a goal.
5. Recognize problem-solving and goal-setting as ongoing practices rather than one-time events.

---

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- NOT FOR PARTICIPANT DISTRIBUTION**

### Framework Alignment

This module addresses the following framework components:

- **SCBF 2.0 Domain:** Personal Effectiveness and Agency
- **SCBF 2.0 Competency Strand:** Critical Thinking, Planning, and Self-Direction
- **Harrington Model Connection:** This session builds on the Harrington continuum of skill acquisition -- moving participants from awareness (Modules 1-7) toward application and ownership. Problem-solving and goal-setting represent the pivot from reflective understanding to forward-looking action.
- **NAPS Alignment:** Standard 3 (Youth Voice and Leadership) -- participants exercise agency by identifying personal challenges and setting self-directed goals. Standard 4 (Supportive Relationships) -- the peer coaching activity builds mutual accountability and relational support.
- **YAYAPS Integration:** Youth Agency and Youth-Adult Partnership principles are embedded in the peer coaching structure, which positions young people as competent advisors to one another rather than passive recipients of adult instruction.

### Session Design Rationale

The source content for this module runs approximately 60 minutes. The expansion to 90 minutes is achieved by:

- Adding a 10-minute opening warm-up with a concrete problem-solving scenario (Activity 1)
- Extending the SMART goal-setting worksheet time from 10 to 20 minutes, allowing participants to draft, revise, and refine rather than rush
- Adding 10 minutes of structured peer coaching practice (Activity 4), which deepens both the coaching and goal-setting skill sets
- Extending the closing reflection by 5 minutes to include written STAR journaling alongside verbal sharing
- Building in 5 additional minutes of transition and debrief time across segments

### Practical Skills Emphasis

This is a hands-on, practical skills module. Participants should leave this session with a tangible product: a completed personal SMART goal with action steps. Resist the temptation to turn this into an abstract lecture about planning. Keep the energy active and applied. If participants finish early, have them set a second goal rather than waiting.

### Common Facilitation Challenges

- **Vague goals:** Participants often write goals like "do better in school" or "be healthier." Use probing questions to help them get specific without doing the thinking for them. The peer coaching activity is designed to address this.
- **Perfectionism and avoidance:** Some participants may resist writing a goal down because they are afraid of failing. Normalize revision. Emphasize that a goal is a draft, not a contract.
- **Problem-solving feels abstract:** Ground every step in a concrete, relatable example. The "Lost Phone" scenario in Activity 1 is deliberately low-stakes and universal.
- **Peer coaching goes off-track:** The Question Card (Handout 8C) provides structure. Circulate actively during the peer coaching activity and redirect pairs who are chatting rather than coaching.

### Room Setup

Arrange seating in pairs or clusters of four. Participants will need a writing surface. If using a community space with limited table access, provide clipboards or hard-backed folders.

## Activities

### Welcome and Warm-Up (10 minutes)

**Facilitator says:**

> "Welcome back, everyone. Today we are going to work on two skills that connect everything we have been building so far: solving problems and setting goals. These are skills you already use every day, even if you have never thought of them that way. We are going to make them sharper and more intentional."

**Opening Check-In (3 minutes):**

Go around the room (or use a popcorn format for larger groups). Each participant answers one of the following:

- "What is one small problem you solved this week -- even something tiny?"
- "What is one thing you are working toward right now, big or small?"

**Quick Scenario (5 minutes):**

Present this scenario verbally or write it on the board:

> *"You realize your phone is not in your pocket. You had it 30 minutes ago. You are at a community event with a lot of people around. What do you do?"*

Ask participants to call out their responses. As they share, notice the natural steps: they retrace their steps, they check likely places, they ask people, they consider possibilities, they take action.

**Facilitator says:**

> "Look at what you just did -- without any instruction, you followed a process. You figured out the problem, considered options, and decided on an action. That is problem-solving. Today we are going to take that instinct and turn it into a tool you can use on the bigger, harder stuff."

**Transition:** Distribute **Handout 8A: The Problem-Solving Pathway**.

---

### Activity 1: The Problem-Solving Pathway (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Teach a repeatable, structured approach to problem-solving that participants can apply independently.

#### The Five Steps

Write these on the board or flip chart. Walk through each one using the "Lost Phone" scenario as a running example:

| Step | Name | What You Do |
|------|------|-------------|
| 1 | **Name the Problem** | Say exactly what is wrong -- be specific, not vague |
| 2 | **Explore the Causes** | Ask "why" and "how did this happen" -- look beneath the surface |
| 3 | **Brainstorm Options** | List every possible action, even ones that seem unlikely |
| 4 | **Choose and Act** | Pick the best option based on what you know and what you can control |
| 5 | **Review and Adjust** | After acting, check: did it work? What would you do differently? |

**Worked Example (7 minutes):**

Walk through the Lost Phone scenario step by step on the board:

1. **Name the Problem:** "My phone is missing. I last had it 30 minutes ago at the snack table."
2. **Explore the Causes:** "Maybe I set it down and forgot. Maybe it fell out of my pocket. Maybe someone picked it up by mistake."
3. **Brainstorm Options:** "Retrace my steps. Ask the event organizer. Call my phone from someone else's phone. Check lost and found. Post on the event group chat."
4. **Choose and Act:** "First, I will call my phone. If no answer, I will retrace my steps to the snack table. Then I will ask the organizer."
5. **Review and Adjust:** "I found it under a napkin at the snack table. Next time, I will keep my phone in a zipped pocket at events."

**Individual Practice (10 minutes):**

**Facilitator says:**

> "Now it is your turn. Think of a real problem you are dealing with right now -- or one you dealt with recently. It does not have to be dramatic. It could be a conflict with a friend, trouble getting to work on time, or figuring out how to pay for something. Use the five steps on your handout to work through it."

Give participants 10 minutes of quiet writing time. Circulate and offer support. Use these coaching prompts if participants are stuck:

- "Can you make the problem more specific? Instead of 'school is hard,' what exactly is hard?"
- "What are three things you could do about this -- even if some of them are a stretch?"
- "Which option is most within your control right now?"

**Brief share (3 minutes):** Ask two or three volunteers to share their problem and one option they identified. Affirm effort and specificity.

### Activity 2: What Makes a Goal SMART? (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Introduce the SMART goal framework and help participants distinguish between vague wishes and actionable goals.

**Facilitator says:**

> "Now that we have a tool for solving problems, let us talk about where you want to go. A lot of people say things like 'I want to do better' or 'I want to be successful.' Those are good intentions, but they are not goals -- not yet. A goal needs to be specific enough that you know when you have reached it."

**Introduce SMART on the board:**

| Letter | Stands For | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **S** | Specific | What exactly will you do? |
| **M** | Measurable | How will you know you did it? |
| **A** | Achievable | Is this realistic given your current situation? |
| **R** | Relevant | Does this matter to you? Is it connected to something you care about? |
| **T** | Time-bound | By when? What is your deadline? |

**Compare and Contrast (5 minutes):**

Show these pairs on the board. Ask participants which is the SMART goal and why:

| Vague Intention | SMART Goal |
|-----------------|------------|
| "I want to get healthier." | "I will walk for 20 minutes three times a week for the next month." |
| "I want to save money." | "I will save $25 per week for 8 weeks by packing lunch instead of buying it." |
| "I want to read more." | "I will read one book per month for the next three months, starting with a book I choose this week." |

For each pair, ask:
- "What makes the second one better?"
- "How would you know if you succeeded?"
- "What is the deadline?"

**Transition:** Distribute **Handout 8B: My SMART Goal Worksheet**.

**Facilitator says:**

> "Now you are going to write your own. This is not a test -- it is a draft. You can change it. The point is to get something down on paper that is specific enough to act on."

---

### Activity 3: My SMART Goal Worksheet (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Participants draft a personal SMART goal with concrete action steps.

**Individual Work Time (20 minutes):**

Participants work independently on Handout 8B. This is the core product of the session -- protect this time. Play quiet background music if it helps focus, but keep the room calm and focused.

**Facilitator circulates** and uses these coaching prompts:

- "Can you make the 'what' even more specific?"
- "What would it look like if you measured this -- what number or milestone would you track?"
- "What is one obstacle that might get in the way? What would you do about it?"
- "Who in your life could support you in this?"
- "Is this timeline realistic for you? Too long? Too short?"

**If participants finish early:**
- Have them add a second goal on the back of the sheet
- Ask them to write a brief paragraph: "Why does this goal matter to me?"
- Invite them to think about how the problem-solving steps connect to their goal

### Activity 4: Peer Coaching Practice (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Participants practice helping someone else strengthen a goal, building mutual accountability and deepening their own goal-setting skills.

**Setup (2 minutes):**

**Facilitator says:**

> "One of the most powerful things you can do is help someone else get clear on what they want. It is not about telling them what to do -- it is about asking the right questions. You are about to be a coach for your partner."

Pair participants up. Distribute **Handout 8C: Peer Coaching Question Card**. Assign roles: Person A is the "Goal Setter" first, Person B is the "Coach" first. They will switch halfway.

**Round 1 (5 minutes):**

- Person A shares their SMART goal from Handout 8B.
- Person B listens and uses the questions on the card to help Person A strengthen the goal.
- Person B does NOT give advice or tell Person A what to do. They only ask questions.

**Switch (1 minute):** Reverse roles.

**Round 2 (5 minutes):**

- Person B shares their SMART goal.
- Person A coaches using the question card.

**Debrief (2 minutes):**

Ask the full group:
- "What was it like to be the coach?"
- "Did your partner's questions help you see something you missed?"
- "What made the coaching helpful -- or not?"

---

> **--- HANDOUT 8C: Peer Coaching Question Card ---**
>
> **Your Role as a Coach**
>
> Your job is to help your partner think more clearly about their goal. You are NOT giving advice. You are asking good questions and listening carefully.
>
> **Rules for Coaching:**
> - Listen more than you talk
> - Ask open-ended questions (not yes/no)
> - Do not judge or criticize the goal
> - If your partner gets stuck, try a different question
> - Encourage them -- this takes courage
>
> ---
>
> **Coaching Questions to Use:**
>
> *Start here:*
> - "Tell me about your goal. What are you working toward?"
> - "Why does this matter to you?"
>
> *To help them get specific:*
> - "What exactly will that look like when you achieve it?"
> - "How will you know you have made progress?"
> - "Can you break that down into a smaller first step?"
>
> *To help them think about obstacles:*
> - "What might get in the way?"
> - "What has stopped you before when you tried something like this?"
> - "Who could help you if you get stuck?"
>
> *To help them commit:*
> - "What is your first action step -- and when will you do it?"
> - "How will you hold yourself accountable?"
> - "On a scale of 1 to 10, how confident are you that you will follow through? What would make it a higher number?"
>
> ---
>
> **After Coaching, Write:**
>
> One thing my coach helped me see: _______________________________________________________________
>
> One change I made to my goal after being coached: _______________________________________________________________
>
> **--- END HANDOUT 8C ---**

## STAR Reflection Prompts

**Duration:** 10 minutes (within the 1:15 -- 1:25 segment)

**Facilitator says:**

> "Before we close, let us take a few minutes to reflect on today. Think about what you experienced, what you did, and what came out of it. Use the four prompts below to write a short reflection. Then we will hear from a few volunteers."

**Written Reflection (5 minutes):**

Participants write in their journals, on the back of a handout, or on a separate sheet:

---

**STAR Reflection -- Module 8**

**Situation:** Describe where you were and what you were doing during today's session. What were you working on?

> *Example: "I was sitting with my partner working on my SMART goal during the goal-setting activity."*

**Task:** What were you trying to accomplish? What was the challenge or assignment in front of you?

> *Example: "I needed to turn my general idea about improving my grades into a goal I could actually measure and act on."*

**Action:** What specific steps did you take? What did you write, say, or decide?

> *Example: "I used the SMART criteria to rewrite my goal. My coach asked me how I would know I had succeeded, which made me add a specific grade target and a weekly study schedule."*

**Result:** What was the outcome? What did you learn, create, or realize? What will you do next?

> *Example: "I ended up with a goal I actually believe I can follow through on. I am going to check in with my partner next week to report on my first step."*

---

**Voluntary Sharing (5 minutes):**

Invite two or three participants to share any part of their STAR reflection. Affirm specificity and honesty. Highlight moments where the problem-solving steps or SMART criteria made a difference.

**Facilitator prompts for sharing:**
- "Who is willing to share one thing they realized during the reflection?"
- "Did anyone change their goal after the peer coaching? What happened?"
- "What is one thing you are taking away from today that you will actually use?"

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Informal Assessment (during session)

Use the following observation checklist while circulating during Activities 1, 3, and 4:

| Indicator | Look For | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Problem identification** | Participant names a specific, concrete problem (not vague or overly broad) | |
| **Process application** | Participant uses the five steps in order; can articulate why each step matters | |
| **SMART criteria application** | Participant's written goal addresses all five criteria (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound) | |
| **Peer coaching quality** | Coach asks open-ended questions; avoids giving unsolicited advice; listens actively | |
| **Revised goal quality** | After coaching, the goal is more specific or actionable than the first draft | |
| **STAR reflection depth** | Reflection moves beyond surface description; includes genuine insight or planned action | |

### Exit Ticket (optional, 2 minutes during closing)

If using a quick written check, ask participants to answer on a sticky note:

1. "Write one SMART goal you set today in one sentence."
2. "Name one step in the problem-solving process that was most useful to you and explain why."

Collect sticky notes as participants leave. Review for patterns and to identify participants who may need follow-up support.

### Portfolio Artifact

The completed **Handout 8B: My SMART Goal Worksheet** serves as the portfolio artifact for this module. Encourage participants to keep it in their folder or portfolio. If your program uses progress tracking, photograph or copy the worksheets for documentation.

## Community Partner Integration

### Before the Session

- **Invite a community partner to share a brief story (3-5 minutes during the warm-up or closing)** about a time they used problem-solving or goal-setting in their professional life. Good candidates: small business owners, program coordinators, community organizers, mentors. Ask them to keep it concrete and personal rather than abstract.
- **Coordinate with workforce development partners** (e.g., local workforce boards, job training programs) to align the SMART goal activity with career-readiness resources they offer. Provide them with a copy of Handout 8B so they can reference participants' goals in future interactions.

### During the Session

- **If a community partner is present,** position them as a third coaching voice during Activity 4. They can circulate and offer questions (not answers) to pairs working on goals. Brief them on the coaching rules from Handout 8C.
- **If your program has an established mentorship component,** connect the peer coaching practice to the mentorship model. Frame it as: "You just practiced what mentors do -- ask good questions and believe in someone's ability to figure things out."

### After the Session

- **Follow-up check-in:** Schedule a brief goal check-in during the next session or between sessions. This can be done via text, email, a program app, or in person. The check-in should ask: "What was your first action step? Did you do it? What happened?"
- **Community partner follow-through:** Share (with participant permission) broad goal themes with community partners so they can offer relevant support. For example, if several participants set education-related goals, a partnering tutoring organization can be connected.
- **Goal wall or display:** If your community space allows, create a voluntary "Goals in Progress" display where participants can post their SMART goal (or a shortened version). This creates public accountability and celebrates commitment.
- **Parent/family engagement:** Consider sending home a simplified version of the SMART goal worksheet and inviting family members to set a goal alongside the participant. This extends the skill practice beyond the session.

## Closing and Next Steps (5 minutes)

**Facilitator says:**

> "Today you practiced two skills that connect to everything we do in this program and beyond: solving problems step by step and setting goals that are clear enough to actually reach. You now have a tool for each one, and you practiced being a coach to someone else -- which is a skill in itself."

> "Your assignment between now and our next session is simple: take your first action step. Just the first one. When we come back together, I will ask how it went -- and we will use the problem-solving steps if you ran into any obstacles."

**Preview next session** briefly if the topic is known.

**Final round (optional):** Each participant says one word that describes how they feel about their goal (e.g., "motivated," "nervous," "ready," "curious").
