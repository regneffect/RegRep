# Module 2: Who Am I?
**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify at least five distinct dimensions of their personal identity (e.g., cultural background, interests, values, roles, aspirations).
2. Articulate three or more personal strengths and describe how those strengths show up in their daily lives.
3. Create an identity map that visually represents who they are across multiple life domains.
4. Practice sharing aspects of their identity with peers in a structured, supportive small-group setting.
5. Reflect on how self-awareness connects to building stronger relationships within their community.

---

## Materials Needed

- Printed "Who Am I?" Identity Map worksheets (one per participant)
- Printed "My Strengths Spotlight" handout (one per participant)
- Colored markers, crayons, or colored pencils (enough for each participant to have multiple colors)
- Large chart paper or poster paper (one sheet per small group of 4-5)

- Name tags or name tents (if participants are still building familiarity)

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- This section is for facilitator use only. Do not distribute to participants.**

### Framework Alignment

This module maps to the **SCBF 2.0** framework's foundational domain of Self-Awareness and Identity Development. The session operationalizes the concept of "shared lived experience" (Harrington, 2019) by guiding participants to explore their own identities before connecting those identities to peers in later modules. Per **NAPS** Quality Standards, this session addresses Element 3.1 (Youth Development -- Supporting Social-Emotional Growth) and Element 4.2 (Culturally Responsive Programming).

### Key Concepts for the Facilitator

- **Identity is multi-dimensional.** Participants may initially default to surface-level descriptors (name, age, grade). Your role is to model depth by sharing your own identity dimensions and gently prompting beyond the obvious.
- **Shared lived experience.** This module builds the foundation for the "shared lived experience" concept that becomes explicit in Modules 4-6. At this stage, do NOT label the concept for participants. Instead, let organic connections emerge during small-group sharing. Participants will begin noticing overlaps in their identity maps naturally.
- **Strengths-based framing.** All activities in this module are designed around asset-based language. Redirect any deficit-oriented self-talk ("I'm bad at...") toward reframing ("Something I'm still working on is...").
- **Cultural humility.** Some participants may include cultural, religious, or gender-identity dimensions that feel vulnerable. Reinforce community agreements from Module 1. Never require participants to share anything they are not comfortable with.

### Common Pitfalls

- **Rushing the identity map.** The identity map is the anchor activity. If you are short on time, compress the closing rather than cutting identity map work time. Depth matters more than speed here.
- **Over-sharing as facilitator.** Model vulnerability at an appropriate level. Share enough to normalize the exercise but not so much that participants feel they must match your depth.
- **Comparison culture.** During small-group sharing, watch for participants who begin comparing identities competitively ("I have more strengths than you"). Redirect toward curiosity: "What can you learn from how someone else sees themselves?"
- **Quiet participants.** Some participants may be reluctant to share in groups. The worksheet provides a private, written channel. Affirm that the written work is complete participation -- sharing aloud is an invitation, not a requirement.

- [ ] Art supplies (markers, colored pencils) distributed to each table
- [ ] Chart paper and sticky notes at each table group
- [ ] Timer accessible and tested
- [ ] Optional: calming or reflective background music cued up for individual work time

## Activities

### Activity 1: Identity Mapping
**Duration:** 25 minutes
**Format:** Individual guided work with brief facilitator modeling
**Materials:** "Who Am I?" Identity Map worksheet, colored markers or pencils, pens

**Instructions:**

1. **(3 min) Facilitator Modeling.** Display a sample identity map (your own or a fictional example) on chart paper. Walk participants through the structure: a center circle with their name, surrounded by connected bubbles representing different parts of who they are. Show at least six dimensions: things you value, roles you play, places that matter to you, people who shaped you, things you are good at, and hopes for your future. Emphasize that there are no wrong answers.

2. **(2 min) Distribute and Orient.** Hand out the "Who Am I?" Identity Map worksheet. Read through the category prompts together. Clarify that participants can add their own categories beyond those listed.

3. **(18 min) Individual Work Time.** Invite participants to complete their identity maps. Encourage use of color, drawings, symbols, or words -- whatever feels right. Circulate quietly. Offer gentle prompts to participants who seem stuck:
   - "What is something you do that makes you feel most like yourself?"
   - "Who is someone that has shaped the way you see the world?"
   - "If your best friend described you, what would they say?"
   - "What is one thing about you that people might be surprised to learn?"

4. **(2 min) Pause and Appreciate.** Ask participants to set down their markers and take a moment to look at what they created. Prompt: "Notice how many different things make up who you are. No single word or label could capture all of this."

**Participant Handout Content:**

---

> **Who Am I? -- Identity Map**
>
> *Directions:* Write your name in the center circle. In each surrounding bubble, write or draw something that represents a part of who you are. Use the prompts below to get started, and feel free to add your own categories. Use colors, symbols, or images -- make this map yours.
>
> **Center Circle:** My Name
>
> **Surrounding Bubbles (fill in as many as you like):**
>
> - **My Culture & Background:** Where does your family come from? What traditions matter to you?
> - **My Roles:** What roles do you play? (Examples: sibling, friend, teammate, artist, caregiver)
> - **My Values:** What matters most to you? What do you stand for?
> - **My Interests & Passions:** What do you love to do? What could you talk about for hours?
> - **My Strengths:** What are you good at? What do people come to you for?
> - **People Who Shaped Me:** Who has had the biggest impact on who you are today?
> - **Places That Matter:** What places feel like "home" or feel important to your story?
> - **My Goals & Dreams:** Where do you see yourself going? What do you hope for?
> - **Add Your Own:** ____________________________________________
>
> *Remember: There are no wrong answers. This map is about YOU -- all the different parts that make you who you are.*

### Activity 2: My Strengths Spotlight
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Individual reflection (8 min) followed by partner exchange (7 min)
**Materials:** "My Strengths Spotlight" handout, pens

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min) Frame the Activity.** Tell participants: "Now that we've mapped out many parts of our identity, let's zoom in on one area -- our strengths. Strengths are not just things you're good at in school. They include how you treat people, how you solve problems, how you show up when things get hard, and what makes you someone others can count on."

2. **(6 min) Individual Strengths Reflection.** Distribute the "My Strengths Spotlight" handout. Ask participants to complete it individually. Circulate and offer support. For participants who struggle to name strengths, try:
   - "Think about a time someone thanked you or said something nice about what you did. What strength were you showing?"
   - "What do your friends or family come to you for help with?"

3. **(7 min) Partner Exchange.** Have participants pair up (use a quick pairing method: "Turn to the person next to you" or "Find someone wearing a similar color"). Each partner has 3 minutes to share one strength and the story behind it. The listening partner practices active listening and then offers one thing they noticed or appreciated. Switch after 3 minutes. Reserve 1 minute at the end for both partners to thank each other.

### Activity 3: Small Group Sharing Circle
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Small groups of 4-5 participants, structured sharing rounds
**Materials:** Identity Map worksheets (completed), chart paper, sticky notes, markers

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min) Set Up Sharing Norms.** Bring attention back to the whole group. Review sharing expectations:
   - "Share what you are comfortable sharing. You can pass on any round."
   - "When someone is sharing, give them your full attention."
   - "What is shared in the circle stays in the circle."
   - "After someone shares, respond with curiosity -- ask a question or share what you appreciated."

2. **(10 min) Structured Sharing Rounds.** In their table groups, participants complete two sharing rounds:

   **Round 1 -- Identity Snapshot (5 min):** Each person shares two things from their identity map -- one that is visible to most people and one that might surprise others. After each share, the group offers one brief affirmation or follow-up question.

   **Round 2 -- Strength in Action (5 min):** Each person shares one strength and a brief example of how they have used it. Group members write a sticky note to that person with something they appreciated or connected with and hand it to them.

3. **(3 min) Group Connections.** Give each group a piece of chart paper. Ask them to identify and write down 2-3 things they discovered they have in common. Post these on the wall. Briefly acknowledge the patterns that are emerging across groups.

## STAR Reflection Prompts

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Whole group, facilitator-led discussion

Use the following STAR-structured prompts to guide the closing reflection. Select 2-3 based on group energy and time. Invite volunteers to respond aloud, and affirm that internal reflection is also valuable.

### Prompt 1: Discovering Something New
- **Situation:** Today you spent time thinking deeply about your own identity and strengths.
- **Task:** You were asked to map out parts of who you are and name what you are good at.
- **Action:** What is one thing you wrote down or thought about today that you had never really put into words before?
- **Result:** How does it feel to have that part of yourself named or visible to you now?

### Prompt 2: Sharing With Others
- **Situation:** During the small group sharing circle, you had the chance to tell others about parts of your identity.
- **Task:** You chose something to share with your group -- something visible and something that might surprise people.
- **Action:** What did you decide to share, and what made you choose that?
- **Result:** What happened when you shared it? Did anything your group members said or did stand out to you?

### Prompt 3: Finding Common Ground
- **Situation:** At the end of the sharing circle, your group looked for things you had in common.
- **Task:** You needed to find connections across different people's identity maps.
- **Action:** What common ground did your group discover?
- **Result:** Why do you think finding those connections matters when you are part of a community?

### Prompt 4: Strengths Beyond School
- **Situation:** Today's strengths activity asked you to think about strengths in all parts of your life, not just academics.
- **Task:** You had to name strengths and describe how they show up in real situations.
- **Action:** What strength did you identify that you do not usually get recognized for?
- **Result:** How might naming that strength change the way you see yourself going forward?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Formative Assessment (During Session)

| What to Observe | What It Tells You | How to Respond |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Participant completes 5+ bubbles on the identity map with specific detail | Strong self-awareness engagement; participant is connecting with the activity | Affirm depth: "I can see how much thought you put into this." |
| Participant fills in only 1-2 bubbles or writes very general responses | May need more scaffolding or may be uncomfortable with self-disclosure | Offer private prompts; normalize that some bubbles are harder than others |
| Participant names 3 strengths with concrete examples | Solid grasp of strengths-based self-reflection | Encourage them to support peers who are struggling |
| Participant cannot name any strengths or uses only deficit language | May have internalized negative self-narratives; needs strengths reframing | Use the "someone else" approach: "What would your best friend say you are good at?" |
| Active participation in small-group sharing with follow-up questions | Building relational trust and community connection | Acknowledge the group dynamic publicly as a model for others |
| Participant passes on sharing but completes written work | Participation through writing is valid; they may need more time to build trust | Respect the pass; check in privately after the session |

### Exit Ticket (Final 2 minutes of session)

On a sticky note or index card, each participant writes:

1. **One word** that describes how you feel after today's session.
2. **One thing** you learned about yourself today.
3. **One question** you still have about identity or strengths (or write "none").

Collect exit tickets as participants leave. Review before Module 3 to identify participants who may need additional support or follow-up.

## Community Partner Integration

### Before the Session
- **Invite a community partner representative** (e.g., mentor, community organization leader, local business owner) to participate in the session as a guest. Ask them to prepare a brief (3-minute) identity map of their own to share during the session framing segment (0:10-0:15). This models self-reflection from an adult community member and validates the exercise.
- **Coordinate with partner organizations** to identify local identity-affirming events, cultural programming, or mentorship opportunities that can be referenced during the Community Connection segment.

### During the Session (Community Connection Segment, 1:20-1:25)
- Share information about upcoming community partner events or programs that connect to identity exploration (e.g., cultural heritage celebrations, mentorship sign-ups, leadership programs, storytelling events).
- If a community partner guest is present, invite them to share one sentence about why self-awareness matters in their work or life.
- Distribute any partner-provided flyers, sign-up sheets, or resource cards.

### After the Session
- Share anonymized, aggregate themes from the session with community partners (with program coordinator approval) to help partners tailor their offerings to participant interests and strengths. Examples: "Many participants identified artistic expression as a core strength" or "Several participants expressed interest in cultural heritage exploration."
- Connect individual participants (with their consent) to community partners whose programs align with the strengths or interests identified during the session.
- Invite community partners to serve as "Strengths Mentors" in future modules, pairing with participants who share similar identity dimensions or professional interests.

### Pedagogical Notes

- The progression from individual work (identity map) to paired exchange (strengths) to small group sharing (circle) follows a deliberate scaffolding of vulnerability. This mirrors the SCBF 2.0 framework's principle of "concentric trust building" -- participants develop comfort with self-disclosure in progressively larger social configurations.
- STAR reflection prompts are calibrated to this module's content. The Situation and Task components ground participants in the concrete activities they completed, while Action and Result components invite metacognitive processing of the experience.

### Adaptation Notes for Facilitators

- **If participants finish the identity map early:** Invite them to add a second layer -- connecting lines between bubbles that are related to each other (e.g., "my culture" connects to "my values" connects to "people who shaped me"). This deepens the complexity of their self-concept.
- **If the group is very large (20+):** Conduct the sharing circle in two simultaneous groups with a co-facilitator. Maintain the same structure and norms.
- **If a participant becomes emotional:** Identity work can surface difficult experiences. Have a private space identified in advance. Offer the participant the choice to step out with a trusted adult or to continue at their own pace. Follow up individually after the session.
- **Connecting to Module 3:** Participants will carry their identity maps forward into Module 3 (My Story, My Voice), where they will select one dimension of their identity to develop into a personal narrative. Remind participants to keep their worksheets in their program folders.
