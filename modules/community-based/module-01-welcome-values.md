# Module 1: Welcome + Values Activity

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Introduce themselves to peers using a structured prompt that highlights personal strengths and interests.
2. Collaboratively develop a set of group agreements that establish norms for respectful participation throughout the program.
3. Identify at least three personal core values and articulate why those values are important to them.
4. Connect their personal values to the goals of the group and the broader community.
5. Demonstrate active listening and affirming communication during group discussion.

---

- Chart paper or large poster paper (3-4 sheets)

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- This section is for facilitator use only. Do not distribute to participants.**

### Framework Alignment

This module aligns with the **SCBF 2.0** framework's foundational phase, which emphasizes building relational trust and establishing psychological safety before introducing skill-building content. The session draws on **Harrington's Community Learning Model** (Harrington, 2019), specifically the principle that belonging must precede engagement. **NAPS Standard 2 (Relationships and Interactions)** and **NAPS Standard 4 (Youth Voice and Leadership)** are addressed through the collaborative agreements process and the values identification activity.

### Setup Instructions

1. **Room arrangement:** Set up seating in a circle or U-shape. Avoid classroom-style rows. If tables are needed, use small clusters of 4-5 seats to facilitate small-group work. Leave an open area at the front for chart paper posting.
2. **Materials station:** Pre-set a materials station with markers, sticky notes, and name tag supplies so participants can access them during arrival.
3. **Values cards:** Pre-sort values card decks into individual sets. Each set should contain 30-40 value words (e.g., Family, Honesty, Respect, Education, Faith, Independence, Creativity, Service, Justice, Fun). Print on cardstock for durability. Include 3-5 blank cards per set so participants can write in values not represented.
4. **Chart paper:** Pre-write the heading "Our Group Agreements" on one sheet. Leave the rest blank for group input. On a second sheet, write "Values We Share" as a header for the closing gallery.
5. **Arrival buffer:** Plan for 5-10 minutes of informal arrival time. Community-based settings often have staggered arrivals. Use background music and the name tag activity to productively occupy early arrivers.

### Key Concepts to Emphasize

- **Psychological safety:** This session sets the emotional tone for every session that follows. Prioritize warmth, low-pressure participation, and genuine curiosity about each participant.
- **Peer-to-peer dynamic:** Position yourself as a facilitator, not a teacher. Use language like "we" and "our group" rather than "my class" or "you all need to."
- **Values as neutral:** During the values sort, emphasize that no value is better or worse than another. The goal is self-awareness, not hierarchy.
- **Voluntary depth:** Participants choose how much to share. Never pressure someone to disclose personal stories. Offer multiple entry points (writing, drawing, speaking, gesturing).

### Common Pitfalls

- **Rushing the agreements:** Groups need genuine ownership of their norms. Do not pre-write agreements and ask for rubber-stamp approval. Facilitate authentic negotiation even if it takes longer than planned. Draw extra time from Activity 3 if necessary.
- **Tokenizing participants:** During introductions, avoid singling out participants based on visible identity markers. Let participants define themselves.
- **Skipping the debrief:** The STAR reflection at the close is where learning consolidates. Protect this time.
- **Over-facilitating the values sort:** Let participants sit with ambiguity. Resist the urge to define values for them.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Icebreaker Introductions -- "Three Words and a Why"

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Whole group (circle)
**Materials:** Name tags (already created during arrival)

**Instructions:**

1. Gather participants into a standing or seated circle. If the group is larger than 15, split into two concentric circles.
2. Introduce the prompt: "We're going to go around the circle and share three things: your name, the name you want us to call you if it's different, and three words that describe who you are outside of this room. Then tell us why one of those words matters to you."
3. Model the activity yourself first. Example: "I'm Ms. Daniels -- you can call me Coach D. My three words are curious, outdoors, and loyal. Loyal matters to me because I grew up watching my grandmother show up for her neighbors no matter what, and I try to carry that with me."
4. Move around the circle. Thank each person after they share. Use brief, genuine affirmations ("Thank you for sharing that," "That's a powerful word," "I can tell that matters to you").
5. If a participant passes, say: "That's okay. If something comes to you later, we'd love to hear it." Do not pressure.
6. After the full round, offer one observation: "I'm noticing some themes already -- I heard a lot about [family/creativity/strength/etc.]. That tells me something great about this group."

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Welcome!**
>
> Create your name tag at the materials station. Write the name you want us to use.
>
> When it's your turn in the circle, share:
> - Your name (and what you'd like to be called)
> - Three words that describe who you are
> - Why one of those words matters to you
>
> There are no wrong answers. Share what feels right.

### Activity 2: Building Our Group Agreements

**Duration:** 25 minutes
**Format:** Small groups (4-5), then whole group
**Materials:** Chart paper (pre-headed "Our Group Agreements"), markers, sticky notes

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min) Frame the activity:** "Before we go deeper, we need to decide together how we want to treat each other in this space. These aren't rules I'm giving you -- these are agreements we make as a group. They belong to all of us."
2. **(3 min) Individual brainstorm:** Hand out sticky notes. Ask: "Think about a time you were in a group that felt safe, respectful, and productive. What made it that way? Write one idea per sticky note. Try to come up with at least two."
3. **(7 min) Small-group sort:** Have participants form groups of 4-5. Each group combines their sticky notes, looks for themes, and selects their top 3-4 agreements to propose to the whole group. Each group writes their proposals on chart paper in large letters.
4. **(10 min) Whole-group negotiation:** Each small group presents their proposals. Facilitator captures them on the main "Our Group Agreements" chart paper. Look for overlaps and consolidate. Invite discussion: "Does anyone want to add to this?" "Is there anything here that wouldn't work for you?" "What does [agreement] look like in practice?"
5. **(3 min) Finalize and commit:** Read the final list aloud. Ask: "Can everyone commit to these? If you're in, give a thumbs up." Post the agreements prominently in the meeting space. Let participants know these can be revisited and revised in future sessions.

**Facilitator tip:** Common agreements that emerge include: "Respect different opinions," "What's shared here stays here," "Step up / step back," "It's okay to disagree," "Phones on silent." If key safety agreements do not emerge organically (e.g., confidentiality), gently prompt: "What about when someone shares something personal? How do we want to handle that?"

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Building Our Group Agreements**
>
> Great groups don't happen by accident. They happen because people decide how they want to show up for each other.
>
> **Step 1 -- Think Back**
> Think about a time you were in a group that felt safe and respectful. What made it work? Write your ideas on sticky notes (one idea per note).
>
> **Step 2 -- Team Up**
> Join your small group. Share your sticky notes, find common themes, and choose your group's top 3-4 proposed agreements.
>
> **Step 3 -- Decide Together**
> We'll hear from every group and build one shared list of agreements for our time together. These agreements belong to all of us, and we can revisit them at any point.

### Transition + Stretch Break

**Duration:** 5 minutes

**Instructions:**

1. Invite participants to stand and stretch. Offer a quick movement option: "Shake out your hands, roll your shoulders, take a deep breath."
2. While participants stretch, distribute the Values Card Sort decks and Handout 1.1 to each seat.
3. Refill water, adjust lighting or temperature if needed.

### Activity 3: Values Card Sort -- "What Matters Most"

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Individual (8 min), then pairs (7 min), then small group share (5 min)
**Materials:** Values Card Sort deck (one per participant), Participant Handout 1.1, pens or pencils

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min) Introduce the activity:** "We just talked about how we want to treat each other. Now let's go a level deeper and explore what drives us -- our personal values. Values are the things that matter most to you. They shape your decisions, your relationships, and your goals."
2. **(8 min) Individual sort:** "Spread out your values cards. Read through them all first. Then sort them into three piles: Very Important to Me, Somewhat Important, and Not a Priority Right Now. There are blank cards if a value that matters to you isn't in the deck -- write it in. Once you've sorted, choose your top five. Then narrow it down to your top three. Record your top three on the handout."
3. **(7 min) Pair share:** "Turn to the person next to you. Share your top three values. For each one, briefly share why it made your list. Your partner's job is to listen and ask one curious question."
4. **(3 min) Small-group connection:** In groups of 4, have each person share just their number-one value and one sentence about why. On a shared sticky note, each group writes down any values they have in common.
5. Collect the "values in common" sticky notes for use in Activity 4.

**Facilitator tip:** Walk the room during the individual sort. Some participants may struggle to narrow down. Coaching language: "There are no wrong choices. This isn't permanent -- your values can shift over time. For today, go with your gut." If a participant finishes quickly, prompt them: "Look at your top three. Can you rank them? Which one would be hardest to give up?"

**Participant Handout Content (Handout 1.1: "My Values, My Voice"):**

> **My Values, My Voice**
>
> Values are the principles and beliefs that matter most to you. They guide how you make decisions, how you treat people, and what you stand for.
>
> **Part 1: Sort**
> Spread out your values cards. Sort them into three groups:
> - **Very Important to Me**
> - **Somewhat Important**
> - **Not a Priority Right Now**
>
> Don't overthink it. Go with your gut. If a value that matters to you isn't in the deck, write it on a blank card.
>
> **Part 2: Narrow Down**
> From your "Very Important" pile, choose your **top 5**. Then narrow to your **top 3**.
>
> | Rank | My Value | Why This Matters to Me |
> |------|----------|----------------------|
> | 1 | | |
> | 2 | | |
> | 3 | | |
>
> **Part 3: Share**
> With a partner, share your top three values and what they mean to you. Listen to your partner's values with curiosity. Ask at least one question.
>
> **My partner's top value:** ____________________
>
> **One thing I learned about my partner:** ____________________

---

### Activity 4: Values Gallery + Share-Out

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Whole group
**Materials:** "Values We Share" chart paper, sticky notes from Activity 3, markers, tape

**Instructions:**

1. **(3 min) Build the gallery:** Invite each small group to post their "values in common" sticky note on the "Values We Share" chart paper. As groups post, read a few aloud.
2. **(5 min) Facilitated share-out:** Stand near the chart and lead a brief discussion:
   - "What do you notice about the values on this chart?"
   - "Are there any surprises?"
   - "What does it tell us about who we are as a group?"
   - "How might these shared values help us as we work together in the sessions ahead?"
3. **(2 min) Bridge to the future:** "These values aren't just words on a wall. They're a compass. As we move through this program together, we'll keep coming back to what matters to us -- individually and as a group. Your values will help guide the work we do, the conversations we have, and the goals we set."

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use the following prompts to guide the closing reflection. Facilitator reads each prompt aloud and gives participants 1-2 minutes to respond (verbally in pairs, or by writing on a notecard). Select 2 of the 3 prompts based on time and group energy.

### Prompt 1: Group Agreements Reflection

- **Situation:** Today, you worked with a group of people you may not have known before to create shared agreements for how this group will operate.
- **Task:** Your group needed to come to consensus on norms that everyone could commit to.
- **Action:** What did you or your group do to make sure everyone's voice was heard during the agreements process?
- **Result:** How do you feel about the agreements we created? What is one agreement you are most committed to honoring, and why?

### Prompt 2: Values Discovery Reflection

- **Situation:** You were given a set of values cards and asked to identify what matters most to you.
- **Task:** You needed to narrow a large set of values down to your personal top three.
- **Action:** What strategy did you use to make your choices? Was it easy or difficult to narrow them down?
- **Result:** What did you learn about yourself through this activity? Did anything surprise you about your own values or your partner's values?

### Prompt 3: Showing Up Reflection

- **Situation:** This was the first session of a new program, and you walked into a space with people you may not know well.
- **Task:** You were asked to introduce yourself, participate in group activities, and share something personal about your values.
- **Action:** How did you decide to show up today? What helped you feel comfortable (or what was challenging)?
- **Result:** What is one thing you want to carry into the next session -- about yourself, this group, or what we're building together?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

Facilitators should use the following methods to gauge participant learning and engagement. These are observational and formative -- no grades or scores are assigned.

**During the session, look for:**

- [ ] Participants can name at least three personal values and articulate a brief reason for their importance (observed during pair share and handout completion).
- [ ] All participants contributed at least one idea to the group agreements process (observed during sticky note brainstorm and whole-group negotiation).
- [ ] Participants demonstrate active listening during peer introductions and values sharing (eye contact, nodding, asking follow-up questions, not interrupting).
- [ ] The group's posted agreements reflect authentic negotiation rather than surface-level compliance (evidence: discussion during the finalization step, range of ideas represented).

**Exit Ticket (optional):**

On a notecard or sticky note, have each participant write:

1. One value that is important to me: __________
2. One group agreement I'm committed to: __________
3. One word that describes how I feel about being part of this group: __________

Collect the notecards. Review them after the session to identify participants who may need additional support, connection, or encouragement.

## Community Partner Integration

This module includes the following touchpoints for community partner involvement. Coordinate with partners in advance of the session.

### Before the Session
- **Partner introduction:** If a community partner (e.g., site host, mentoring organization representative, local business sponsor) is present, invite them to briefly introduce themselves during the opening (1-2 minutes maximum). Frame their role as part of the community supporting participants.
- **Space and logistics:** Community partners hosting the session site can support with room setup, signage, snacks, and welcoming participants at the door.

### During the Session
- **Activity 2 (Group Agreements):** Invite a community partner to participate in the small-group brainstorm as an equal member, not an authority figure. Their presence models intergenerational or cross-sector collaboration.
- **Activity 3 (Values Card Sort):** Community partners can share their own top value and a brief story about how that value shapes their work in the community. This builds relational bridges between participants and local institutions.

### After the Session
- **Debrief with partner:** Share general themes (not individual participant data) from the session with community partners. Discuss how shared values can inform future community-based projects or service-learning opportunities.
- **Ongoing integration:** Community partners can reference the group's posted agreements and shared values chart in future interactions, reinforcing continuity.
