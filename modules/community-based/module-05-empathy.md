# Module 5: Empathy

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Distinguish between empathy and sympathy and explain why the difference matters in relationships and community settings.
2. Demonstrate perspective-taking by articulating another person's feelings and viewpoint without judgment.
3. Practice empathic responding using specific verbal and nonverbal techniques.
4. Apply empathic listening and responding skills to realistic community-based scenarios.
5. Connect empathy skills to the active listening foundations developed in Module 4.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL USE ONLY -- This section is for facilitator reference and should not be distributed to participants.**

### Conceptual Foundation

This module draws on Harrington's model of empathic development, which positions empathy as a learnable skill set rather than a fixed trait. Harrington identifies three dimensions of empathy relevant to youth and community development work:

1. **Cognitive empathy** -- the ability to understand another person's perspective and mental state.
2. **Affective empathy** -- the capacity to share or resonate with another person's emotional experience.
3. **Behavioral empathy** -- the outward expression of understanding through responsive action and communication.

This session emphasizes cognitive and behavioral empathy, building directly on the active listening skills from Module 4. Participants who struggled with listening fundamentals may need additional scaffolding during the scenario practice activities.

### SCBF 2.0 Alignment

This module addresses the SCBF 2.0 Social-Emotional Competency strand, specifically the interpersonal skill cluster. Empathy is positioned as a bridge skill between listening (Module 4) and conflict resolution (Module 6). The session arc follows the SCBF 2.0 experiential learning cycle: Experience, Reflect, Conceptualize, Apply.

### NAPS Alignment

- **Standard 3:** Positive Interactions and Relationships
- **Standard 4:** Youth Voice, Engagement, and Leadership

### Key Facilitation Considerations

- **Emotional safety is paramount.** Empathy exercises can surface personal experiences of being misunderstood or dismissed. Establish and reinforce group agreements before beginning.
- **Avoid the "empathy performance" trap.** Participants may try to say what sounds right rather than genuinely engage. Normalize imperfection and emphasize practice over polish.
- **Cultural responsiveness.** Expressions of empathy vary across cultures. Avoid prescribing a single "correct" way to show empathy. Invite participants to share how empathy looks and sounds in their families and communities.
- **Connection to Module 4.** Explicitly reference the listening skills participants practiced previously. Empathic responding is an extension of active listening, not a replacement for it. If your group did not complete Module 4, budget an additional 5-10 minutes for a brief listening review at the start of this session.

### Video Selection Guidance

Select a short video (3-5 minutes) that clearly illustrates the difference between empathy and sympathy. Recommended options:

- Brene Brown's animated short on empathy (RSA, approx. 3 minutes)
- Any age-appropriate clip that demonstrates someone attempting to comfort another person using sympathy ("at least...," "it could be worse...") versus empathy ("that sounds really hard," "I'm here with you")

Preview the video before the session to confirm it is appropriate for your participant group.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Empathy vs. Sympathy -- Video and Discussion

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Whole group
**Materials:** Video clip, projector/screen, chart paper, markers

#### Setup

Write two column headers on chart paper before the session begins: **"Empathy Sounds Like..."** and **"Sympathy Sounds Like..."**

#### Instructions

1. **Frame the activity** (2 minutes):
   - "In our last session, we practiced active listening -- really hearing what someone is saying. Today we're going to build on that. We're going to explore what it means to truly understand what someone is feeling and to respond in a way that lets them know they've been heard and understood."
   - "We're going to start with a short video that shows the difference between two things that might seem similar but are actually very different: empathy and sympathy."

2. **Play the video** (3-5 minutes):
   - Ask participants to watch for specific moments where they can tell the difference between the two approaches.

3. **Facilitate discussion** (8-10 minutes):
   - "What stood out to you?"
   - "When someone responds with sympathy, how might the other person feel?"
   - "When someone responds with empathy, how might the other person feel?"
   - "Can you think of a time when someone responded to you with sympathy when you really needed empathy? What was that like?" (Invite but do not pressure sharing.)

4. **Chart responses:**
   - Record participant observations on the two-column chart.
   - Aim for at least 4-5 entries per column.

#### Key Distinctions to Surface

| Empathy | Sympathy |
|---------|----------|
| "I'm with you in this." | "I feel sorry for you." |
| Connects | Creates distance |
| Acknowledges feelings | Tries to fix or minimize |
| Requires vulnerability | Stays on the surface |
| "That sounds really hard." | "At least it's not worse." |
| Listens to understand | Listens to respond |

### Mini-Lesson: What Empathic Responding Looks Like

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Whole group with brief partner check-ins
**Materials:** Empathic Response Stems handout

#### Facilitator Talking Points

1. **Empathy is a skill, not a personality trait.**
   - "Some people think you're either an empathetic person or you're not. That's not true. Empathy is something we can all practice and get better at. That's exactly what we're going to do today."

2. **Three parts of an empathic response:**
   - **Attend:** Use your body to show you are present. Face the person. Make appropriate eye contact. Put away distractions.
   - **Acknowledge:** Name or reflect the emotion you are hearing. You don't have to be exactly right -- the other person will correct you, and that's okay.
   - **Ask:** Invite the person to share more, on their terms. Don't interrogate. Don't rush to advice.

3. **Common pitfalls to avoid:**
   - Jumping to advice ("You should...")
   - Making it about yourself ("That happened to me too, let me tell you...")
   - Minimizing ("It's not that bad" or "Everything happens for a reason")
   - Interrogating ("Why did you do that?" or "What were you thinking?")

4. **Distribute the Empathic Response Stems handout** and walk through several examples.

---

#### Participant Handout: Empathic Response Stems

> Use these sentence starters to practice responding with empathy. You don't have to use them word-for-word -- let them guide you toward responding in a way that helps the other person feel heard and understood.

**Acknowledging Feelings:**
- "It sounds like you're feeling..."
- "That must be really..."
- "I can hear how [frustrated / hurt / overwhelmed / excited] you are."
- "It makes sense that you'd feel that way."

**Showing Presence:**
- "I'm here. I'm listening."
- "Thank you for trusting me with this."
- "You don't have to go through this alone."
- "I may not fully understand, but I want to."

**Inviting More:**
- "Do you want to tell me more about that?"
- "What was that like for you?"
- "What would be most helpful right now?"
- "Is there more you want to share, or do you just need someone to sit with you?"

**When You're Not Sure What to Say:**
- "I don't know exactly what to say, but I'm glad you told me."
- "That sounds really hard. I'm here."
- Sometimes silence and presence are the most empathic response of all.

### Activity 2: Empathic Response Practice (Pairs)

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Partners
**Materials:** Empathic Response Stems handout, timer

#### Instructions for Participants

1. **Find a partner.** Decide who will be Person A and who will be Person B.

2. **Round 1 (5 minutes):**
   - Person A reads one of the prompts below and responds as if it happened to them, sharing for about 1-2 minutes.
   - Person B practices empathic responding using the response stems handout. Focus on acknowledging, not fixing.
   - After 2-3 minutes of exchange, pause and debrief briefly: Person A, how did it feel to be responded to that way?

3. **Round 2 (5 minutes):**
   - Switch roles. Person B shares; Person A practices empathic responding.

4. **Debrief together (5 minutes):**
   - Return to the whole group. Facilitator asks:
     - "What felt natural? What felt awkward?"
     - "Did anyone notice a difference between how you usually respond and what you tried today?"
     - "What did it feel like to be on the receiving end of an empathic response?"

#### Practice Prompts

Choose one prompt per round:

- "You worked really hard on a project for school or work, and nobody acknowledged your effort."
- "A friend made plans with someone else after telling you they were busy."
- "You're dealing with a big change at home and you're not sure how to feel about it."
- "Someone you look up to said something that really hurt your feelings."
- "You tried something new and it didn't go the way you hoped."

### Activity 3: Scenario Cards -- Guided Group Practice

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Small groups (3-4 participants), rotating stations
**Materials:** Printed Scenario Cards (below), timer

#### Setup

- Arrange 4-5 stations around the room, each with one Scenario Card placed face-down.
- Divide participants into small groups of 3-4.
- Each group starts at one station.

#### Instructions for Participants

1. At your station, turn over the Scenario Card and read it aloud as a group.
2. As a group, discuss:
   - What might this person be feeling?
   - What do they need right now?
   - What would an empathic response sound like?
   - What would a sympathetic (but unhelpful) response sound like?
3. Choose one person to practice delivering the empathic response out loud while others observe and offer kind feedback.
4. When the facilitator signals (after approximately 4-5 minutes), rotate to the next station.
5. Complete at least 3 stations.

#### Scenario Cards

**Scenario Card A:**
> Jaylen comes to your community group looking upset. When you ask what's going on, Jaylen says, "My mom and I got into a huge argument this morning. She said I never help out at home, but I've been trying really hard. She just doesn't see it."

*Consider: What emotions might Jaylen be experiencing? How can you respond without taking sides or offering solutions?*

---

**Scenario Card B:**
> Maria just found out she didn't get accepted into a summer program she applied to. She says, "I don't even know why I try. I'm never good enough for anything."

*Consider: Maria may be feeling more than just disappointment. How can you acknowledge her pain without minimizing it or jumping to reassurance?*

---

**Scenario Card C:**
> Devon is new to the neighborhood and has been coming to community sessions for three weeks. Today Devon is sitting alone and says, "Everyone here already knows each other. I feel like I don't belong."

*Consider: How can you respond in a way that validates Devon's experience without dismissing it ("Oh, everyone feels that way at first!")?*

---

**Scenario Card D:**
> Aisha tells you that her best friend just moved to another city. She says, "I know it's not a big deal, people move all the time. But I just feel really alone now."

*Consider: Aisha is already minimizing her own feelings. How can your response give her permission to feel what she's feeling?*

---

**Scenario Card E:**
> Marcus just got into a heated disagreement with another participant during an activity. Afterward, he comes to you and says, "Everyone thinks I was wrong, but nobody even asked my side. Nobody ever listens to me."

*Consider: Marcus is both frustrated and hurt. How can you respond to both emotions? How does this connect to the listening skills from our last session?*

---

#### Facilitator Debrief Questions (after rotations)

- "Which scenario was the hardest to respond to? Why?"
- "Did anyone notice themselves wanting to give advice instead of just being present?"
- "How did it feel to pause and focus on understanding before responding?"
- "Where in your real life -- at home, at school, in your community -- could you use these skills this week?"

## STAR Reflection Prompts

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Individual journaling followed by optional pair or whole-group sharing

> Write or think through the following using the STAR format. Choose one prompt that resonates with you.

### Prompt Option 1: A Time You Received Empathy

- **Situation:** Describe a time when you were going through something difficult and someone responded to you in a way that truly helped.
- **Task:** What did you need from that person in that moment?
- **Action:** What specifically did they say or do that made you feel heard and understood?
- **Result:** How did their response affect you? How did it change how you felt about the situation or about yourself?

### Prompt Option 2: A Time You Could Have Responded Differently

- **Situation:** Think of a time when someone came to you with a problem or difficult emotion, and you responded with advice, reassurance, or minimizing rather than empathy.
- **Task:** What did that person likely need from you?
- **Action:** Knowing what you practiced today, what could you have said or done differently?
- **Result:** How might a more empathic response have changed the outcome for that person? For your relationship?

### Prompt Option 3: Applying Empathy This Week

- **Situation:** Think of a relationship or setting in your life where empathy could make a difference right now.
- **Task:** What is one specific opportunity you have this week to practice empathic responding?
- **Action:** What will you specifically do or say? Which response stems or techniques from today will you try?
- **Result:** What outcome are you hoping for? How will you know if your empathic response made a difference?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Informal Assessment (During Session)

Facilitators should observe and note the following throughout the session:

- [ ] Can participants articulate at least two differences between empathy and sympathy?
- [ ] During partner practice, are participants using acknowledging language rather than jumping to advice?
- [ ] During scenario rotations, are groups identifying underlying emotions (not just surface-level reactions)?
- [ ] Are participants connecting today's empathy skills to the listening skills from Module 4?

### Exit Ticket (Closing Circle)

Ask each participant to respond to one of the following before leaving. This can be done verbally in the circle or on a sticky note turned in to the facilitator:

1. **Name one thing** you will do differently this week because of what you practiced today.
2. **Complete this sentence:** "Empathy is different from sympathy because..."
3. **On a scale of 1-5** (1 = not at all comfortable, 5 = very comfortable), how comfortable do you feel responding to someone with empathy right now? What would help you move one number higher?

### Facilitator Self-Assessment

After the session, reflect on:

- Did participants move beyond surface-level understanding of empathy to actual behavioral practice?
- Were there moments where participants slipped into sympathy, advice-giving, or minimizing? How were those addressed?
- Which scenarios generated the most engagement? Which need revision?
- Are there participants who may need additional support or follow-up before Module 6 (Conflict Resolution)?

## Community Partner Integration

### Pre-Session Opportunities

- **Guest speaker:** Invite a community partner (counselor, social worker, peer mentor, faith leader, or community health worker) to share a brief story about how empathy shows up in their professional work. Keep the presentation to 5-7 minutes and integrate it into the Opening Circle or as a bridge between Activity 1 and the Mini-Lesson.
- **Co-facilitation:** A community partner who works in mediation, counseling, or youth mentoring can co-facilitate the scenario card rotations, offering real-world perspective during debriefs.

### During-Session Integration

- **Scenario card contributions:** Before the session, ask community partners to contribute one scenario card based on real situations they encounter in their work (with all identifying details removed). Locally relevant scenarios increase participant engagement.
- **Modeling:** If a community partner is present, ask them to model an empathic response live during the Mini-Lesson. Seeing a respected adult practice the skill normalizes the learning process.

### Post-Session Opportunities

- **Empathy in action project:** Partner with a local organization to create a follow-up service opportunity where participants can practice empathy skills in a real community context (e.g., visiting a senior center, volunteering at a food pantry, participating in a peer mentoring program).
- **Family and community extension:** Provide a take-home conversation guide that families or caregivers can use to practice empathic responding together. Frame it as a shared family activity, not homework.
- **Community partner feedback loop:** Share anonymized, aggregated participant reflections with community partners to help them understand the skills participants are developing. This strengthens the partnership and informs future collaboration.
