# Module 4: Active Listening
**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Define active listening and distinguish it from passive hearing.
2. Identify at least three verbal and three nonverbal cues that signal active listening.
3. Practice reflective listening techniques, including paraphrasing and clarifying questions.
4. Recognize common barriers to effective listening and generate strategies to overcome them.
5. Demonstrate active listening in a paired practice setting with peer feedback.

---

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL USE ONLY -- Not for participant distribution.**

### Conceptual Foundation

This module draws on Harrington's communication competency framework, which positions active listening as the foundational skill upon which all other interpersonal communication capacities are built. Harrington's research emphasizes that listening is a teachable, practicable skill rather than a fixed trait, and that structured repetition with immediate feedback accelerates acquisition. The NAPS standards for youth development programming further support prioritizing listening skills as a core social-emotional competency.

### Key Facilitation Considerations

- **High-practice design.** The original source content runs approximately 60 minutes. The additional 30 minutes in this community-based edition are allocated to extra paired practice rotations and an extended barrier-identification brainstorm. Do not shorten practice time to accommodate lecture; the research base is clear that skill acquisition requires repetition.
- **Pair rotations.** Plan for a minimum of three practice rounds with different partners. Rotating partners exposes participants to varied communication styles and prevents comfort-zone stagnation. If your group is smaller than eight, allow repeat pairings but change roles (speaker/listener) each round.
- **Modeling matters.** Before the first paired exercise, conduct a live demonstration with a co-facilitator or a prepared volunteer. Show both a "poor listening" example and an "active listening" example side by side. Exaggerate contrasts so participants can clearly identify the differences.
- **Emotional safety.** Listening exercises can surface personal stories. Establish ground rules before practice begins: participants choose what to share, no one is required to disclose anything sensitive, and what is shared in practice stays in the room.
- **Common pitfalls:**
  - Facilitators sometimes rush the debrief after paired exercises. Protect at least five minutes for whole-group reflection after each rotation block.
  - Participants may confuse "giving advice" with "reflective listening." Be prepared to redirect with the phrase: "Right now we are practicing hearing, not helping."
  - Watch for participants who dominate the speaker role. Use the timer and enforce equal turns.

### Room Setup

- Arrange chairs in pairs facing each other, spread across the room so pairs have conversational privacy.
- Keep a clear central area for the opening demonstration and whole-group debrief.
- Post chart paper on walls for the barrier brainstorm activity.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Listening Skills Demonstration
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Facilitator-led whole group
**Materials:** None required (optional: two chairs at the front of the room)

**Instructions:**

1. Invite participants to observe carefully. Explain that they will see two short conversations and their job is to spot the differences.
2. **Round A -- Passive Hearing (2 minutes):** The facilitator (or co-facilitator pair) acts out a conversation where the "listener" checks their phone, looks around the room, gives one-word responses, interrupts, and changes the subject. The "speaker" tries to share a story about something meaningful to them.
3. **Round B -- Active Listening (2 minutes):** Replay a similar conversation, but now the listener maintains eye contact, nods, paraphrases what the speaker says, asks follow-up questions, and waits before responding.
4. After both rounds, facilitate a brief group discussion (4 minutes):
   - "What did you notice was different between the two conversations?"
   - "How do you think the speaker felt in each scenario?"
   - "Which specific behaviors made the biggest difference?"
5. Capture participant observations on the whiteboard in two columns: "What listeners do" and "What listeners avoid."

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Observation Guide -- Listening Demonstration**
>
> Watch both conversations carefully. After each one, jot down your answers:
>
> **Conversation A:**
> - What did the listener do with their body? ___________________________
> - How did the listener respond to what was being said? ___________________________
> - How do you think the speaker felt? ___________________________
>
> **Conversation B:**
> - What did the listener do differently? ___________________________
> - What words or phrases did the listener use? ___________________________
> - How do you think the speaker felt this time? ___________________________
>
> **Your takeaway:** One thing I want to practice from what I observed: ___________________________

### Activity 2: Verbal and Nonverbal Cues Mini-Lesson
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Interactive whole-group discussion with handout
**Materials:** "Active Listening Cue Cards" handout, whiteboard

**Instructions:**

1. Distribute the Active Listening Cue Cards.
2. Walk through each category, asking participants to contribute examples from their own experience before revealing the card content.
3. For nonverbal cues, invite volunteers to demonstrate each one (e.g., "Show me what attentive posture looks like").
4. For verbal cues, practice as a group: read a sample statement aloud and ask three participants to offer a paraphrasing response.
5. Emphasize that active listening is a combination of verbal and nonverbal behaviors working together.

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Active Listening Cue Cards**
>
> **Nonverbal Cues -- What Your Body Says**
>
> | Cue | What It Looks Like |
> |-----|-------------------|
> | Eye contact | Looking at the speaker naturally (not staring); glancing away briefly is normal |
> | Open posture | Facing the speaker, arms uncrossed, leaning slightly forward |
> | Nodding | Small, periodic nods to show you are following along |
> | Facial expression | Matching your expression to the tone of what is being shared (concern, enthusiasm, curiosity) |
> | Stillness | Putting away distractions; keeping hands calm; not fidgeting with objects |
>
> **Verbal Cues -- What Your Words Say**
>
> | Cue | Example Phrases |
> |-----|----------------|
> | Paraphrasing | "So what I hear you saying is..." / "It sounds like..." |
> | Clarifying questions | "Can you tell me more about...?" / "What did you mean when you said...?" |
> | Reflecting feelings | "That sounds like it was really frustrating." / "It seems like that made you proud." |
> | Summarizing | "Let me make sure I understand -- you're saying that..." |
> | Minimal encouragers | "Mm-hmm." / "Go on." / "I see." / "Tell me more." |
>
> **Remember:** Active listening is not about having the perfect response. It is about making the other person feel genuinely heard.

### Activity 3: Paired Practice Exercises
**Duration:** 25 minutes (three 7-minute rounds with 1-minute transitions)
**Format:** Paired practice with rotating partners
**Materials:** "Reflective Listening Practice Scenarios" handout (one per pair), timer

**Instructions:**

1. Organize participants into pairs. Designate one person as Partner A (speaker) and one as Partner B (listener).
2. Distribute the scenario sheet. Each round uses a different scenario prompt.
3. Explain the structure for each 7-minute round:
   - Speaker talks for 3 minutes using the scenario prompt as a starting point. They may share a real experience or create one.
   - Listener practices active listening skills for the full 3 minutes. No advice-giving, no problem-solving -- only listening, paraphrasing, and asking clarifying questions.
   - Both partners spend 1 minute giving each other feedback: "One thing you did that made me feel heard was..." and "One thing you could try next time is..."
4. After each round, call time and instruct participants to find a new partner. Roles switch (previous speakers become listeners).
5. Circulate during practice. Offer quiet, specific feedback to pairs. Watch for advice-giving disguised as listening and gently redirect.

**Round Transition Protocol:**
- Sound the chime or call "Rotate!"
- Participants stand, find a new partner, and sit down.
- Announce the new scenario number and which partner speaks first.
- Allow 30 seconds for settling, then start the timer.

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Reflective Listening Practice Scenarios**
>
> *Instructions: In each round, the speaker uses the prompt below as a starting point. You may share a real experience or make one up -- the goal is to give your partner something meaningful to listen to. The listener practices the cues from the Active Listening Cue Cards.*
>
> **Round 1 Prompt:**
> Tell your partner about a time you worked on something as part of a team. What was the experience like? What went well, and what was challenging?
>
> **Round 2 Prompt:**
> Describe a situation where you felt someone really listened to you -- or a time when you felt like no one was listening. What made the difference?
>
> **Round 3 Prompt:**
> Share something you are working toward right now -- a goal, a project, or a change you want to make in your life or community. What matters to you about it?
>
> ---
>
> **Feedback Sentence Starters (use after each round):**
> - "One thing you did that made me feel heard was..."
> - "I noticed you used [specific cue] when you..."
> - "One thing you could try next time is..."
> - "I felt most listened to when you..."

### Activity 4: Barrier Identification Brainstorm
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Gallery walk and small-group brainstorm
**Materials:** Three sheets of chart paper posted on walls (pre-labeled), markers, sticky notes (two colors)

**Instructions:**

1. Post three chart paper sheets around the room, each labeled with a barrier category:
   - **Station 1:** "Internal Barriers" (things happening inside us that block listening)
   - **Station 2:** "External Barriers" (things in our environment that block listening)
   - **Station 3:** "Relational Barriers" (things between people that block listening)
2. Divide participants into three groups. Each group starts at one station.
3. **Round 1 (3 minutes):** Groups brainstorm barriers on sticky notes (one barrier per note, using Color A) and post them on the chart paper.
4. **Rotate.** Groups move to the next station.
5. **Round 2 (3 minutes):** Groups read what the previous group wrote, then add new barriers they did not see listed. They also use Color B sticky notes to write one strategy for overcoming each barrier they find most relevant.
6. **Rotate** one final time. Groups review both barriers and strategies at the third station and star the top two strategies they think are most practical.
7. **Whole-group share (4 minutes):** Each group presents the top two strategies from the station they ended at. Facilitator synthesizes themes on the whiteboard.

**Participant Handout Content:**

>

>

>
> **External Barriers** (things in my environment):
> - _________________________________
> - _________________________________
> - _________________________________

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Informal Assessment (During Session)

- **Observation during paired practice:** Circulate and note whether participants are using at least two verbal and two nonverbal cues from the cue cards. Use a simple checklist if tracking across a cohort.
- **Feedback quality:** Listen to the peer feedback exchanges at the end of each round. Are participants identifying specific behaviors, or giving only vague praise? Coach toward specificity.
- **Barrier brainstorm participation:** Check that all participants are contributing sticky notes and that strategies are actionable rather than abstract.

### Formal Check for Understanding

At the end of the session, ask participants to complete the following (verbally in the closing circle or on an index card):

> **Three-Two-One Exit Ticket**
>
> - **3** verbal or nonverbal cues I will practice this week: __________, __________, __________
> - **2** barriers to listening I personally struggle with: __________, __________
> - **1** situation where I plan to use active listening before our next session: __________

### Facilitator Self-Assessment

After the session, reflect on:

- Did all participants complete at least three practice rounds with different partners?
- Were transitions between rounds smooth, or did they consume too much time?
- Did any participants default to advice-giving during listening practice? If so, was the redirection effective?
- Did the barrier brainstorm generate actionable strategies, or did it stay at the surface level?

## Community Partner Integration

### Before the Session

- **Guest listener invitation:** Invite a community partner (e.g., a mentor, counselor, social worker, community organizer, or peer leader) to participate in the paired practice exercises as a rotation partner. Brief them in advance on the active listening cues being taught so they can model the skills authentically.
- **Real-world scenario contribution:** Ask a community partner to provide a brief (2-minute) opening story about a time when active listening made a difference in their professional or community role. This grounds the session in local, real-world relevance.

### During the Session

- **Practice partner rotation:** If a community partner is present, include them in the paired practice rotations. Participants benefit from practicing with someone outside their peer group who brings a different communication style and life experience.
- **Barrier brainstorm input:** Invite the community partner to add barriers and strategies from their professional experience during the gallery walk. Their perspective enriches the brainstorm with examples participants may not have considered.

### After the Session

- **Listening challenge connection:** Encourage participants to practice their active listening skills in a real community setting before the next session. Examples:
  - Attend a community meeting and practice listening without interrupting.
  - Have a conversation with a family member or neighbor using at least three cues from the cue card.
  - Volunteer with a community partner organization in a role that requires listening (e.g., tutoring, peer mentoring, event setup with a team).
- **Partner follow-up:** Share a brief session summary with the community partner, including the top strategies generated during the barrier brainstorm. This keeps partners informed and invested in the program arc.

### Suggested Community Partners for This Module

| Partner Type | Integration Opportunity |
|-------------|------------------------|

| Local mentor program coordinator | Participate in paired practice; share how mentors use listening skills |
| Community mediation center staff | Contribute to the barrier brainstorm with real-world mediation examples |
| Youth peer leaders (alumni) | Serve as practice partners; model near-peer active listening |
