# Module 12: Building Community

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify assets, resources, and strengths already present in their own communities.
2. Create a community resource map that connects peer support needs to local supports.
3. Develop a realistic action plan for sustaining peer support practices beyond this training.
4. Articulate how peer support can be woven into everyday settings (schools, neighborhoods, faith communities, workplaces, and social groups).
5. Collaborate with community partners to identify shared goals and mutual support opportunities.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- FACILITATOR USE ONLY. Do not distribute to participants.**

### Conceptual Grounding

This module draws on the National AfterSchool Partnership Standards (NAPS) framework, particularly:

- **NAPS Standard 4: Community and Family Engagement** -- Programs actively connect youth and families to community resources and build reciprocal relationships with local organizations.
- **NAPS Standard 6: Program Sustainability and Continuous Improvement** -- Programs establish structures and partnerships that support long-term viability and impact.

These standards inform the session's emphasis on asset-based thinking, reciprocal community relationships, and sustainability planning. Do not reference NAPS or any framework names during delivery. Instead, let the activities themselves embody these principles.

### Session Design Notes

- The source content for this module was designed for approximately 60 minutes. This Community-Based edition expands to 90 minutes by deepening the community asset mapping exercise, adding a structured community partner integration segment, and extending the group action planning activity.
- This module marks the transition from skills acquisition (Modules 1--11) to real-world application. The tone should shift from "learning how" to "planning to do." Reinforce that participants already have what they need; this session is about putting it into practice.
- If community partners are present in the session (strongly recommended for this module), integrate them into Activities 2 and 3 as resource people and collaborative planners.
- Be prepared for participants to express anxiety about "doing this on their own." Normalize this. Emphasize that peer support is never done alone -- that is the entire point of building community.

### Preparation Checklist

1. **Pre-session:** Contact 2--3 local community partners (if not attending in person) and request a brief written or video introduction to their services. Have these ready to share during Activity 2.
2. **Pre-session:** Prepare a sample community asset map on flip chart paper to use as a model during Activity 1.
3. **Pre-session:** Print and review all handouts. Confirm that the Community Resource Directory Template includes space for at least 10 entries.
4. **Room setup:** Arrange tables in small groups of 4--6. Post the sample asset map where all groups can see it. Set up a "Resource Wall" area where groups will post their completed maps.

## Activities

### Opening and Warm-Up (10 minutes)

**"Community Connections" Activity**

1. Ask participants to stand and form a circle (or remain seated in a circle arrangement).
2. Provide the prompt: *"Name one person, place, or group in your community that has supported you -- even in a small way -- at some point in your life."*
3. Each participant shares briefly (15--20 seconds). As each person shares, they hold one end of a ball of yarn and toss it to the next speaker, creating a visual web.
4. After everyone has shared, ask the group to look at the web they have created. Facilitate a brief observation:
   - *"What do you notice about this web?"*
   - *"What happens if one person lets go?"*
   - *"What does this tell us about community?"*
5. Gather the yarn and transition: *"Today, we are going to map these kinds of connections intentionally -- and plan how to strengthen and use them as peer supporters."*

> **Facilitator Tip:** If the group is larger than 15, split into two circles. If space does not allow standing, use a seated version where participants point or gesture to the next speaker instead of tossing yarn.

---

### Activity 1: Community Asset Mapping (25 minutes)

**Purpose:** Participants identify the strengths, resources, and supports that already exist in their communities, shifting from a deficit-based view ("what's missing") to an asset-based view ("what's already here").

#### Setup (2 minutes)
- Divide participants into small groups of 4--6 people, ideally grouped by geographic proximity or shared community context.
- Distribute flip chart paper and markers to each group.
- Display or point to the sample asset map you prepared.

#### Instructions to Participants (3 minutes)
Read aloud or paraphrase:

*"In your groups, you are going to create a map of the assets -- the strengths and resources -- in your community. This is not about what is missing. This is about what is already there. Think broadly. We are mapping four categories:"*

Write on the board or flip chart:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **People** | Mentors, coaches, elders, teachers, neighbors, friends, family members, faith leaders |
| **Places** | Community centers, libraries, parks, places of worship, schools, barbershops/salons, recreation centers |
| **Organizations** | Nonprofits, clubs, sports leagues, support groups, civic organizations, cultural associations |
| **Informal Supports** | Group chats, neighborhood traditions, mutual aid networks, pickup games, shared meals, carpools |

*"Start by drawing your community in the center of the page -- this could be your neighborhood, your school zone, your town, whatever feels like 'your community.' Then fill in assets in each of the four categories around it. Use different-colored markers for each category. Be as specific as you can -- names, locations, details."*

#### Small Group Work (18 minutes)
- Circulate among groups. Encourage specificity (*"Can you name that person or place?"*).
- Prompt groups that get stuck:
  - *"Where do people in your community go when they need to talk?"*
  - *"Who are the unofficial leaders -- the people everyone trusts?"*
  - *"What happens in your community that brings people together, even if it is not a formal program?"*
  - *"Think about online spaces too. Are there group chats, social media pages, or online groups that support people?"*
- Note: Some participants may realize their community has more assets than they initially thought. Affirm this. Others may identify gaps. Acknowledge these without dwelling on deficits -- they will be addressed in the action planning activity.

#### Wrap-Up (2 minutes)
- Give a two-minute warning. Ask groups to circle their top three assets -- the ones they think are most valuable or most underutilized.

---

### Activity 1 Debrief: Gallery Walk and Discussion (15 minutes)

#### Gallery Walk (7 minutes)
1. Have each group post their community asset map on the designated wall or table area.
2. Distribute 3 sticky notes to each participant (one color).
3. Instruct participants: *"Walk around and look at every group's map. Place a sticky note on any asset that also exists in your community or that you had not thought of but want to learn more about."*
4. Allow 5--7 minutes for the walk. Encourage participants to read carefully and talk to members of other groups if they have questions.

#### Full-Group Discussion (8 minutes)
Facilitate a brief discussion using the following prompts:

- *"What patterns do you notice? Which types of assets showed up on many maps?"*
- *"Were there any surprises -- assets you hadn't considered before?"*
- *"Which assets received the most sticky notes? What does that tell us?"*
- *"Did anyone notice gaps -- categories where most groups had fewer entries?"*
- *"How might you, as a peer supporter, connect someone to one of these assets?"*

> **Facilitator Transition:** *"Now that we can see what already exists, let's add another layer -- community organizations and partners who can support your work as peer supporters."*

---

### Activity 2: Community Resource Sharing (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Participants learn about specific community resources and begin building a personal resource directory they can use when supporting peers.

#### Community Partner Segment (8 minutes)

**If community partners are present in the room:**
- Invite each partner (limit to 2--3) to provide a 2-minute introduction:
  - Who they are and what their organization does
  - Who they serve and how to access their services
  - One way they could collaborate with peer supporters
- After introductions, allow 2 minutes for participant questions.

**If community partners are not physically present:**
- Share pre-recorded video introductions or read brief written statements from 2--3 local partners.
- Display contact information and access details on the screen or flip chart.

> **Facilitator Tip:** Prioritize partners that offer services relevant to the peer support context -- mental health resources, youth programs, crisis lines, housing support, food assistance, mentorship programs.

#### Resource Directory Building (7 minutes)

Distribute the **Community Resource Directory Template** handout.

Instructions to participants:

*"Using what you have learned from our community partners and from the asset mapping exercise, begin filling in your personal resource directory. This is a tool you will take with you. When you are supporting a peer and they need more help than you can provide, you will want to know exactly where to point them."*

- Ask participants to pair up with someone (preferably not from their earlier small group).

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use these prompts during the closing reflection segment. Participants can respond verbally in pairs, in a full-group share, or in writing (journal or sticky note).

### Prompt 1: Discovering Community Assets
- **Situation:** *Think about a time before today when you needed support or resources in your community.*
- **Task:** *What did you need, and what were you trying to find or accomplish?*
- **Action:** *What did you actually do to find support? Where did you look? Who did you ask?*
- **Result:** *What happened? Looking at your community asset map now, what additional resources could you have connected with? How would that have changed the outcome?*

### Prompt 2: Becoming a Resource Connector
- **Situation:** *Imagine a peer comes to you and shares that they are struggling with something -- loneliness, stress, a family problem, or a practical need like food or transportation.*
- **Task:** *Your role as a peer supporter is not to fix the problem but to listen, support, and connect them with resources.*
- **Action:** *Using your resource directory and asset map, what steps would you take? What would you say? Where would you point them?*
- **Result:** *What would a good outcome look like -- for your peer and for you? How would you follow up?*

### Prompt 3: Sustaining Your Practice
- **Situation:** *It is 30 days from now. You are back in your everyday routine -- school, work, home, social life.*
- **Task:** *You committed to practicing peer support skills and following your action plan.*
- **Action:** *Describe what you imagine yourself doing. Where are you? Who are you supporting? Which skills are you using?*
- **Result:** *What impact do you hope to see -- in yourself, in your relationships, and in your community?*

> **Facilitator Note:** For this module, Prompt 3 is especially important. It bridges this session to Module 13 (Closing and Reflection) and helps participants visualize themselves as peer supporters beyond the training context.

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Formative Assessment (During Session)

| Assessment Point | What to Observe | Action if Gaps Are Identified |
|------------------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| Community Asset Map | Are groups identifying assets across all four categories (People, Places, Organizations, Informal Supports)? Are entries specific rather than vague? | Prompt with targeted questions for underrepresented categories. Offer examples without supplying answers. |
| Gallery Walk | Are participants engaging with other groups' maps? Are sticky notes being placed thoughtfully? | Redirect participants who are skimming. Ask them to find at least one asset they want to learn more about. |
| Resource Directory | Are participants recording actionable contact information, not just organization names? | Model a complete entry on the board. Emphasize that "knowing about" a resource is not the same as "knowing how to connect someone." |
| Action Plan | Are plans specific and realistic? Do participants identify support systems and potential challenges? | During pair share, encourage partners to ask "How will you actually do that?" and "What might stop you?" |

### Summative Check (End of Session)

**Quick-Write Exit Ticket** (can be completed on a sticky note or index card):

*"Name one community resource you learned about today that you did not know about before, and describe one specific situation where you might connect a peer to that resource."*

Collect exit tickets. Review after the session to identify participants who may need additional resource orientation or follow-up.

## Community Partner Integration

> **This module is the primary integration point for community partners in the training sequence.** Maximize partner involvement here.

### Before the Session

| Task | Timeline | Details |
|------|----------|--------|
| Identify 2--3 community partners relevant to participants' communities | 2--3 weeks before session | Prioritize mental health services, youth-serving organizations, crisis resources, and basic needs organizations (food, housing, transportation). |
| Invite partners to attend the session in person | 2 weeks before session | Provide a brief overview of the training, the module objectives, and what you are asking them to contribute (2-minute introduction, availability for Q&A). |
| Prepare backup materials | 1 week before session | If partners cannot attend, request a short video (under 2 minutes), a written introduction, or a flyer with service details and contact info. |
| Collect resource materials | 1 week before session | Ask partners to bring brochures, business cards, or referral forms that participants can take with them. |

### During the Session

- **Activity 1 (Asset Mapping):** If partners are present, assign one partner to each small group as a "resource consultant." Partners can answer questions about services in the community and help groups identify assets they may not be aware of.
- **Activity 2 (Resource Sharing):** Partners deliver their 2-minute introductions. This is their primary speaking role. Keep it brief, practical, and participant-focused. Partners should address: What do you do? Who do you serve? How does someone access your services?
- **Activity 3 (Action Planning):** Partners circulate during individual planning time. They can help participants fill in the "professional resource" section of their support system and offer concrete next steps for connecting.

### After the Session

| Task | Timeline | Details |
|------|----------|--------|
| Send thank-you communication to partners | Within 48 hours | Include a summary of the session, number of participants, and any follow-up actions you committed to. |
| Share aggregated themes (no PII) | Within 1 week | Let partners know what resource gaps participants identified and what types of support were most requested. This helps partners plan outreach. |
| Establish follow-up connection points | Within 2 weeks | Work with partners to create a simple referral pathway that participants (as peer supporters) can use when connecting peers to services. |
| Plan ongoing engagement | Within 1 month | Explore whether partners can participate in follow-up check-ins, booster sessions, or ongoing peer supporter meetings. |

### Sustainability Planning Framework

Use this structure to guide the community partner conversation toward long-term sustainability:

1. **Reciprocity:** What do peer supporters offer community partners? (Outreach, trusted messengers, cultural knowledge, peer engagement.) What do partners offer peer supporters? (Resources, training, supervision, referral pathways.)
2. **Access Points:** Where and how will peer supporters connect peers to community resources? (Warm handoffs, printed directories, shared contact lists, texting resource info.)
3. **Feedback Loops:** How will peer supporters and community partners communicate about what is working and what is not? (Monthly check-ins, shared group chats, quarterly meetings.)
4. **Growth:** How might this network of peer supporters and community partners grow over time? (Training additional peer supporters, adding new partners, expanding to new settings.)
