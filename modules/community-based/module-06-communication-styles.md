# Module 6: Communication Styles

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. **Identify** four communication styles -- assertive, passive, aggressive, and passive-aggressive -- and describe the core characteristics of each.
2. **Recognize** their own default communication style and understand how it affects interactions with peers.
3. **Construct** effective "I" statements that express feelings and needs without blame or judgment.
4. **Demonstrate** assertive communication through guided role-play scenarios.
5. **Explain** why assertive communication strengthens peer relationships and mutual support.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- FOR FACILITATOR USE ONLY. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE TO PARTICIPANTS.**

### Framework Alignment

This module aligns with the following standards and frameworks:

- **NAPS (National AfterSchool Partnership Standards):** This module addresses NAPS Standard Area 2 -- *Relationships* (building positive communication among youth and between youth and adults) and Standard Area 4 -- *Youth Development* (fostering social-emotional competencies including self-awareness and interpersonal skills). Assertive communication is a foundational interpersonal skill identified across NAPS quality benchmarks.
- **SCBF 2.0 Framework:** Module 6 sits within the Skills Development strand (Day 2), building on the relational foundation established in Modules 1-5 (values, identity, peer support, active listening, empathy). Communication Styles bridges receptive skills (listening, empathy) to expressive skills (assertive self-expression, "I" statements).

### Facilitator Readiness Checklist

1. **Review all four communication styles** so you can model each convincingly during the overview segment. Practice brief demonstrations of each style responding to the same scenario.
2. **Complete the self-assessment yourself** before the session. Reflect on your own default style -- self-awareness makes facilitation more authentic.
3. **Pre-read all role-play scenario cards** and anticipate participant reactions. Several scenarios touch on peer conflict and authority dynamics that may surface strong feelings.
4. **Prepare your "I" statement examples** in advance. Have at least three ready-made examples that are relevant to the age group and community context.
5. **Room setup:** Arrange seating in a circle or U-shape for the opening and closing. Ensure space for small groups of 3-4 to work together during role-play activities.
6. **Sensitivity awareness:** Some participants may recognize aggressive or passive-aggressive communication patterns from their home environments. Be prepared to normalize this without pathologizing families. Emphasize that we all use different styles in different contexts and the goal is expanding our range, not judging anyone.
7. **Time management:** The source content runs approximately 60 minutes. The additional 30 minutes in this Community-Based edition are allocated to deeper role-play practice (Activity 3) and the extended STAR reflection. Watch pacing during Activity 2 ("I" Statement Practice) -- it can run long if groups are engaged.

### Key Facilitation Moves

- **Default style identification:** When participants identify their default style, affirm that no style is "who they are" -- styles are habits that can shift. Avoid letting participants label themselves or each other negatively.
- **Assertive as a skill, not a personality trait:** Reinforce throughout that assertive communication is a learnable skill, not something people are born with.
- **Redirect competitive dynamics:** In role-play, some participants may turn aggressive communication into humor or performance. Gently redirect to the skill-building purpose.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Communication Styles Overview + Self-Assessment

**Duration:** 15 minutes (10 min overview + 5 min self-assessment)
**Format:** Whole group instruction followed by individual work
**Materials:** Communication Styles Self-Assessment handout, flip chart or projected visual

#### Facilitator Instructions

1. **Set the frame (2 minutes):**
   - "Today we're going to explore how we communicate, especially when things get tense or emotional. There isn't one 'right' way to communicate all the time, but understanding our patterns helps us make better choices."

2. **Introduce the four styles (8 minutes):**
   - Present each style using the descriptions below. For each one, give the verbal example AND briefly model the body language and tone.
   - Use the same base scenario for all four so participants can see the contrast clearly.

   **Base scenario for demonstration:** *"Your friend borrowed something important to you and returned it damaged."*

   Walk through how each style would respond to this scenario (use the examples from the participant handout section below).

3. **Self-Assessment (5 minutes):**
   - Distribute the Communication Styles Self-Assessment handout.
   - "Take about five minutes to work through this on your own. There are no right or wrong answers -- this is about noticing your patterns. Be honest with yourself."
   - Circulate and be available for questions but do not look at individual responses.

#### Participant Handout: Communication Styles Overview

**How Do You Say What You Mean?**

We all have ways of expressing ourselves, especially when we feel strongly about something. Understanding these patterns is the first step toward communicating in ways that build stronger relationships.

**Four Common Communication Styles:**

| Style | What It Sounds Like | What It Looks Like | How Others May Feel |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| **Passive** | "It's fine, don't worry about it..." (even when it's not fine) | Avoids eye contact; quiet voice; closed body language | Confused, unsure what the person actually needs |
| **Aggressive** | "You always do this! You don't even care about my stuff!" | Loud voice; pointed finger; tense posture; may invade space | Defensive, scared, angry, shut down |
| **Passive-Aggressive** | "No, it's fine..." (slams door, gives silent treatment, makes sarcastic comments later) | Appears calm on the surface; indirect actions that show anger | Confused, frustrated, unable to address the real issue |
| **Assertive** | "I felt upset when I saw the damage. That item is important to me. Can we talk about how to handle this?" | Steady eye contact; calm, clear voice; open body language | Respected, clear on what's needed, able to respond |

**Key Insight:** Most of us have a *default* style -- the one we go to automatically, especially under stress. We might also use different styles in different relationships (with friends vs. family vs. authority figures). The goal isn't to judge your default -- it's to expand your options.

#### Participant Handout: Communication Styles Self-Assessment

**Directions:** For each situation below, read all four responses. Circle the letter (A, B, C, or D) of the response that is *most like what you would actually do* -- not what you think you *should* do. Be honest.

**Situation 1:** A classmate or co-worker takes credit for an idea you shared in a group.

- **A.** You stay quiet and don't say anything, but you feel resentful inside.
- **B.** You confront them loudly in front of the group: "That was MY idea and everyone knows it!"
- **C.** You say nothing directly, but later you make sarcastic comments about people who "steal ideas" when they're nearby.
- **D.** You speak to them privately: "I noticed the idea I mentioned earlier was presented as yours. I'd appreciate being credited when I contribute."

**Situation 2:** A friend keeps canceling plans with you at the last minute.

- **A.** You stop making plans with them but don't explain why.
- **B.** You send an angry text: "You're the worst friend. You NEVER follow through."
- **C.** You say "it's totally fine" when they cancel, then post indirect comments on social media about people who waste your time.
- **D.** You tell them: "When plans get canceled last minute, I feel like my time isn't valued. Can we figure out a way to make our plans more reliable?"

**Situation 3:** Someone in your family or household makes a decision that affects you without asking your opinion.

- **A.** You go along with it even though you disagree, telling yourself it doesn't matter.
- **B.** You blow up: "Nobody ever asks me anything! I don't even matter in this house!"
- **C.** You comply but do a poor job on purpose, or bring it up passive-aggressively weeks later.
- **D.** You say: "I'd like to be included in decisions that affect me. Can we talk about this one together?"

**Situation 4:** A peer says something that hurts your feelings during a group conversation.

- **A.** You laugh it off even though it stung, and try to move on.
- **B.** You fire back with a personal insult to "even the score."
- **C.** You act like it didn't bother you but give them the cold shoulder for the rest of the day.
- **D.** You pull them aside later: "Hey, that comment earlier actually hurt. I don't think you meant it that way, but I wanted to let you know."

**Situation 5:** You need help with something but everyone around you seems busy.

- **A.** You don't ask and try to figure it out alone, even if you struggle.
- **B.** You interrupt someone and demand their help immediately.
- **C.** You sigh loudly and make comments about how "nobody helps around here" until someone notices.
- **D.** You approach someone calmly: "I know you're busy, but I could use some help when you have a few minutes. Would that work?"

### Debrief: Self-Assessment Discussion

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Whole group discussion

#### Facilitator Instructions

1. **Open the debrief (1 minute):**
   - "Let's talk about what you noticed. You don't need to share your specific scores unless you want to."

2. **Discussion prompts (7 minutes):**
   - "Without sharing your exact results, was anyone surprised by what they found?"
   - "Did anyone notice they use different styles in different situations or relationships? What do you think drives that shift?"
   - "What do you think makes assertive communication harder than the other styles?"
   - "Why might someone choose a passive or passive-aggressive style even when they know assertive might work better?"

3. **Synthesize (2 minutes):**
   - Highlight that context matters -- sometimes being quiet (passive) is a survival strategy and has served a real purpose. The goal is having assertive communication as an option, not forcing it in every situation.
   - Transition: "Now that we know our patterns, let's build a specific tool for assertive communication -- the 'I' statement."

### Activity 2: "I" Statement Construction Practice

**Duration:** 15 minutes (5 min instruction + 10 min practice)
**Format:** Whole group instruction, then partner practice
**Materials:** "I" Statement Builder Worksheet handout

#### Facilitator Instructions

1. **Teach the formula (5 minutes):**
   - Write on the flip chart or display:

     > **"I feel __________ when __________ because __________. I need/would like __________."**

   - Walk through each part:
     - **"I feel..."** -- Name the emotion (one word: frustrated, hurt, worried, disrespected). Avoid "I feel like you..." which is actually a "you" statement in disguise.
     - **"When..."** -- Describe the specific behavior or situation factually, without judgment or exaggeration. Not "when you always..." but "when the dishes are left in the sink."
     - **"Because..."** -- Explain the impact on you. This is the part that helps the other person understand *why* it matters.
     - **"I need/would like..."** -- Make a clear, specific request. This gives the other person something actionable.

   - **Model two examples:**
     - "I feel worried when I don't hear back after texting, because I start thinking something is wrong. I'd like a quick reply even if it's just 'I'll text later.'"
     - "I feel frustrated when I'm interrupted during group discussions, because I lose my train of thought. I'd like to finish my point before others respond."

   - **Common mistakes to flag:**
     - "I feel like you don't care" -- This is a thought, not a feeling. Reframe: "I feel unimportant."
     - "I feel angry when you ALWAYS ignore me" -- "Always" is an exaggeration that triggers defensiveness. Be specific.
     - Making demands instead of requests: "I need you to stop" vs. "I'd like us to find a different approach."

2. **Partner practice (10 minutes):**
   - Pair participants up. Distribute the "I" Statement Builder Worksheet.
   - Partners take turns building "I" statements for three provided scenarios and one personal scenario.
   - Circulate, listen, and coach gently. Common coaching moments:
     - Helping participants separate feelings from thoughts
     - Encouraging specificity in the "when" clause
     - Making sure the request is realistic and actionable

#### Participant Handout: "I" Statement Builder Worksheet

**Building Stronger Communication, One Statement at a Time**

The "I" statement is one of the most powerful tools for assertive communication. It helps you express what you're feeling and what you need -- clearly and without blame.

**The Formula:**

> **"I feel __________ when __________ because __________. I need / would like __________."**

| Part | Purpose | Tips |
|------|---------|------|
| **I feel...** | Names your emotion | Use one feeling word (frustrated, hurt, anxious, disrespected). Avoid "I feel like you..." |
| **When...** | Describes the specific behavior or event | Be factual, not judgmental. Say what happened, not your interpretation of why |
| **Because...** | Explains the impact on you | Helps the other person understand why it matters to you |
| **I need / would like...** | Makes a clear request | Be specific and realistic. Give them something they can actually do |

### Activity 3: Role-Play Scenarios

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Small groups of 3-4
**Materials:** Role-Play Scenario Cards (one set per group)

#### Facilitator Instructions

1. **Setup (2 minutes):**
   - Divide into small groups of 3-4.
   - Distribute one set of scenario cards per group.
   - Explain the structure:
     - For each scenario, one person plays the speaker, one plays the other person in the situation, and one (or two) observes.
     - **Round 1:** The speaker responds to the scenario using their *default* style (whatever comes naturally). The observer names which style they saw.
     - **Round 2:** The speaker tries again using *assertive* communication and an "I" statement. The observer gives specific feedback.
     - Rotate roles for each scenario card.

2. **Practice time (10 minutes):**
   - Groups work through as many scenario cards as time allows (aim for at least 2-3 per group).
   - Circulate between groups. Listen more than intervene. Offer coaching only if a group is stuck or if someone is uncomfortable.

3. **Wrap role-play (3 minutes):**
   - Call time. Ask groups to finish their current round.
   - Brief check: "How did it feel to try the assertive version after doing your default? What did you notice?"

### Large Group Debrief

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Whole group discussion

#### Facilitator Instructions

1. **Harvest insights (6 minutes):**
   - "What was it like to try your default style first and then switch to assertive?"
   - "Which scenario was hardest to respond to assertively? Why?"
   - "Observers -- what differences did you notice between the default and assertive versions? In tone? In body language? In how the other person reacted?"

2. **Connect to peer support (4 minutes):**
   - "How does the way we communicate affect our ability to support our peers?"
   - "Can you think of a situation where using an 'I' statement could strengthen a peer relationship rather than damage it?"
   - Synthesize: "Assertive communication isn't about winning or being right. It's about being honest and respectful at the same time -- with others AND with ourselves. That's what makes it such a powerful skill for anyone who supports others."

## STAR Reflection Prompts

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Individual written reflection (5 minutes), then voluntary pair or whole-group sharing (5 minutes)

**Instructions to participants:** "Think about a real or realistic situation where communication style made a difference. Use the framework below to reflect. Write your answers -- you'll have the option to share with a partner or the group, but sharing is always your choice."

---

### Reflection Prompt Option A: A Time Communication Went Wrong

- **Situation:** Describe a time when a conversation didn't go well. Where were you? Who was involved? What was the context?
- **Task:** What were you trying to communicate or accomplish in that moment?
- **Action:** How did you actually communicate? Looking back, which communication style were you using? What specific words, tone, or body language did you use?
- **Result:** What happened as a result? How did the other person respond? How did you feel afterward? If you could redo it with an assertive "I" statement, what would you say differently?

---

### Reflection Prompt Option B: A Time Communication Went Right

- **Situation:** Describe a time when you communicated something difficult effectively. What was the setting? Who was the other person?
- **Task:** What did you need to express or accomplish?
- **Action:** What did you say or do? What style were you using? What made it effective?
- **Result:** How did the other person respond? What was the outcome for the relationship? What can you learn from that experience to use again in the future?

---

### Reflection Prompt Option C: Looking Forward

- **Situation:** Think about a current relationship or situation where communication is difficult or strained.
- **Task:** What do you need to communicate that you haven't yet? What outcome are you hoping for?
- **Action:** Draft an "I" statement or assertive response you could use. What style have you been defaulting to in this situation, and what would you change?
- **Result:** What do you think might happen if you shifted to assertive communication in this situation? What's the best realistic outcome? What support would you need to try it?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Informal Assessment (During Session)

Facilitators should observe and note the following throughout the session:

| Indicator | What to Look For |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Style identification** | Can participants accurately name the four styles when given examples? During role-play debrief, do they correctly identify which style was demonstrated? |
| **Self-awareness** | During self-assessment debrief, do participants show genuine reflection about their patterns? Do they recognize contextual differences (style shifts by relationship)? |
| **"I" statement construction** | Are participants producing "I" statements that include all four components? Are they avoiding common mistakes (thoughts as feelings, "always/never," demands vs. requests)? |
| **Assertive practice** | During role-play Round 2, are participants demonstrating a visible shift toward assertive communication? Is there evidence of improved body language, tone, and word choice? |

## Community Partner Integration

This module offers several meaningful integration points for community partners involved in the program:

### Before the Session
- **Guest speaker invitation:** Invite a community partner professional (counselor, mediator, youth program coordinator, social worker) to share a brief (5-minute) story about how assertive communication has been important in their professional role. This adds real-world relevance and exposes participants to community resources.
- **Scenario co-development:** Work with community partners to develop role-play scenarios that reflect communication challenges specific to the local community or population served. This increases relevance and engagement.

### During the Session
- **Co-facilitation of role-play:** A community partner familiar with conflict resolution or mediation can co-facilitate the role-play activity, providing expert coaching during small group practice.
- **Resource connection:** If a participant's self-assessment or reflection reveals communication challenges connected to deeper issues (family conflict, workplace difficulties, relationship concerns), the facilitator can make a warm referral to the community partner for follow-up support.

### After the Session
- **Practice partnerships:** Community partners who operate drop-in centers, mentorship programs, or youth groups can reinforce "I" statement practice in their own programming, creating continuity between sessions.
- **Follow-up workshops:** Partners specializing in conflict resolution, restorative practices, or family communication can offer optional follow-up workshops that build on Module 6 content.
- **Community practice challenge:** Partner organizations can support a "Communication Challenge" where participants practice one assertive conversation before the next session and report back. Partners can serve as accountability check-in points.

- School counseling departments (for cross-referral)
