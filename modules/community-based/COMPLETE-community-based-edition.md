# Module 1: Welcome + Values Activity

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Introduce themselves to peers using a structured prompt that highlights personal strengths and interests.
2. Collaboratively develop a set of group agreements that establish norms for respectful participation throughout the program.
3. Identify at least three personal core values and articulate why those values are important to them.
4. Connect their personal values to the goals of the group and the broader community.
5. Demonstrate active listening and affirming communication during group discussion.

---

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- This section is for facilitator use only. Do not distribute to participants.**

### Framework Alignment

This module aligns with the **SCBF 2.0** framework's foundational phase, which emphasizes building relational trust and establishing psychological safety before introducing skill-building content. The session draws on **Harrington's Community Learning Model** (Harrington, 2019), specifically the principle that belonging must precede engagement. **NAPS Standard 2 (Relationships and Interactions)** and **NAPS Standard 4 (Youth Voice and Leadership)** are addressed through the collaborative agreements process and the values identification activity.

### Setup Instructions

1. **Room arrangement:** Set up seating in a circle or U-shape. Avoid classroom-style rows. If tables are needed, use small clusters of 4-5 seats to facilitate small-group work. Leave an open area at the front for chart paper posting.
2. **Materials station:** Pre-set a materials station with markers, sticky notes, and name tag supplies so participants can access them during arrival.
3. **Values cards:** Pre-sort values card decks into individual sets. Each set should contain 30-40 value words (e.g., Family, Honesty, Respect, Education, Faith, Independence, Creativity, Service, Justice, Fun). Print on cardstock for durability. Include 3-5 blank cards per set so participants can write in values not represented.
4. **Chart paper:** Pre-write the heading "Our Group Agreements" on one sheet. Leave the rest blank for group input. On a second sheet, write "Values We Share" as a header for the closing gallery.
5. **Arrival buffer:** Plan for 5-10 minutes of informal arrival time. Community-based settings often have staggered arrivals. Use background music and the name tag activity to productively occupy early arrivers.

### Key Concepts to Emphasize

- **Psychological safety:** This session sets the emotional tone for every session that follows. Prioritize warmth, low-pressure participation, and genuine curiosity about each participant.
- **Peer-to-peer dynamic:** Position yourself as a facilitator, not a teacher. Use language like "we" and "our group" rather than "my class" or "you all need to."
- **Values as neutral:** During the values sort, emphasize that no value is better or worse than another. The goal is self-awareness, not hierarchy.
- **Voluntary depth:** Participants choose how much to share. Never pressure someone to disclose personal stories. Offer multiple entry points (writing, drawing, speaking, gesturing).

### Common Pitfalls

- **Rushing the agreements:** Groups need genuine ownership of their norms. Do not pre-write agreements and ask for rubber-stamp approval. Facilitate authentic negotiation even if it takes longer than planned. Draw extra time from Activity 3 if necessary.
- **Tokenizing participants:** During introductions, avoid singling out participants based on visible identity markers. Let participants define themselves.
- **Skipping the debrief:** The STAR reflection at the close is where learning consolidates. Protect this time.
- **Over-facilitating the values sort:** Let participants sit with ambiguity. Resist the urge to define values for them.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Icebreaker Introductions -- "Three Words and a Why"

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Whole group (circle)
**Materials:** Name tags (already created during arrival)

**Instructions:**

1. Gather participants into a standing or seated circle. If the group is larger than 15, split into two concentric circles.
2. Introduce the prompt: "We're going to go around the circle and share three things: your name, the name you want us to call you if it's different, and three words that describe who you are outside of this room. Then tell us why one of those words matters to you."
3. Model the activity yourself first. Example: "I'm Ms. Daniels -- you can call me Coach D. My three words are curious, outdoors, and loyal. Loyal matters to me because I grew up watching my grandmother show up for her neighbors no matter what, and I try to carry that with me."
4. Move around the circle. Thank each person after they share. Use brief, genuine affirmations ("Thank you for sharing that," "That's a powerful word," "I can tell that matters to you").
5. If a participant passes, say: "That's okay. If something comes to you later, we'd love to hear it." Do not pressure.
6. After the full round, offer one observation: "I'm noticing some themes already -- I heard a lot about [family/creativity/strength/etc.]. That tells me something great about this group."

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Welcome!**
>
> Create your name tag at the materials station. Write the name you want us to use.
>
> When it's your turn in the circle, share:
> - Your name (and what you'd like to be called)
> - Three words that describe who you are
> - Why one of those words matters to you
>
> There are no wrong answers. Share what feels right.

---

### Activity 2: Building Our Group Agreements

**Duration:** 25 minutes
**Format:** Small groups (4-5), then whole group
**Materials:** Chart paper (pre-headed "Our Group Agreements"), markers, sticky notes

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min) Frame the activity:** "Before we go deeper, we need to decide together how we want to treat each other in this space. These aren't rules I'm giving you -- these are agreements we make as a group. They belong to all of us."
2. **(3 min) Individual brainstorm:** Hand out sticky notes. Ask: "Think about a time you were in a group that felt safe, respectful, and productive. What made it that way? Write one idea per sticky note. Try to come up with at least two."
3. **(7 min) Small-group sort:** Have participants form groups of 4-5. Each group combines their sticky notes, looks for themes, and selects their top 3-4 agreements to propose to the whole group. Each group writes their proposals on chart paper in large letters.
4. **(10 min) Whole-group negotiation:** Each small group presents their proposals. Facilitator captures them on the main "Our Group Agreements" chart paper. Look for overlaps and consolidate. Invite discussion: "Does anyone want to add to this?" "Is there anything here that wouldn't work for you?" "What does [agreement] look like in practice?"
5. **(3 min) Finalize and commit:** Read the final list aloud. Ask: "Can everyone commit to these? If you're in, give a thumbs up." Post the agreements prominently in the meeting space. Let participants know these can be revisited and revised in future sessions.

**Facilitator tip:** Common agreements that emerge include: "Respect different opinions," "What's shared here stays here," "Step up / step back," "It's okay to disagree," "Phones on silent." If key safety agreements do not emerge organically (e.g., confidentiality), gently prompt: "What about when someone shares something personal? How do we want to handle that?"

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Building Our Group Agreements**
>
> Great groups don't happen by accident. They happen because people decide how they want to show up for each other.
>
> **Step 1 -- Think Back**
> Think about a time you were in a group that felt safe and respectful. What made it work? Write your ideas on sticky notes (one idea per note).
>
> **Step 2 -- Team Up**
> Join your small group. Share your sticky notes, find common themes, and choose your group's top 3-4 proposed agreements.
>
> **Step 3 -- Decide Together**
> We'll hear from every group and build one shared list of agreements for our time together. These agreements belong to all of us, and we can revisit them at any point.

### Activity 3: Values Card Sort -- "What Matters Most"

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Individual (8 min), then pairs (7 min), then small group share (5 min)
**Materials:** Values Card Sort deck (one per participant), Participant Handout 1.1, pens or pencils

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min) Introduce the activity:** "We just talked about how we want to treat each other. Now let's go a level deeper and explore what drives us -- our personal values. Values are the things that matter most to you. They shape your decisions, your relationships, and your goals."
2. **(8 min) Individual sort:** "Spread out your values cards. Read through them all first. Then sort them into three piles: Very Important to Me, Somewhat Important, and Not a Priority Right Now. There are blank cards if a value that matters to you isn't in the deck -- write it in. Once you've sorted, choose your top five. Then narrow it down to your top three. Record your top three on the handout."
3. **(7 min) Pair share:** "Turn to the person next to you. Share your top three values. For each one, briefly share why it made your list. Your partner's job is to listen and ask one curious question."
4. **(3 min) Small-group connection:** In groups of 4, have each person share just their number-one value and one sentence about why. On a shared sticky note, each group writes down any values they have in common.
5. Collect the "values in common" sticky notes for use in Activity 4.

**Facilitator tip:** Walk the room during the individual sort. Some participants may struggle to narrow down. Coaching language: "There are no wrong choices. This isn't permanent -- your values can shift over time. For today, go with your gut." If a participant finishes quickly, prompt them: "Look at your top three. Can you rank them? Which one would be hardest to give up?"

**Participant Handout Content (Handout 1.1: "My Values, My Voice"):**

> **My Values, My Voice**
>
> Values are the principles and beliefs that matter most to you. They guide how you make decisions, how you treat people, and what you stand for.
>
> **Part 1: Sort**
> Spread out your values cards. Sort them into three groups:
> - **Very Important to Me**
> - **Somewhat Important**
> - **Not a Priority Right Now**
>
> Don't overthink it. Go with your gut. If a value that matters to you isn't in the deck, write it on a blank card.
>
> **Part 2: Narrow Down**
> From your "Very Important" pile, choose your **top 5**. Then narrow to your **top 3**.
>
> | Rank | My Value | Why This Matters to Me |
> |------|----------|----------------------|
> | 1 | | |
> | 2 | | |
> | 3 | | |
>
> **Part 3: Share**
> With a partner, share your top three values and what they mean to you. Listen to your partner's values with curiosity. Ask at least one question.
>
> **My partner's top value:** ____________________
>
> **One thing I learned about my partner:** ____________________

---

### Activity 4: Values Gallery + Share-Out

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Whole group
**Materials:** "Values We Share" chart paper, sticky notes from Activity 3, markers, tape

**Instructions:**

1. **(3 min) Build the gallery:** Invite each small group to post their "values in common" sticky note on the "Values We Share" chart paper. As groups post, read a few aloud.
2. **(5 min) Facilitated share-out:** Stand near the chart and lead a brief discussion:
   - "What do you notice about the values on this chart?"
   - "Are there any surprises?"
   - "What does it tell us about who we are as a group?"
   - "How might these shared values help us as we work together in the sessions ahead?"
3. **(2 min) Bridge to the future:** "These values aren't just words on a wall. They're a compass. As we move through this program together, we'll keep coming back to what matters to us -- individually and as a group. Your values will help guide the work we do, the conversations we have, and the goals we set."

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use the following prompts to guide the closing reflection. Facilitator reads each prompt aloud and gives participants 1-2 minutes to respond (verbally in pairs, or by writing on a notecard). Select 2 of the 3 prompts based on time and group energy.

### Prompt 1: Group Agreements Reflection

- **Situation:** Today, you worked with a group of people you may not have known before to create shared agreements for how this group will operate.
- **Task:** Your group needed to come to consensus on norms that everyone could commit to.
- **Action:** What did you or your group do to make sure everyone's voice was heard during the agreements process?
- **Result:** How do you feel about the agreements we created? What is one agreement you are most committed to honoring, and why?

### Prompt 2: Values Discovery Reflection

- **Situation:** You were given a set of values cards and asked to identify what matters most to you.
- **Task:** You needed to narrow a large set of values down to your personal top three.
- **Action:** What strategy did you use to make your choices? Was it easy or difficult to narrow them down?
- **Result:** What did you learn about yourself through this activity? Did anything surprise you about your own values or your partner's values?

### Prompt 3: Showing Up Reflection

- **Situation:** This was the first session of a new program, and you walked into a space with people you may not know well.
- **Task:** You were asked to introduce yourself, participate in group activities, and share something personal about your values.
- **Action:** How did you decide to show up today? What helped you feel comfortable (or what was challenging)?
- **Result:** What is one thing you want to carry into the next session -- about yourself, this group, or what we're building together?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

Facilitators should use the following methods to gauge participant learning and engagement. These are observational and formative -- no grades or scores are assigned.

**During the session, look for:**

- [ ] Participants can name at least three personal values and articulate a brief reason for their importance (observed during pair share and handout completion).
- [ ] All participants contributed at least one idea to the group agreements process (observed during sticky note brainstorm and whole-group negotiation).
- [ ] Participants demonstrate active listening during peer introductions and values sharing (eye contact, nodding, asking follow-up questions, not interrupting).
- [ ] The group's posted agreements reflect authentic negotiation rather than surface-level compliance (evidence: discussion during the finalization step, range of ideas represented).

**Exit Ticket (optional):**

On a notecard or sticky note, have each participant write:

1. One value that is important to me: __________
2. One group agreement I'm committed to: __________
3. One word that describes how I feel about being part of this group: __________

Collect the notecards. Review them after the session to identify participants who may need additional support, connection, or encouragement.

## Community Partner Integration

This module includes the following touchpoints for community partner involvement. Coordinate with partners in advance of the session.

### Before the Session
- **Partner introduction:** If a community partner (e.g., site host, mentoring organization representative, local business sponsor) is present, invite them to briefly introduce themselves during the opening (1-2 minutes maximum). Frame their role as part of the community supporting participants.
- **Space and logistics:** Community partners hosting the session site can support with room setup, signage, snacks, and welcoming participants at the door.

### During the Session
- **Activity 2 (Group Agreements):** Invite a community partner to participate in the small-group brainstorm as an equal member, not an authority figure. Their presence models intergenerational or cross-sector collaboration.
- **Activity 3 (Values Card Sort):** Community partners can share their own top value and a brief story about how that value shapes their work in the community. This builds relational bridges between participants and local institutions.

### After the Session
- **Debrief with partner:** Share general themes (not individual participant data) from the session with community partners. Discuss how shared values can inform future community-based projects or service-learning opportunities.
- **Ongoing integration:** Community partners can reference the group's posted agreements and shared values chart in future interactions, reinforcing continuity.

# Module 2: Who Am I?

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify at least five distinct dimensions of their personal identity (e.g., cultural background, interests, values, roles, aspirations).

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- This section is for facilitator use only. Do not distribute to participants.**

### Framework Alignment

This module maps to the **SCBF 2.0** framework's foundational domain of Self-Awareness and Identity Development. The session operationalizes the concept of "shared lived experience" (Harrington, 2019) by guiding participants to explore their own identities before connecting those identities to peers in later modules. Per **NAPS** Quality Standards, this session addresses Element 3.1 (Youth Development -- Supporting Social-Emotional Growth) and Element 4.2 (Culturally Responsive Programming).

### Key Concepts for the Facilitator

- **Identity is multi-dimensional.** Participants may initially default to surface-level descriptors (name, age, grade). Your role is to model depth by sharing your own identity dimensions and gently prompting beyond the obvious.
- **Shared lived experience.** This module builds the foundation for the "shared lived experience" concept that becomes explicit in Modules 4-6. At this stage, do NOT label the concept for participants. Instead, let organic connections emerge during small-group sharing. Participants will begin noticing overlaps in their identity maps naturally.
- **Strengths-based framing.** All activities in this module are designed around asset-based language. Redirect any deficit-oriented self-talk ("I'm bad at...") toward reframing ("Something I'm still working on is...").
- **Cultural humility.** Some participants may include cultural, religious, or gender-identity dimensions that feel vulnerable. Reinforce community agreements from Module 1. Never require participants to share anything they are not comfortable with.

### Common Pitfalls

- **Rushing the identity map.** The identity map is the anchor activity. If you are short on time, compress the closing rather than cutting identity map work time. Depth matters more than speed here.
- **Over-sharing as facilitator.** Model vulnerability at an appropriate level. Share enough to normalize the exercise but not so much that participants feel they must match your depth.
- **Comparison culture.** During small-group sharing, watch for participants who begin comparing identities competitively ("I have more strengths than you"). Redirect toward curiosity: "What can you learn from how someone else sees themselves?"
- **Quiet participants.** Some participants may be reluctant to share in groups. The worksheet provides a private, written channel. Affirm that the written work is complete participation -- sharing aloud is an invitation, not a requirement.

- [ ] Art supplies (markers, colored pencils) distributed to each table

## Activities

### Activity 1: Identity Mapping
**Duration:** 25 minutes
**Format:** Individual guided work with brief facilitator modeling
**Materials:** "Who Am I?" Identity Map worksheet, colored markers or pencils, pens

**Instructions:**

1. **(3 min) Facilitator Modeling.** Display a sample identity map (your own or a fictional example) on chart paper. Walk participants through the structure: a center circle with their name, surrounded by connected bubbles representing different parts of who they are. Show at least six dimensions: things you value, roles you play, places that matter to you, people who shaped you, things you are good at, and hopes for your future. Emphasize that there are no wrong answers.

2. **(2 min) Distribute and Orient.** Hand out the "Who Am I?" Identity Map worksheet. Read through the category prompts together. Clarify that participants can add their own categories beyond those listed.

3. **(18 min) Individual Work Time.** Invite participants to complete their identity maps. Encourage use of color, drawings, symbols, or words -- whatever feels right. Circulate quietly. Offer gentle prompts to participants who seem stuck:
   - "What is something you do that makes you feel most like yourself?"
   - "Who is someone that has shaped the way you see the world?"
   - "If your best friend described you, what would they say?"
   - "What is one thing about you that people might be surprised to learn?"

4. **(2 min) Pause and Appreciate.** Ask participants to set down their markers and take a moment to look at what they created. Prompt: "Notice how many different things make up who you are. No single word or label could capture all of this."

**Participant Handout Content:**

---

> **Who Am I? -- Identity Map**
>

> **Center Circle:** My Name
>
> **Surrounding Bubbles (fill in as many as you like):**
>
> - **My Culture & Background:** Where does your family come from? What traditions matter to you?

> - **My Interests & Passions:** What do you love to do? What could you talk about for hours?
> - **My Strengths:** What are you good at? What do people come to you for?

### Activity 3: Small Group Sharing Circle
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Small groups of 4-5 participants, structured sharing rounds
**Materials:** Identity Map worksheets (completed), chart paper, sticky notes, markers

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min) Set Up Sharing Norms.** Bring attention back to the whole group. Review sharing expectations:
   - "Share what you are comfortable sharing. You can pass on any round."
   - "When someone is sharing, give them your full attention."
   - "What is shared in the circle stays in the circle."
   - "After someone shares, respond with curiosity -- ask a question or share what you appreciated."

2. **(10 min) Structured Sharing Rounds.** In their table groups, participants complete two sharing rounds:

   **Round 1 -- Identity Snapshot (5 min):** Each person shares two things from their identity map -- one that is visible to most people and one that might surprise others. After each share, the group offers one brief affirmation or follow-up question.

   **Round 2 -- Strength in Action (5 min):** Each person shares one strength and a brief example of how they have used it. Group members write a sticky note to that person with something they appreciated or connected with and hand it to them.

# Module 3: What is Peer Support?

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Define peer support in their own words and identify at least three core principles that guide it.

### Conceptual Foundation

This module is the conceptual cornerstone of the curriculum. It establishes the working definition of peer support that all subsequent modules build upon. The session design draws on the **Harrington framework** for peer support, which emphasizes mutuality, shared lived experience, self-determination, and a non-hierarchical relationship between supporter and supported. Facilitators should internalize these four pillars because they recur throughout later modules (especially Modules 4, 5, 9, and 11).

### Standards Alignment

## Activities

### Activity 1: Defining Peer Support Together

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Full group brainstorm followed by structured synthesis
**Materials:** Easel pad, markers (3-4 colors), sticky notes (one color), pens, Participant Handout 3A

**Instructions:**

1. **(3 minutes)** Open with the prompt written on the easel pad: *"What does support from a peer look like?"* Ask participants to think silently for 60 seconds, then write one idea per sticky note (2-3 notes each).

2. **(5 minutes)** Invite participants to post their sticky notes on the easel pad. As they post, ask a few volunteers to read theirs aloud. Facilitator clusters similar ideas together in real time, narrating the emerging themes: *"I'm noticing several of you mentioned listening. And these over here are about not judging someone. Let's keep going."*

3. **(5 minutes)** Facilitate a brief discussion drawing out core principles:
   - *"What do these clusters have in common?"*
   - *"What makes support from a peer different from support from an adult professional?"*
   - *"Does the person giving support need to have gone through the same thing? Why or why not?"*

4. **(4 minutes)** Using the group's language, co-construct a working definition on the easel pad. Write it in a color that stands out. A sample scaffold if the group needs help:

   > "Peer support is when someone who has been through similar experiences offers understanding, encouragement, and practical help to someone else -- as an equal, not an expert."

5. **(3 minutes)** Distribute **Handout 3A** and ask participants to write the group's definition in the space provided at the top. Then review the four core principles listed on the handout, connecting each one back to the sticky notes already on the wall.

**Facilitator Tip:** Resist the urge to provide the "correct" definition first. The learning lives in the co-construction. If critical principles are missing from the group's version, weave them in through follow-up questions rather than direct correction.

### Activity 2: Role Comparison -- Who Does What?

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Think-pair-share followed by full-group chart completion
**Materials:** Participant Handout 3B, pens, easel pad with pre-drawn comparison chart

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 minutes)** Distribute **Handout 3B**. Introduce the activity: *"We've defined peer support. Now let's get clearer on what makes a peer supporter different from other people in a helping role. We're going to compare three roles: a peer supporter, a professional counselor or therapist, and a friend."*

2. **(5 minutes -- Think-Pair)** Ask participants to work in pairs. Each pair should discuss and fill in at least two characteristics for each column on Handout 3B. Circulate and listen for strong examples to lift up during the full-group share.

3. **(8 minutes -- Share)** Bring the group back together. Using the easel pad chart, fill in each column by soliciting contributions from pairs. Probe for clarity:
   - *"Can a friend also be a peer supporter? What would have to change?"*
   - *"What can a counselor do that a peer supporter should not do?"*
   - *"What can a peer supporter do that a counselor cannot?"*

4. **(5 minutes)** Facilitate a closing discussion about what sits in the overlapping space and what is unique to each role. Key takeaways to surface:
   - A peer supporter shares experience; a counselor provides clinical expertise. Both matter.
   - A friend has personal investment in the relationship; a peer supporter maintains a different kind of care that includes intentional boundaries.
   - Peer support is a *role*, not just a personality trait. It can be learned and practiced.

**Facilitator Tip:** Participants sometimes worry that peer support sounds like "being a therapist without a license." Name this concern directly: *"Peer support is not therapy. It is its own thing, and it is powerful precisely because it is different."*

### Activity 3: "Is This Peer Support?" Scenarios

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Small groups (4-5 per group) with scenario cards, followed by full-group debrief
**Materials:** Participant Handout 3C (pre-cut scenario cards, one set per group), sticky notes (second color), pens

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 minutes)** Organize participants into small groups of 4-5. Distribute one set of scenario cards (Handout 3C) and a stack of sticky notes (second color) to each group.

2. **(10 minutes -- Small Group Work)** Each group reads through their six scenario cards. For each scenario, the group must:
   - Decide together: **Yes, this is peer support** / **No, this is not peer support** / **It depends**
   - Write their reasoning on a sticky note and attach it to the card
   - If they say "It depends," they must identify what would need to change to make it peer support

3. **(8 minutes -- Debrief)** Bring all groups back. Select 3-4 scenarios that generated the most discussion or disagreement. For each:
   - Ask the group to share their verdict and reasoning
   - Invite other groups to agree, disagree, or add nuance
   - Highlight where boundaries show up: *"Notice how in this scenario, the peer supporter is doing something that really belongs to a counselor. That's a boundary we'll explore much more in a later session."*

**Facilitator Tip:** The "It depends" option is intentionally included. Real-world peer support is rarely black and white. Celebrate groups that engage with the gray areas -- that is where the deepest learning happens.

The additional 30 minutes in this community-based edition are allocated to extra paired practice rotations and an extended barrier-identification brainstorm. Do not shorten practice time to accommodate lecture; the research base is clear that skill acquisition requires repetition.
- **Pair rotations.** Plan for a minimum of three practice rounds with different partners. Rotating partners exposes participants to varied communication styles and prevents comfort-zone stagnation. If your group is smaller than eight, allow repeat pairings but change roles (speaker/listener) each round.
- **Modeling matters.** Before the first paired exercise, conduct a live demonstration with a co-facilitator or a prepared volunteer. Show both a "poor listening" example and an "active listening" example side by side. Exaggerate contrasts so participants can clearly identify the differences.
- **Emotional safety.** Listening exercises can surface personal stories. Establish ground rules before practice begins: participants choose what to share, no one is required to disclose anything sensitive, and what is shared in practice stays in the room.
- **Common pitfalls:**
  - Facilitators sometimes rush the debrief after paired exercises. Protect at least five minutes for whole-group reflection after each rotation block.
  - Participants may confuse "giving advice" with "reflective listening." Be prepared to redirect with the phrase: "Right now we are practicing hearing, not helping."
  - Watch for participants who dominate the speaker role. Use the timer and enforce equal turns.

### Room Setup

- Arrange chairs in pairs facing each other, spread across the room so pairs have conversational privacy.
- Keep a clear central area for the opening demonstration and whole-group debrief.
- Post chart paper on walls for the barrier brainstorm activity.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Listening Skills Demonstration
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Facilitator-led whole group
**Materials:** None required (optional: two chairs at the front of the room)

**Instructions:**

1. Invite participants to observe carefully. Explain that they will see two short conversations and their job is to spot the differences.
2. **Round A -- Passive Hearing (2 minutes):** The facilitator (or co-facilitator pair) acts out a conversation where the "listener" checks their phone, looks around the room, gives one-word responses, interrupts, and changes the subject. The "speaker" tries to share a story about something meaningful to them.
3. **Round B -- Active Listening (2 minutes):** Replay a similar conversation, but now the listener maintains eye contact, nods, paraphrases what the speaker says, asks follow-up questions, and waits before responding.
4. After both rounds, facilitate a brief group discussion (4 minutes):
   - "What did you notice was different between the two conversations?"
   - "How do you think the speaker felt in each scenario?"
   - "Which specific behaviors made the biggest difference?"
5. Capture participant observations on the whiteboard in two columns: "What listeners do" and "What listeners avoid."

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Observation Guide -- Listening Demonstration**
>
> Watch both conversations carefully. After each one, jot down your answers:
>
> **Conversation A:**
> - What did the listener do with their body? ___________________________
> - How did the listener respond to what was being said? ___________________________
> - How do you think the speaker felt? ___________________________
>
> **Conversation B:**
> - What did the listener do differently? ___________________________
> - What words or phrases did the listener use? ___________________________
> - How do you think the speaker felt this time? ___________________________
>
> **Your takeaway:** One thing I want to practice from what I observed: ___________________________

### Activity 2: Verbal and Nonverbal Cues Mini-Lesson
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Format:** Interactive whole-group discussion with handout
**Materials:** "Active Listening Cue Cards" handout, whiteboard

**Instructions:**

1. Distribute the Active Listening Cue Cards.
2. Walk through each category, asking participants to contribute examples from their own experience before revealing the card content.
3. For nonverbal cues, invite volunteers to demonstrate each one (e.g., "Show me what attentive posture looks like").
4. For verbal cues, practice as a group: read a sample statement aloud and ask three participants to offer a paraphrasing response.
5. Emphasize that active listening is a combination of verbal and nonverbal behaviors working together.

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Active Listening Cue Cards**
>
> **Nonverbal Cues -- What Your Body Says**
>
> | Cue | What It Looks Like |
> |-----|-------------------|
> | Eye contact | Looking at the speaker naturally (not staring); glancing away briefly is normal |
> | Open posture | Facing the speaker, arms uncrossed, leaning slightly forward |
> | Nodding | Small, periodic nods to show you are following along |
> | Facial expression | Matching your expression to the tone of what is being shared (concern, enthusiasm, curiosity) |
> | Stillness | Putting away distractions; keeping hands calm; not fidgeting with objects |
>
> **Verbal Cues -- What Your Words Say**
>
> | Cue | Example Phrases |
> |-----|----------------|
> | Paraphrasing | "So what I hear you saying is..." / "It sounds like..." |
> | Clarifying questions | "Can you tell me more about...?" / "What did you mean when you said...?" |
> | Reflecting feelings | "That sounds like it was really frustrating." / "It seems like that made you proud." |
> | Summarizing | "Let me make sure I understand -- you're saying that..." |
> | Minimal encouragers | "Mm-hmm." / "Go on." / "I see." / "Tell me more." |
>
> **Remember:** Active listening is not about having the perfect response. It is about making the other person feel genuinely heard.

### Activity 3: Paired Practice Exercises
**Duration:** 25 minutes (three 7-minute rounds with 1-minute transitions)
**Format:** Paired practice with rotating partners
**Materials:** "Reflective Listening Practice Scenarios" handout (one per pair), timer

**Instructions:**

1. Organize participants into pairs. Designate one person as Partner A (speaker) and one as Partner B (listener).
2. Distribute the scenario sheet. Each round uses a different scenario prompt.
3. Explain the structure for each 7-minute round:
   - Speaker talks for 3 minutes using the scenario prompt as a starting point. They may share a real experience or create one.
   - Listener practices active listening skills for the full 3 minutes. No advice-giving, no problem-solving -- only listening, paraphrasing, and asking clarifying questions.
   - Both partners spend 1 minute giving each other feedback: "One thing you did that made me feel heard was..." and "One thing you could try next time is..."
4. After each round, call time and instruct participants to find a new partner. Roles switch (previous speakers become listeners).
5. Circulate during practice. Offer quiet, specific feedback to pairs. Watch for advice-giving disguised as listening and gently redirect.

**Round Transition Protocol:**
- Sound the chime or call "Rotate!"
- Participants stand, find a new partner, and sit down.
- Announce the new scenario number and which partner speaks first.
- Allow 30 seconds for settling, then start the timer.

**Participant Handout Content:**

> **Reflective Listening Practice Scenarios**
>
> *Instructions: In each round, the speaker uses the prompt below as a starting point. You may share a real experience or make one up -- the goal is to give your partner something meaningful to listen to. The listener practices the cues from the Active Listening Cue Cards.*
>
> **Round 1 Prompt:**
> Tell your partner about a time you worked on something as part of a team. What was the experience like? What went well, and what was challenging?
>
> **Round 2 Prompt:**
> Describe a situation where you felt someone really listened to you -- or a time when you felt like no one was listening. What made the difference?
>
> **Round 3 Prompt:**
> Share something you are working toward right now -- a goal, a project, or a change you want to make in your life or community. What matters to you about it?
>
> ---
>
> **Feedback Sentence Starters (use after each round):**
> - "One thing you did that made me feel heard was..."
> - "I noticed you used [specific cue] when you..."
> - "One thing you could try next time is..."
> - "I felt most listened to when you..."

### Activity 4: Barrier Identification Brainstorm
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Gallery walk and small-group brainstorm
**Materials:** Three sheets of chart paper posted on walls (pre-labeled), markers, sticky notes (two colors)

**Instructions:**

1. Post three chart paper sheets around the room, each labeled with a barrier category:
   - **Station 1:** "Internal Barriers" (things happening inside us that block listening)
   - **Station 2:** "External Barriers" (things in our environment that block listening)
   - **Station 3:** "Relational Barriers" (things between people that block listening)
2. Divide participants into three groups. Each group starts at one station.
3. **Round 1 (3 minutes):** Groups brainstorm barriers on sticky notes (one barrier per note, using Color A) and post them on the chart paper.
4. **Rotate.** Groups move to the next station.
5. **Round 2 (3 minutes):** Groups read what the previous group wrote, then add new barriers they did not see listed. They also use Color B sticky notes to write one strategy for overcoming each barrier they find most relevant.
6. **Rotate** one final time. Groups review both barriers and strategies at the third station and star the top two strategies they think are most practical.
7. **Whole-group share (4 minutes):** Each group presents the top two strategies from the station they ended at. Facilitator synthesizes themes on the whiteboard.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL USE ONLY -- This section is for facilitator reference and should not be distributed to participants.**

### Conceptual Foundation

This module draws on Harrington's model of empathic development, which positions empathy as a learnable skill set rather than a fixed trait. Harrington identifies three dimensions of empathy relevant to youth and community development work:

1. **Cognitive empathy** -- the ability to understand another person's perspective and mental state.
2. **Affective empathy** -- the capacity to share or resonate with another person's emotional experience.
3. **Behavioral empathy** -- the outward expression of understanding through responsive action and communication.

This session emphasizes cognitive and behavioral empathy, building directly on the active listening skills from Module 4. Participants who struggled with listening fundamentals may need additional scaffolding during the scenario practice activities.

### SCBF 2.0 Alignment

This module addresses the SCBF 2.0 Social-Emotional Competency strand, specifically the interpersonal skill cluster. Empathy is positioned as a bridge skill between listening (Module 4) and conflict resolution (Module 6). The session arc follows the SCBF 2.0 experiential learning cycle: Experience, Reflect, Conceptualize, Apply.

### NAPS Alignment

- **Standard 3:** Positive Interactions and Relationships
- **Standard 4:** Youth Voice, Engagement, and Leadership

### Key Facilitation Considerations

- **Emotional safety is paramount.** Empathy exercises can surface personal experiences of being misunderstood or dismissed. Establish and reinforce group agreements before beginning.
- **Avoid the "empathy performance" trap.** Participants may try to say what sounds right rather than genuinely engage. Normalize imperfection and emphasize practice over polish.
- **Cultural responsiveness.** Expressions of empathy vary across cultures. Avoid prescribing a single "correct" way to show empathy. Invite participants to share how empathy looks and sounds in their families and communities.
- **Connection to Module 4.** Explicitly reference the listening skills participants practiced previously. Empathic responding is an extension of active listening, not a replacement for it. If your group did not complete Module 4, budget an additional 5-10 minutes for a brief listening review at the start of this session.

### Video Selection Guidance

Select a short video (3-5 minutes) that clearly illustrates the difference between empathy and sympathy. Recommended options:

- Brene Brown's animated short on empathy (RSA, approx. 3 minutes)
- Any age-appropriate clip that demonstrates someone attempting to comfort another person using sympathy ("at least...," "it could be worse...") versus empathy ("that sounds really hard," "I'm here with you")

Preview the video before the session to confirm it is appropriate for your participant group.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Empathy vs. Sympathy -- Video and Discussion

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Whole group
**Materials:** Video clip, projector/screen, chart paper, markers

#### Setup

Write two column headers on chart paper before the session begins: **"Empathy Sounds Like..."** and **"Sympathy Sounds Like..."**

#### Instructions

1. **Frame the activity** (2 minutes):
   - "In our last session, we practiced active listening -- really hearing what someone is saying. Today we're going to build on that. We're going to explore what it means to truly understand what someone is feeling and to respond in a way that lets them know they've been heard and understood."
   - "We're going to start with a short video that shows the difference between two things that might seem similar but are actually very different: empathy and sympathy."

2. **Play the video** (3-5 minutes):
   - Ask participants to watch for specific moments where they can tell the difference between the two approaches.

3. **Facilitate discussion** (8-10 minutes):
   - "What stood out to you?"
   - "When someone responds with sympathy, how might the other person feel?"
   - "When someone responds with empathy, how might the other person feel?"
   - "Can you think of a time when someone responded to you with sympathy when you really needed empathy? What was that like?" (Invite but do not pressure sharing.)

4. **Chart responses:**
   - Record participant observations on the two-column chart.
   - Aim for at least 4-5 entries per column.

#### Key Distinctions to Surface

| Empathy | Sympathy |
|---------|----------|
| "I'm with you in this." | "I feel sorry for you." |
| Connects | Creates distance |
| Acknowledges feelings | Tries to fix or minimize |
| Requires vulnerability | Stays on the surface |
| "That sounds really hard." | "At least it's not worse." |
| Listens to understand | Listens to respond |

### Mini-Lesson: What Empathic Responding Looks Like

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Whole group with brief partner check-ins
**Materials:** Empathic Response Stems handout

#### Facilitator Talking Points

1. **Empathy is a skill, not a personality trait.**
   - "Some people think you're either an empathetic person or you're not. That's not true. Empathy is something we can all practice and get better at. That's exactly what we're going to do today."

2. **Three parts of an empathic response:**
   - **Attend:** Use your body to show you are present. Face the person. Make appropriate eye contact. Put away distractions.
   - **Acknowledge:** Name or reflect the emotion you are hearing. You don't have to be exactly right -- the other person will correct you, and that's okay.
   - **Ask:** Invite the person to share more, on their terms. Don't interrogate. Don't rush to advice.

3. **Common pitfalls to avoid:**
   - Jumping to advice ("You should...")
   - Making it about yourself ("That happened to me too, let me tell you...")
   - Minimizing ("It's not that bad" or "Everything happens for a reason")
   - Interrogating ("Why did you do that?" or "What were you thinking?")

4. **Distribute the Empathic Response Stems handout** and walk through several examples.

---

#### Participant Handout: Empathic Response Stems

> Use these sentence starters to practice responding with empathy. You don't have to use them word-for-word -- let them guide you toward responding in a way that helps the other person feel heard and understood.

**Acknowledging Feelings:**
- "It sounds like you're feeling..."
- "That must be really..."
- "I can hear how [frustrated / hurt / overwhelmed / excited] you are."
- "It makes sense that you'd feel that way."

**Showing Presence:**
- "I'm here. I'm listening."
- "Thank you for trusting me with this."
- "You don't have to go through this alone."
- "I may not fully understand, but I want to."

**Inviting More:**
- "Do you want to tell me more about that?"
- "What was that like for you?"
- "What would be most helpful right now?"
- "Is there more you want to share, or do you just need someone to sit with you?"

**When You're Not Sure What to Say:**
- "I don't know exactly what to say, but I'm glad you told me."
- "That sounds really hard. I'm here."
- Sometimes silence and presence are the most empathic response of all.

### Activity 2: Empathic Response Practice (Pairs)

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Format:** Partners
**Materials:** Empathic Response Stems handout, timer

#### Instructions for Participants

1. **Find a partner.** Decide who will be Person A and who will be Person B.

2. **Round 1 (5 minutes):**

- "You worked really hard on a project for school or work, and nobody acknowledged your effort."
- "A friend made plans with someone else after telling you they were busy."
- "You're dealing with a big change at home and you're not sure how to feel about it."
- "Someone you look up to said something that really hurt your feelings."

### Activity 3: Scenario Cards -- Guided Group Practice

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Small groups (3-4 participants), rotating stations
**Materials:** Printed Scenario Cards (below), timer

#### Setup

- Arrange 4-5 stations around the room, each with one Scenario Card placed face-down.
- Divide participants into small groups of 3-4.
- Each group starts at one station.

#### Instructions for Participants

1. At your station, turn over the Scenario Card and read it aloud as a group.
2. As a group, discuss:
   - What might this person be feeling?
   - What do they need right now?
   - What would an empathic response sound like?
   - What would a sympathetic (but unhelpful) response sound like?
3. Choose one person to practice delivering the empathic response out loud while others observe and offer kind feedback.
4. When the facilitator signals (after approximately 4-5 minutes), rotate to the next station.
5. Complete at least 3 stations.

#### Scenario Cards

**Scenario Card A:**
> Jaylen comes to your community group looking upset. When you ask what's going on, Jaylen says, "My mom and I got into a huge argument this morning. She said I never help out at home, but I've been trying really hard. She just doesn't see it."

*Consider: What emotions might Jaylen be experiencing? How can you respond without taking sides or offering solutions?*

---

**Scenario Card B:**
> Maria just found out she didn't get accepted into a summer program she applied to. She says, "I don't even know why I try. I'm never good enough for anything."

*Consider: Maria may be feeling more than just disappointment. How can you acknowledge her pain without minimizing it or jumping to reassurance?*

---

**Scenario Card C:**
> Devon is new to the neighborhood and has been coming to community sessions for three weeks. Today Devon is sitting alone and says, "Everyone here already knows each other. I feel like I don't belong."

*Consider: How can you respond in a way that validates Devon's experience without dismissing it ("Oh, everyone feels that way at first!")?*

---

**Scenario Card D:**
> Aisha tells you that her best friend just moved to another city. She says, "I know it's not a big deal, people move all the time. But I just feel really alone now."

*Consider: Aisha is already minimizing her own feelings. How can your response give her permission to feel what she's feeling?*

---

**Scenario Card E:**
> Marcus just got into a heated disagreement with another participant during an activity. Afterward, he comes to you and says, "Everyone thinks I was wrong, but nobody even asked my side. Nobody ever listens to me."

*Consider: Marcus is both frustrated and hurt. How can you respond to both emotions? How does this connect to the listening skills from our last session?*

#### Participant Handout: Communication Styles Overview

**How Do You Say What You Mean?**

We all have ways of expressing ourselves, especially when we feel strongly about something. Understanding these patterns is the first step toward communicating in ways that build stronger relationships.

**Four Common Communication Styles:**

| Style | What It Sounds Like | What It Looks Like | How Others May Feel |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| **Passive** | "It's fine, don't worry about it..." (even when it's not fine) | Avoids eye contact; quiet voice; closed body language | Confused, unsure what the person actually needs |
| **Aggressive** | "You always do this! You don't even care about my stuff!" | Loud voice; pointed finger; tense posture; may invade space | Defensive, scared, angry, shut down |
| **Passive-Aggressive** | "No, it's fine..." (slams door, gives silent treatment, makes sarcastic comments later) | Appears calm on the surface; indirect actions that show anger | Confused, frustrated, unable to address the real issue |
| **Assertive** | "I felt upset when I saw the damage. That item is important to me. Can we talk about how to handle this?" | Steady eye contact; calm, clear voice; open body language | Respected, clear on what's needed, able to respond |

**Key Insight:** Most of us have a *default* style -- the one we go to automatically, especially under stress. We might also use different styles in different relationships (with friends vs. family vs. authority figures). The goal isn't to judge your default -- it's to expand your options.

### Activity 2: "I" Statement Construction Practice

**Duration:** 15 minutes (5 min instruction + 10 min practice)
**Format:** Whole group instruction, then partner practice
**Materials:** "I" Statement Builder Worksheet handout

#### Facilitator Instructions

1. **Teach the formula (5 minutes):**
   - Write on the flip chart or display:

     > **"I feel __________ when __________ because __________. I need/would like __________."**

   - Walk through each part:
     - **"I feel..."** -- Name the emotion (one word: frustrated, hurt, worried, disrespected). Avoid "I feel like you..." which is actually a "you" statement in disguise.
     - **"When..."** -- Describe the specific behavior or situation factually, without judgment or exaggeration. Not "when you always..." but "when the dishes are left in the sink."
     - **"Because..."** -- Explain the impact on you. This is the part that helps the other person understand *why* it matters.
     - **"I need/would like..."** -- Make a clear, specific request. This gives the other person something actionable.

   - **Model two examples:**
     - "I feel worried when I don't hear back after texting, because I start thinking something is wrong. I'd like a quick reply even if it's just 'I'll text later.'"
     - "I feel frustrated when I'm interrupted during group discussions, because I lose my train of thought. I'd like to finish my point before others respond."

   - **Common mistakes to flag:**
     - "I feel like you don't care" -- This is a thought, not a feeling. Reframe: "I feel unimportant."
     - "I feel angry when you ALWAYS ignore me" -- "Always" is an exaggeration that triggers defensiveness. Be specific.
     - Making demands instead of requests: "I need you to stop" vs. "I'd like us to find a different approach."

2. **Partner practice (10 minutes):**
   - Pair participants up. Distribute the "I" Statement Builder Worksheet.
   - Partners take turns building "I" statements for three provided scenarios and one personal scenario.
   - Circulate, listen, and coach gently. Common coaching moments:
     - Helping participants separate feelings from thoughts
     - Encouraging specificity in the "when" clause
     - Making sure the request is realistic and actionable

**Building Stronger Communication, One Statement at a Time**

The "I" statement is one of the most powerful tools for assertive communication. It helps you express what you're feeling and what you need -- clearly and without blame.

**The Formula:**

> **"I feel __________ when __________ because __________. I need / would like __________."**

| Part | Purpose | Tips |
|------|---------|------|
| **I feel...** | Names your emotion | Use one feeling word (frustrated, hurt, anxious, disrespected). Avoid "I feel like you..." |

| **Because...** | Explains the impact on you | Helps the other person understand why it matters to you |
| **I need / would like...** | Makes a clear request | Be specific and realistic. Give them something they can actually do |

### Activity 1: The Unspoken Message
**Duration:** 20 minutes | **Format:** Paired exercise with full-group debrief

#### Purpose
Participants explore how nonverbal communication -- body language, facial expressions, tone of voice, physical distance, and eye contact -- shapes the meaning of any interaction. This activity builds awareness that *how* we communicate is often more powerful than *what* we say.

#### Setup
- Arrange participants in pairs. If there is an odd number, create one triad.
- Distribute **Handout 7A: Nonverbal Communication Observation Sheet** to each participant.
- Post chart paper labeled: "What We See," "What We Hear," "What We Feel."

#### Instructions

**Round 1: Mismatch (5 minutes)**

Say to participants:
> "In this first round, one person in each pair will be the Speaker and the other will be the Observer. Speakers, I'm going to ask you to tell your partner about something you genuinely enjoy -- a hobby, a favorite food, a place you like to go. But here's the twist: while you're talking about something positive, I want you to use body language that says the opposite. Cross your arms, look away, frown, speak in a flat tone. Observers, use your Observation Sheet to note what you see and what it feels like to receive that message."

Allow 2 minutes for the exercise, then have partners switch roles and repeat.

**Round 2: Alignment (5 minutes)**

> "Now repeat the same exercise, but this time, let your body language match your words. Lean in, make comfortable eye contact, smile if it's natural, use an open posture. Observers, note the difference on your sheet."

Allow 2 minutes, then switch roles and repeat.

**Debrief (10 minutes)**

Bring the full group together. Facilitate discussion using these questions:
- "Observers, what did you notice in Round 1 versus Round 2?"
- "Speakers, how did it feel to deliberately mismatch your words and body language?"
- "When have you experienced someone saying one thing but their body language saying something else? What impact did that have?"

Record key observations on the chart paper under the three headers. Highlight that research suggests nonverbal cues carry significant weight in how messages are received -- often more than the words themselves.

#### Key Takeaways to Emphasize
- Nonverbal communication includes: facial expressions, eye contact, posture, gestures, tone of voice, physical distance, and touch.
- When verbal and nonverbal messages conflict, people tend to trust the nonverbal message.
- As peer supporters, aligning our body language with our words builds trust and safety.

### Activity 2: Cooling the Temperature
**Duration:** 20 minutes | **Format:** Facilitator-led introduction with small-group application

#### Purpose
Participants learn foundational de-escalation techniques -- practical strategies for responding when a conversation becomes heated, tense, or emotionally charged. The emphasis is on creating safety, not on controlling the other person.

#### Setup
- Distribute **Handout 7B: De-Escalation Techniques Quick Reference** to each participant.
- Remain in the full-group circle for the introduction; transition to groups of 3-4 for the application portion.

#### Part 1: Introduction (8 minutes)

Introduce the concept:
> "In Module 6, we practiced communicating assertively and using 'I' statements. Those skills are powerful. But what happens when a conversation starts to escalate -- when emotions are running high and someone is upset, frustrated, or angry? Today we're going to learn some techniques for 'cooling the temperature' so we can stay connected even when things get hard."

Walk through the five de-escalation techniques on Handout 7B, demonstrating each briefly:

1. **Regulate Your Own Response First.** Before trying to calm someone else, notice your own body. Take a slow breath. Unclench your jaw. Drop your shoulders. You cannot de-escalate someone else if you are escalated yourself.

2. **Lower and Slow Your Voice.** When tension rises, voices tend to get louder and faster. Deliberately speak in a calm, steady, slightly slower pace. This signals safety.

3. **Validate the Emotion, Not Necessarily the Behavior.** Name what you observe without judgment. "I can see you're really frustrated right now" acknowledges their experience without agreeing or disagreeing with their actions.

4. **Use Open Body Language.** Uncross your arms. Keep your hands visible. Turn your body toward the person. Maintain a comfortable distance -- not too close, not too far. Avoid standing over someone who is sitting.

5. **Offer Choice and Control.** When people feel out of control, offering small choices helps restore agency. "Would you like to take a walk, or would you rather sit here for a minute?" "Do you want to talk about it now, or come back to it later?"

#### Part 2: Spot the Technique (12 minutes)

Divide participants into groups of 3-4. Give each group two scenario cards from the de-escalation set in **Handout 7C**. Instructions:

> "Read each scenario together. Then discuss: Which de-escalation techniques would be most helpful here? Why? Be ready to share one scenario with the full group and explain your thinking."

Allow 7 minutes for small-group discussion, then have 2-3 groups share their scenarios and reasoning (5 minutes).

#### Key Takeaways to Emphasize
- De-escalation is about creating conditions for connection, not about winning an argument or controlling someone.
- Your own calm is the most powerful de-escalation tool you have.
- These techniques work in everyday life -- not just in crisis moments.

---

### Handout 7B: De-Escalation Techniques Quick Reference

**Five Ways to Cool the Temperature**

| # | Technique | What It Sounds / Looks Like | Why It Works |
|---|-----------|----------------------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Regulate Your Own Response First** | Pause. Breathe slowly. Relax your body. Notice your own emotions without acting on them. | You cannot help someone else calm down if you are escalated. Your calm is contagious. |
| 2 | **Lower and Slow Your Voice** | Speak at a steady, unhurried pace. Keep your volume at or below a normal conversational level. | A slower, quieter voice signals safety and reduces the sense of threat. |
| 3 | **Validate the Emotion** | "I can see this is really upsetting." / "It makes sense that you'd feel that way." | People need to feel heard before they can hear you. Validation is not agreement -- it is acknowledgment. |
| 4 | **Use Open Body Language** | Uncrossed arms. Hands visible. Comfortable distance. Face the person. Get on their physical level. | Open posture communicates respect and reduces defensiveness. |
| 5 | **Offer Choice and Control** | "Would you like to step outside for a minute?" / "Do you want to talk now or take a break first?" | When people feel powerless, small choices restore agency and reduce the need to fight for control. |

**Remember:** De-escalation is not about making someone's feelings go away. It is about creating enough safety that a real conversation can happen.

### Session Design Rationale

The source content for this module runs approximately 60 minutes. The expansion to 90 minutes is achieved by:

- Adding a 10-minute opening warm-up with a concrete problem-solving scenario (Activity 1)
- Extending the SMART goal-setting worksheet time from 10 to 20 minutes, allowing participants to draft, revise, and refine rather than rush
- Adding 10 minutes of structured peer coaching practice (Activity 4), which deepens both the coaching and goal-setting skill sets
- Extending the closing reflection by 5 minutes to include written STAR journaling alongside verbal sharing
- Building in 5 additional minutes of transition and debrief time across segments

### Practical Skills Emphasis

This is a hands-on, practical skills module. Participants should leave this session with a tangible product: a completed personal SMART goal with action steps. Resist the temptation to turn this into an abstract lecture about planning. Keep the energy active and applied. If participants finish early, have them set a second goal rather than waiting.

### Common Facilitation Challenges

- **Vague goals:** Participants often write goals like "do better in school" or "be healthier." Use probing questions to help them get specific without doing the thinking for them. The peer coaching activity is designed to address this.
- **Perfectionism and avoidance:** Some participants may resist writing a goal down because they are afraid of failing. Normalize revision. Emphasize that a goal is a draft, not a contract.
- **Problem-solving feels abstract:** Ground every step in a concrete, relatable example. The "Lost Phone" scenario in Activity 1 is deliberately low-stakes and universal.
- **Peer coaching goes off-track:** The Question Card (Handout 8C) provides structure. Circulate actively during the peer coaching activity and redirect pairs who are chatting rather than coaching.

### Room Setup

Arrange seating in pairs or clusters of four. Participants will need a writing surface. If using a community space with limited table access, provide clipboards or hard-backed folders.

### Welcome and Warm-Up (10 minutes)

**Facilitator says:**

> "Welcome back, everyone. Today we are going to work on two skills that connect everything we have been building so far: solving problems and setting goals. These are skills you already use every day, even if you have never thought of them that way. We are going to make them sharper and more intentional."

**Opening Check-In (3 minutes):**

Go around the room (or use a popcorn format for larger groups). Each participant answers one of the following:

- "What is one small problem you solved this week -- even something tiny?"
- "What is one thing you are working toward right now, big or small?"

**Quick Scenario (5 minutes):**

Present this scenario verbally or write it on the board:

> *"You realize your phone is not in your pocket. You had it 30 minutes ago. You are at a community event with a lot of people around. What do you do?"*

Ask participants to call out their responses. As they share, notice the natural steps: they retrace their steps, they check likely places, they ask people, they consider possibilities, they take action.

**Facilitator says:**

> "Look at what you just did -- without any instruction, you followed a process. You figured out the problem, considered options, and decided on an action. That is problem-solving. Today we are going to take that instinct and turn it into a tool you can use on the bigger, harder stuff."

**Transition:** Distribute **Handout 8A: The Problem-Solving Pathway**.

### Activity 1: The Problem-Solving Pathway (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Teach a repeatable, structured approach to problem-solving that participants can apply independently.

#### The Five Steps

Write these on the board or flip chart. Walk through each one using the "Lost Phone" scenario as a running example:

| Step | Name | What You Do |
|------|------|-------------|
| 1 | **Name the Problem** | Say exactly what is wrong -- be specific, not vague |
| 2 | **Explore the Causes** | Ask "why" and "how did this happen" -- look beneath the surface |
| 3 | **Brainstorm Options** | List every possible action, even ones that seem unlikely |
| 4 | **Choose and Act** | Pick the best option based on what you know and what you can control |
| 5 | **Review and Adjust** | After acting, check: did it work? What would you do differently? |

**Worked Example (7 minutes):**

Walk through the Lost Phone scenario step by step on the board:

1. **Name the Problem:** "My phone is missing. I last had it 30 minutes ago at the snack table."
2. **Explore the Causes:** "Maybe I set it down and forgot. Maybe it fell out of my pocket. Maybe someone picked it up by mistake."
3. **Brainstorm Options:** "Retrace my steps. Ask the event organizer. Call my phone from someone else's phone. Check lost and found. Post on the event group chat."
4. **Choose and Act:** "First, I will call my phone. If no answer, I will retrace my steps to the snack table. Then I will ask the organizer."
5. **Review and Adjust:** "I found it under a napkin at the snack table. Next time, I will keep my phone in a zipped pocket at events."

**Individual Practice (10 minutes):**

**Facilitator says:**

> "Now it is your turn. Think of a real problem you are dealing with right now -- or one you dealt with recently. It does not have to be dramatic. It could be a conflict with a friend, trouble getting to work on time, or figuring out how to pay for something. Use the five steps on your handout to work through it."

Give participants 10 minutes of quiet writing time. Circulate and offer support. Use these coaching prompts if participants are stuck:

- "Can you make the problem more specific? Instead of 'school is hard,' what exactly is hard?"
- "What are three things you could do about this -- even if some of them are a stretch?"
- "Which option is most within your control right now?"

**Brief share (3 minutes):** Ask two or three volunteers to share their problem and one option they identified. Affirm effort and specificity.

### Activity 2: What Makes a Goal SMART? (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Introduce the SMART goal framework and help participants distinguish between vague wishes and actionable goals.

**Facilitator says:**

> "Now that we have a tool for solving problems, let us talk about where you want to go. A lot of people say things like 'I want to do better' or 'I want to be successful.' Those are good intentions, but they are not goals -- not yet. A goal needs to be specific enough that you know when you have reached it."

**Introduce SMART on the board:**

| Letter | Stands For | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **S** | Specific | What exactly will you do? |
| **M** | Measurable | How will you know you did it? |
| **A** | Achievable | Is this realistic given your current situation? |
| **R** | Relevant | Does this matter to you? Is it connected to something you care about? |
| **T** | Time-bound | By when? What is your deadline? |

**Compare and Contrast (5 minutes):**

Show these pairs on the board. Ask participants which is the SMART goal and why:

| Vague Intention | SMART Goal |
|-----------------|------------|
| "I want to get healthier." | "I will walk for 20 minutes three times a week for the next month." |
| "I want to save money." | "I will save $25 per week for 8 weeks by packing lunch instead of buying it." |
| "I want to read more." | "I will read one book per month for the next three months, starting with a book I choose this week." |

For each pair, ask:
- "What makes the second one better?"
- "How would you know if you succeeded?"
- "What is the deadline?"

**Transition:** Distribute **Handout 8B: My SMART Goal Worksheet**.

**Facilitator says:**

> "Now you are going to write your own. This is not a test -- it is a draft. You can change it. The point is to get something down on paper that is specific enough to act on."

### Activity 4: Peer Coaching Practice (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Participants practice helping someone else strengthen a goal, building mutual accountability and deepening their own goal-setting skills.

**Setup (2 minutes):**

**Facilitator says:**

> "One of the most powerful things you can do is help someone else get clear on what they want. It is not about telling them what to do -- it is about asking the right questions. You are about to be a coach for your partner."

Pair participants up. Distribute **Handout 8C: Peer Coaching Question Card**. Assign roles: Person A is the "Goal Setter" first, Person B is the "Coach" first. They will switch halfway.

**Round 1 (5 minutes):**

- Person A shares their SMART goal from Handout 8B.
- Person B listens and uses the questions on the card to help Person A strengthen the goal.
- Person B does NOT give advice or tell Person A what to do. They only ask questions.

**Switch (1 minute):** Reverse roles.

**Round 2 (5 minutes):**

- Person B shares their SMART goal.
- Person A coaches using the question card.

**Debrief (2 minutes):**

Ask the full group:
- "What was it like to be the coach?"
- "Did your partner's questions help you see something you missed?"
- "What made the coaching helpful -- or not?"

**Written Reflection (5 minutes):**

Participants write in their journals, on the back of a handout, or on a separate sheet:

---

**STAR Reflection -- Module 8**

**Situation:** Describe where you were and what you were doing during today's session. What were you working on?

> *Example: "I was sitting with my partner working on my SMART goal during the goal-setting activity."*

**Task:** What were you trying to accomplish? What was the challenge or assignment in front of you?

> *Example: "I needed to turn my general idea about improving my grades into a goal I could actually measure and act on."*

# Module 9: Setting Boundaries

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL USE ONLY -- NOT FOR PARTICIPANT DISTRIBUTION**

### Curriculum Context

This module is a cornerstone of peer supporter safety and sustainability within the SCBF 2.0 framework. Research consistently shows that peer supporters who lack boundary-setting skills are at significantly higher risk of compassion fatigue, burnout, and secondary traumatic stress. This session must be treated with the same seriousness as skill-building modules; it is not optional enrichment.

### Alignment to Standards

This module addresses the following NAPS (National AfterSchool Partnership Standards) domains:

- **NAPS Domain 3: Health and Safety** -- Ensuring the emotional and psychological safety of youth in support roles.
- **NAPS Domain 4: Youth Development** -- Building self-awareness and interpersonal competence as developmental assets.
- **NAPS Domain 6: Family, School, and Community Partnerships** -- Establishing appropriate relational boundaries across contexts.

The Harrington model of peer support competency identifies boundary-setting as a Tier 2 skill (applied competency), requiring both conceptual understanding and repeated guided practice before field deployment.

### Facilitator Mindset

- **Normalize boundary-setting.** Many young people have been socialized to view boundary-setting as selfish or unkind. Actively counter this narrative throughout the session.
- **Watch for disclosures.** Boundary discussions can surface personal experiences of boundary violations, including abuse. Have your referral protocol ready and a co-facilitator or support person available.
- **Model your own boundaries.** Share (at an appropriate level) a time you set a boundary in a professional or volunteer role. Participants learn from what you do, not just what you say.
- **Pacing note.** The source content for this module covers approximately 45 minutes. The 90-minute session expands practice time significantly. Use the extra time for deeper scenario work and role-play rehearsal -- do not rush through activities to fill time with lecture.

### Room Setup

Arrange seating in a circle or U-shape for the opening and closing segments. Ensure space for small groups of 3-4 to work together during scenario activities. Post chart paper on walls for the Boundary Types brainstorm.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Boundary Types Brainstorm (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build shared vocabulary around different types of boundaries.

**Setup:** Post five sheets of chart paper around the room, each labeled with one boundary type: **Physical**, **Emotional**, **Time/Energy**, **Role**, **Digital**.

**Instructions for Participants:**

1. You will rotate through five stations around the room. At each station, you will see a type of boundary.
2. With your marker, write one or two examples of what that type of boundary looks like in real life -- especially in situations where you are helping or supporting someone.
3. Spend about 2 minutes at each station, then rotate when signaled.
4. After the rotation, we will do a gallery walk and discuss what we notice.

**Facilitation Notes:**

- After the gallery walk, highlight patterns. Ask: "Which type of boundary had the most examples? Which had the fewest? Why do you think that is?"
- Introduce any boundary types that participants did not generate organically. Pay special attention to **Role Boundaries** (the limits of what a peer supporter should and should not do) and **Digital Boundaries** (social media, texting availability, sharing personal accounts).

**Definitions to Share (post or distribute):**

| Boundary Type | What It Means |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Physical** | Limits around personal space, touch, and physical proximity |
| **Emotional** | Limits around how much of others' emotional weight you take on; protecting your own feelings |
| **Time/Energy** | Limits on when, how long, and how often you are available to support someone |
| **Role** | Limits on what you are qualified and expected to do as a peer supporter versus what requires a trained professional |
| **Digital** | Limits on online availability, sharing personal accounts, and communication platforms |

### Activity 2: "Where's the Line?" Scenario Discussion (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Apply boundary concepts to realistic peer support situations and practice recognizing when boundaries are at risk.

**Setup:** Divide participants into small groups of 3-4. Distribute one set of scenario cards (Handout 9B) to each group.

**Instructions for Participants:**

> **Handout 9B: "Where's the Line?" Scenario Cards**

Read each scenario aloud in your small group. For each one, discuss:

1. **What type(s) of boundary is involved?** (Physical, Emotional, Time/Energy, Role, Digital)
2. **Is the boundary being respected, tested, or crossed?**
3. **What would you do in this situation?**

---

**Scenario 1: The Late-Night Texter**
A classmate you have been supporting starts texting you every night after 11 PM. The messages are not emergencies -- they want to vent about their day. You have school in the morning and you are losing sleep.

---

**Scenario 2: The Secret Keeper**
Someone you are supporting tells you they have been hurting themselves. They beg you not to tell anyone. "You are the only person I trust," they say.

---

**Scenario 3: The Social Media Request**
A younger participant you mentor asks to follow your personal Instagram account. Your account has photos of your family and personal life.

---

**Scenario 4: The Emotional Sponge**
After every support session, you feel drained and sad. You have started dreaming about the problems the people you support are dealing with. A friend asks if you are okay, and you snap at them.

---

**Scenario 5: The Gift Giver**
A family you have been supporting brings you an expensive gift card as a thank-you. They seem genuinely grateful and you do not want to hurt their feelings.

---

**Scenario 6: The Expanded Role**
Your program coordinator asks you to drive a participant home after a session because their ride fell through. You have your license and it is on your way home.

---

**Debrief Questions (Whole Group):**

After small-group discussion, bring the full group back together and ask:

- Which scenario was hardest to decide on? Why?
- Did anyone disagree within their group? What was the disagreement about?
- In which scenarios would you need to involve an adult, supervisor, or professional? How would you do that?

**Facilitation Notes:**

- Scenario 2 is intentionally high-stakes. Use it to reinforce that **role boundaries** mean knowing when something is beyond your scope and requires adult/professional intervention. Affirm that telling a trusted adult is not a betrayal -- it is the most supportive action.
- Scenario 6 addresses liability and organizational policy. Reinforce that even well-intentioned actions can create risk. Peer supporters should always check with their supervisor before taking on tasks outside their defined role.

### Activity 3: Practice Saying No / Setting Limits (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build muscle memory for assertive boundary-setting language through guided role-play.

**Setup:** Pair participants. Distribute Handout 9C: Assertive Language Stems. One partner will play the person requesting something; the other will practice setting a boundary. They will switch roles after each round.

**Instructions for Participants:**

> **Handout 9C: Assertive Language Stems**

Setting a boundary is a skill. Like any skill, it gets easier with practice. Use the sentence stems below as starting points. You do not have to use these exact words -- adapt them to sound like you.

**Saying No / Declining:**
- "I care about you, and I am not able to do that."
- "That is outside what I can help with. Let me connect you with someone who can."
- "I want to support you, and I also need to take care of myself. I cannot do this right now."
- "I am not the right person for this. Let us find someone who is."

**Setting a Limit:**
- "I am available to talk between [time] and [time]. Outside of those hours, I will not be able to respond until the next day."
- "I can help with [specific thing], but [other thing] is something you will need to talk to [appropriate person] about."
- "I need to take a step back for my own well-being. That does not mean I do not care."

**Redirecting:**
- "It sounds like this is really important. I think [name/resource] would be the best person to help with this."
- "I hear you. This is bigger than what I can handle on my own. Can we talk to [supervisor/adult] together?"
- "I appreciate you trusting me. The best thing I can do for you right now is help you get connected to the right support."

**Practice Rounds:**

Complete three rounds. In each round, one partner reads the prompt and the other practices responding using the language stems. Switch roles after each round.

**Round 1:** "Can you stay late after the session today? I really need to talk to someone."
*(Practice: Setting a time/energy boundary)*

**Round 2:** "Please do not tell anyone what I told you. Promise me."
*(Practice: Setting a role boundary -- when you have a duty to involve a professional)*

**Round 3:** "You are the only one who gets me. Can I have your phone number so I can call you whenever?"
*(Practice: Setting a digital/availability boundary)*

**Debrief:**

- How did it feel to say no or set a limit?
- What made it hard? What made it easier?
- Did using a sentence stem help? Would you change the wording to fit your style?

**Facilitation Notes:**

- Circulate during practice and offer encouragement. Many participants will laugh nervously or break character -- this is normal. Gently redirect them to try again.
- After the debrief, affirm: "Setting boundaries is not mean. It is one of the most professional and caring things you can do as a supporter. It keeps you healthy so you can keep showing up."

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use the following prompts to guide the closing reflection. Participants may journal individually, share with a partner, or contribute to a whole-group discussion. Select one or two prompts based on remaining time.

### Prompt 1: Recognizing a Boundary Moment

- **Situation:** Think about a time when someone asked you for help, and you felt uncomfortable, overwhelmed, or unsure about whether to say yes.
- **Task:** What was being asked of you? What boundary was being tested?
- **Action:** What did you do? Did you set a boundary, go along with the request, or do something else?
- **Result:** What happened as a result? If you could go back, would you handle it differently? How?

### Prompt 2: The Boundary You Need Most

- **Situation:** Consider your current life -- school, home, friendships, any volunteer or support roles you play.
- **Task:** Identify one area where you need a stronger boundary.
- **Action:** What specific boundary would you set? What would you say or do to put it in place?
- **Result:** How do you think setting that boundary would affect your well-being and your relationships?

### Prompt 3: Supporting Someone Else's Boundary

- **Situation:** Think about a time when someone else set a boundary with you -- a friend, family member, teacher, or mentor.
- **Task:** What was the boundary they set? How was it communicated to you?
- **Action:** How did you respond? Did you respect the boundary, push back, or feel hurt?
- **Result:** What did that experience teach you about how boundaries work in relationships? How does it shape how you will communicate your own boundaries?

# Module 10: Self-Care

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- NOT FOR PARTICIPANT DISTRIBUTION**

### Framework Alignment

This module aligns with the **SCBF 2.0** framework's emphasis on sustaining the well-being of peer supporters as a precondition for effective community engagement. The session draws on the **Harrington Model** of holistic youth development, particularly the principle that helpers must maintain their own capacity in order to serve others authentically. **NAPS Standard 4 (Health and Safety)** and **NAPS Standard 6 (Staffing and Professional Development)** are directly addressed through the self-care planning components.

### Pedagogical Notes

- This module is designed as a companion to **Module 9: Boundaries**. If delivered in sequence, open by briefly referencing boundary-setting as one form of self-care before expanding the concept.
- Self-care content can surface difficult emotions. Be prepared for disclosures related to stress, anxiety, family obligations, or trauma. Have referral resources available and know your local crisis protocols.
- Normalize vulnerability. Model openness by sharing (at an appropriate level) your own self-care challenges as a facilitator.
- The session is intentionally expanded from a 45-minute core to 90 minutes. The additional time allows for deeper individual reflection, peer sharing, and thorough plan development. Do not rush the planning activity; it is the anchor of the session.
- Emphasize throughout: self-care is not selfish. For peer supporters, it is a professional and ethical responsibility. A depleted helper cannot hold space for others.

### Key Terminology

| Term | Working Definition |
|------|--------------------|
| Self-care | Intentional actions taken to maintain or improve one's physical, emotional, and mental health. |
| Burnout | A state of chronic stress leading to physical and emotional exhaustion, cynicism, and feelings of ineffectiveness. |
| Compassion fatigue | The gradual erosion of empathy and hope that can affect those in helping roles. |
| Proactive self-care | Planned, routine practices that build resilience before stress becomes overwhelming. |
| Reactive coping | Strategies used in response to a crisis or after stress has already accumulated. |

## Activities

### Opening & Check-In (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Establish a safe, reflective tone for the session.

1. Welcome participants. Briefly review group agreements (confidentiality, respect, right to pass).
2. **One-Word Check-In:** Go around the circle. Ask each participant to share one word that describes how they are feeling right now. No elaboration needed -- just the word.
3. Transition: *"Today we are going to talk about something that is easy to overlook but absolutely essential -- especially for people who show up for others the way you do. We are talking about self-care. And I want to be clear from the start: self-care is not a luxury. It is a responsibility. You cannot pour from an empty cup."*
4. If Module 9 (Boundaries) was delivered previously, bridge: *"Last session we talked about setting boundaries to protect our energy. Today we go deeper: how do we actively refill that energy? How do we take care of ourselves on purpose, not just when we are already running on empty?"*

### Exploring Self-Care Dimensions (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build a shared understanding of self-care as multi-dimensional.

1. Ask the group: *"When you hear 'self-care,' what comes to mind?"* Capture responses on the whiteboard. Expect answers like bubble baths, sleep, exercise, etc.
2. Affirm their answers, then expand: *"All of those count. But self-care is bigger than any single activity. It has multiple dimensions -- areas of your life that each need attention."*
3. Introduce the **six dimensions of self-care** by drawing or revealing them on the whiteboard:

| Dimension | Examples |
|-----------|----------|
| **Physical** | Sleep, nutrition, exercise, medical check-ups |
| **Emotional** | Journaling, therapy, crying, creative expression |
| **Social** | Quality time with friends, setting social boundaries, community involvement |
| **Spiritual** | Meditation, prayer, time in nature, reflection on purpose and values |
| **Professional / Academic** | Time management, saying no to overcommitment, mentorship, skill-building |
| **Psychological** | Learning new things, mindfulness, reducing screen time, self-reflection |

4. Briefly discuss: *"Which of these dimensions do people tend to forget about?"* (Common answers: spiritual, psychological, professional.)
5. Transition to Activity 1: *"Let's take an honest look at how you are doing across all six of these areas right now."*

### Activity 2: Burnout Warning Signs Checklist (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Help participants identify early warning signs of burnout so they can intervene before reaching a crisis point.

**Instructions for Facilitator:**
1. Transition: *"Now that we have a picture of where our self-care stands, let's talk about what happens when self-care breaks down over time. The word for that is burnout."*
2. Ask: *"Has anyone ever felt completely drained -- not just tired from one day, but exhausted in a way that sleep doesn't fix?"* Validate responses.
3. Explain: *"Burnout doesn't happen overnight. It builds up slowly, and it sends warning signs along the way. The tricky part is that we often don't notice them until we're already deep in it. This checklist is designed to help you catch those signs early."*
4. Distribute **Handout 10-B: Burnout Warning Signs Checklist**.
5. Allow 5-6 minutes for individual completion.
6. Form groups of 3-4. Give groups 5 minutes to discuss using the group discussion prompts on the handout.
7. Bring the full group back together. Ask each small group to share one key takeaway. Reinforce: *"Recognizing burnout early is itself an act of self-care."*

#### Handout 10-B: Burnout Warning Signs Checklist

**Instructions:** Read each statement below. Check the box if you have experienced this in the **past two weeks**. Be honest -- this is for your eyes only.

**Physical Warning Signs**
- [ ] I feel tired even after a full night of sleep.
- [ ] I have frequent headaches, stomachaches, or muscle tension.
- [ ] My eating habits have changed significantly (eating much more or much less).
- [ ] I am getting sick more often than usual.
- [ ] I have trouble falling asleep or staying asleep.

**Emotional Warning Signs**
- [ ] I feel overwhelmed by tasks that used to feel manageable.
- [ ] I am more irritable, impatient, or short-tempered than usual.
- [ ] I feel numb, detached, or "going through the motions."
- [ ] I feel like nothing I do makes a difference.
- [ ] I dread activities or responsibilities I used to enjoy.

**Social Warning Signs**
- [ ] I am withdrawing from friends, family, or my community.
- [ ] I avoid answering messages or phone calls.
- [ ] I feel resentful when people ask me for help.
- [ ] I compare myself negatively to others more often.
- [ ] I feel isolated even when I am around people.

**Behavioral Warning Signs**
- [ ] I am procrastinating more than usual.
- [ ] I have stopped doing hobbies or activities I used to enjoy.
- [ ] I am relying more on unhealthy coping (junk food, excessive screen time, substances).
- [ ] I find it hard to concentrate or make decisions.
- [ ] I have been skipping commitments or responsibilities.

**Scoring Guide:**
- **0-4 checks:** You are managing well. Keep building your self-care practices.
- **5-9 checks:** Caution zone. Some warning signs are present. Review your self-care wheel for gaps.
- **10-14 checks:** Alert zone. Burnout is building. Take action this week -- identify one change you can make immediately.
- **15+ checks:** Urgent. Please talk to a trusted adult, mentor, counselor, or support person. You deserve help.

**My total checks:** ______

**Group Discussion Prompts:**

2. What makes it hard to notice burnout when it is happening to you?
3. What is one thing a friend could say or do that would help you recognize you are burning out?

### Movement Break (5 minutes)

**Purpose:** Model self-care in real time by pausing for physical and mental reset.

Lead participants through one of the following (choose based on group energy):

**Option A -- Box Breathing:**
- Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts, exhale for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts.
- Repeat 4 cycles.

**Option B -- Guided Stretch:**
- Stand up. Reach arms overhead. Stretch to the left, hold 10 seconds. Stretch to the right, hold 10 seconds.
- Roll shoulders forward 5 times, backward 5 times.
- Shake out hands and arms vigorously for 10 seconds.

**Option C -- Grounding Exercise (5-4-3-2-1):**
- Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste.

After the break: *"That right there -- that was self-care. It took three minutes. Self-care doesn't have to be expensive or time-consuming. It can be small, intentional pauses like that one."*

### Activity 3: Personal Self-Care Plan (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Translate awareness into action by building a concrete, personalized self-care plan.

**Instructions for Facilitator:**

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- FOR FACILITATOR USE ONLY**

### Content Warning and Emotional Safety

This is the heaviest content module in the curriculum. The material addresses crisis recognition, suicidal ideation, self-harm, abuse, and mandated reporting. Facilitators must prepare with the following protocols:

**Before the Session:**

- Review Harrington's framework on trauma-informed peer education delivery, particularly the scaffolded disclosure model and the "contain, connect, convey" facilitation sequence (Harrington, 2021, Chapter 7).
- Confirm that at least one licensed counselor, social worker, or mental health professional is on-site or on-call during this session. Do not facilitate this module without backup clinical support.
- Pre-populate Handout 11-E with local crisis resources: crisis hotline numbers, local mental health agencies, school counselor contacts (if applicable), and community partner referral pathways.
- Review your organization's mandated reporting policy and know your state/local reporting obligations.
- Prepare a "quiet space" near the session room where a participant can step out with a support person if content becomes triggering.

**Emotional Safety Protocols:**

1. **Opening Frame:** Begin the session by normalizing that this content is heavy. Explicitly state that participants may step out at any time without explanation and that a support person is available.
2. **Grounding Anchors:** Place fidget tools, stress balls, and tissues on every table. Briefly teach a 5-4-3-2-1 grounding exercise at the start (see Activity 1).
3. **Disclosure Protocol:** If a participant discloses active crisis, abuse, or harm during the session:
   - Remain calm. Thank them for trusting the group.
   - Do not promise confidentiality for safety concerns.
   - Gently transition the group activity and privately connect the participant with the on-site support person.
   - Document the disclosure per your organization's incident reporting policy.
   - Follow mandated reporting requirements as applicable.
4. **Facilitator Self-Care:** Debrief with a co-facilitator or supervisor after the session. This content affects facilitators too.

**Key Facilitation Reminders (per Harrington):**

- Harrington emphasizes that peer supporters must understand their role boundaries before engaging with crisis content. The "helper's boundary" concept (Harrington, 2021, pp. 134-138) should undergird every activity. Peer supporters are bridges to professional help, never replacements for it.
- Use Harrington's "three-gate check" before each activity: (1) Is the environment safe? (2) Are participants emotionally regulated enough to proceed? (3) Is professional backup accessible? If any gate fails, pause and address before continuing.
- Align to NAPS Standard 4 (Safe and Supportive Environments) and Standard 6 (Community and Family Engagement) throughout the session, particularly during the referral pathway and community partner segments.

### Activity 1: Recognizing the Signs (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build participants' ability to notice when a peer may be in crisis.

### Activity 2: My Role, Their Role (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Establish clear boundaries between what a peer supporter does and what a professional crisis counselor does.

**Setup:** Distribute **Handout 11-B**. Prepare a T-chart on the whiteboard labeled "Peer Supporter" and "Professional Counselor."

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min)** Facilitator sets the frame:
   > "This is one of the most important things we will talk about today. You are learning to be a peer supporter -- someone who listens, cares, and connects people to help. You are NOT being trained to be a crisis counselor, therapist, or mental health professional. Knowing the difference is not a limitation. It is what keeps both you and the people you support safe."

2. **(5 min)** In pairs, participants review Handout 11-B and discuss: *"Which of these responsibilities surprised you? Which ones feel hard to accept?"*

3. **(5 min)** Full group share-out. Facilitator records key points on the T-chart and reinforces boundaries. Address common pushback:
   - *"But what if there is no counselor available?"* -- You still refer. You contact a hotline together. You tell a trusted adult. You do not become the counselor.
   - *"What if they made me promise not to tell?"* -- You cannot keep a secret that puts someone's life at risk. You can say: "I care about you too much to keep this to myself. Let us get help together."
   - *"What if I have been through the same thing?"* -- Your experience gives you empathy, but it does not give you clinical training. Share that you understand, and still help them connect to a professional.

4. **(3 min)** Facilitator summarizes with the key message:
   > "Your job is to be the bridge, not the destination. You walk with someone to the door of professional help. You do not have to have all the answers behind that door."

**How to Start the Conversation**

Starting a conversation about crisis can feel intimidating. Here are some phrases you can adapt to your own voice:

### Activity 4: Mandated Reporting -- What You Need to Know (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Provide a basic, age-appropriate understanding of mandated reporting so participants understand why adults in their lives may be required to act on certain disclosures.

**Setup:** No additional handout needed. This is a facilitator-led informational segment. Use the whiteboard for key points.

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min)** Facilitator introduces the concept:
   > "You may have heard the term 'mandated reporter' before. Today we are going to talk about what that means, because understanding it helps you understand what happens when you or someone you know shares information about certain kinds of harm."

2. **(5 min)** Facilitator covers the following key points (write main ideas on the board):

   **What is mandated reporting?**
   - Certain adults -- like teachers, counselors, coaches, social workers, and medical professionals -- are required by law to report when they have a reasonable suspicion that a young person is being abused, neglected, or is at risk of serious harm.
   - They do not need proof. They report the concern, and trained investigators follow up.

   **Why does it exist?**
   - It exists to protect young people. It is not about getting anyone in trouble. It is about making sure people who need help get it.

   **What gets reported?**
   - Physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, and situations where someone is at imminent risk of harming themselves or others.

   **What does NOT happen:**
   - The reporter does not investigate. They pass the concern to the appropriate agency.
   - A report does not automatically mean someone is removed from their home. Investigators assess the situation and determine the best course of action, which often includes supportive services.

   **What this means for you as a peer supporter:**
   - If someone tells you something that involves abuse, neglect, or danger, and you share that with a trusted adult, that adult may be required to report it. This is not a betrayal. It is part of a system designed to help.
   - You should never promise total confidentiality when safety is at stake.

3. **(3 min)** Open the floor for questions. Address common concerns:
   - *"Will the person know I told?"* -- You can ask the trusted adult about how the process works. Your role is to connect, not to manage the outcome.
   - *"What if reporting makes things worse?"* -- This is a real concern, and it is taken seriously by professionals who handle reports. Not reporting when someone is in danger can also make things worse.

#### Handout 11-D: Safety Planning Overview

**PARTICIPANT HANDOUT**

**What is a Safety Plan?**

A safety plan is a written document created by a person in partnership with a mental health professional. It is a personalized guide for what to do during moments of crisis. It is NOT a contract or a promise. It is a practical, step-by-step tool.

**Components of a Safety Plan:**

| Step | What It Includes |
|------|-----------------|
| **1. Warning Signs** | Personal signs that a crisis may be developing (thoughts, feelings, behaviors, situations) |
| **2. Internal Coping Strategies** | Things the person can do on their own to manage the moment (deep breathing, journaling, physical activity, grounding exercises) |
| **3. Social Distractions** | People and places that can provide healthy distraction and a sense of normalcy |
| **4. People to Contact for Help** | Trusted friends, family members, or mentors the person can reach out to when coping alone is not enough |
| **5. Professional and Crisis Resources** | Therapists, counselors, crisis hotlines, and emergency services |
| **6. Making the Environment Safe** | Steps to reduce access to means of harm during a crisis |

**Your Role as a Peer Supporter:**

- You do NOT create safety plans. That is a professional's role.
- You CAN encourage a peer to work with a professional on a safety plan.
- If a peer shares their safety plan with you, you can support them by gently reminding them of the steps they identified.
- You can be one of the "People to Contact for Help" on someone's safety plan. That means your job is to listen, be present, and help them move to the next step on their plan.

> **Prompt Option 3:**

>

## Community Partner Integration

### Why This Module Requires Community Partners

Module 11 is the most critical point for community partner involvement in the curriculum. Crisis awareness cannot exist in a vacuum -- participants need to know that real, local support systems exist and are accessible to them. Abstract knowledge of crisis resources is insufficient; participants need tangible connections.

### Recommended Community Partner Roles for This Module

| Partner Type | Role in This Module |

| **Local Mental Health Agency** | Guest speaker or on-site support during session; provide referral pathway information for Handout 11-E; serve as follow-up resource for participants who need support after the session |

| **Community Health Worker** | Bridge between program and family/community resources; support culturally responsive referral pathways |

### Integration Points During the Session

1. **Activity 1 (Crisis Warning Signs):** A mental health professional can co-facilitate, adding clinical context to participant-generated lists while keeping language accessible.
2. **Activity 3 (Making the Connection):** A crisis hotline representative can walk participants through what actually happens when you call a crisis line, reducing fear and stigma.
3. **Activity 4 (Mandated Reporting):** A school counselor or social worker can share (in age-appropriate terms) what the reporting process looks like from the professional's perspective.
4. **Activity 5 (Safety Planning):** A licensed counselor can briefly explain how safety plans are developed and answer questions.
5. **Closing and Resource Distribution:** Community partners distribute their own resource cards and make themselves available for one-on-one conversations after the session.

### Post-Session Follow-Up

- Schedule a follow-up check-in with community partners within one week of this session to review any concerns, disclosures, or referrals that emerged.
- Ensure that all participants have at least one direct point of contact (name and number) for a local crisis resource -- not just a generic hotline number.
- Community partners should be informed in advance about the session content so they can prepare appropriately for potential disclosures or emotional responses.

## Activities

### Opening and Warm-Up (10 minutes)

**"Community Connections" Activity**

1. Ask participants to stand and form a circle (or remain seated in a circle arrangement).
2. Provide the prompt: *"Name one person, place, or group in your community that has supported you -- even in a small way -- at some point in your life."*
3. Each participant shares briefly (15--20 seconds). As each person shares, they hold one end of a ball of yarn and toss it to the next speaker, creating a visual web.
4. After everyone has shared, ask the group to look at the web they have created. Facilitate a brief observation:
   - *"What do you notice about this web?"*
   - *"What happens if one person lets go?"*
   - *"What does this tell us about community?"*
5. Gather the yarn and transition: *"Today, we are going to map these kinds of connections intentionally -- and plan how to strengthen and use them as peer supporters."*

> **Facilitator Tip:** If the group is larger than 15, split into two circles. If space does not allow standing, use a seated version where participants point or gesture to the next speaker instead of tossing yarn.

---

### Activity 1: Community Asset Mapping (25 minutes)

**Purpose:** Participants identify the strengths, resources, and supports that already exist in their communities, shifting from a deficit-based view ("what's missing") to an asset-based view ("what's already here").

#### Setup (2 minutes)
- Divide participants into small groups of 4--6 people, ideally grouped by geographic proximity or shared community context.
- Distribute flip chart paper and markers to each group.
- Display or point to the sample asset map you prepared.

#### Instructions to Participants (3 minutes)
Read aloud or paraphrase:

*"In your groups, you are going to create a map of the assets -- the strengths and resources -- in your community. This is not about what is missing. This is about what is already there. Think broadly. We are mapping four categories:"*

Write on the board or flip chart:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **People** | Mentors, coaches, elders, teachers, neighbors, friends, family members, faith leaders |
| **Places** | Community centers, libraries, parks, places of worship, schools, barbershops/salons, recreation centers |
| **Organizations** | Nonprofits, clubs, sports leagues, support groups, civic organizations, cultural associations |
| **Informal Supports** | Group chats, neighborhood traditions, mutual aid networks, pickup games, shared meals, carpools |

*"Start by drawing your community in the center of the page -- this could be your neighborhood, your school zone, your town, whatever feels like 'your community.' Then fill in assets in each of the four categories around it. Use different-colored markers for each category. Be as specific as you can -- names, locations, details."*

#### Small Group Work (18 minutes)
- Circulate among groups. Encourage specificity (*"Can you name that person or place?"*).
- Prompt groups that get stuck:
  - *"Where do people in your community go when they need to talk?"*
  - *"Who are the unofficial leaders -- the people everyone trusts?"*
  - *"What happens in your community that brings people together, even if it is not a formal program?"*
  - *"Think about online spaces too. Are there group chats, social media pages, or online groups that support people?"*
- Note: Some participants may realize their community has more assets than they initially thought. Affirm this. Others may identify gaps. Acknowledge these without dwelling on deficits -- they will be addressed in the action planning activity.

#### Wrap-Up (2 minutes)
- Give a two-minute warning. Ask groups to circle their top three assets -- the ones they think are most valuable or most underutilized.

---

### Activity 1 Debrief: Gallery Walk and Discussion (15 minutes)

#### Gallery Walk (7 minutes)
1. Have each group post their community asset map on the designated wall or table area.
2. Distribute 3 sticky notes to each participant (one color).
3. Instruct participants: *"Walk around and look at every group's map. Place a sticky note on any asset that also exists in your community or that you had not thought of but want to learn more about."*
4. Allow 5--7 minutes for the walk. Encourage participants to read carefully and talk to members of other groups if they have questions.

#### Full-Group Discussion (8 minutes)
Facilitate a brief discussion using the following prompts:

- *"What patterns do you notice? Which types of assets showed up on many maps?"*
- *"Were there any surprises -- assets you hadn't considered before?"*
- *"Which assets received the most sticky notes? What does that tell us?"*
- *"Did anyone notice gaps -- categories where most groups had fewer entries?"*
- *"How might you, as a peer supporter, connect someone to one of these assets?"*

> **Facilitator Transition:** *"Now that we can see what already exists, let's add another layer -- community organizations and partners who can support your work as peer supporters."*

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use these prompts during the closing reflection segment. Participants can respond verbally in pairs, in a full-group share, or in writing (journal or sticky note).

### Prompt 1: Discovering Community Assets
- **Situation:** *Think about a time before today when you needed support or resources in your community.*
- **Task:** *What did you need, and what were you trying to find or accomplish?*
- **Action:** *What did you actually do to find support? Where did you look? Who did you ask?*
- **Result:** *What happened? Looking at your community asset map now, what additional resources could you have connected with? How would that have changed the outcome?*

### Prompt 2: Becoming a Resource Connector
- **Situation:** *Imagine a peer comes to you and shares that they are struggling with something -- loneliness, stress, a family problem, or a practical need like food or transportation.*
- **Task:** *Your role as a peer supporter is not to fix the problem but to listen, support, and connect them with resources.*
- **Action:** *Using your resource directory and asset map, what steps would you take? What would you say? Where would you point them?*
- **Result:** *What would a good outcome look like -- for your peer and for you? How would you follow up?*

### Prompt 3: Sustaining Your Practice
- **Situation:** *It is 30 days from now. You are back in your everyday routine -- school, work, home, social life.*
- **Task:** *You committed to practicing peer support skills and following your action plan.*
- **Action:** *Describe what you imagine yourself doing. Where are you? Who are you supporting? Which skills are you using?*
- **Result:** *What impact do you hope to see -- in yourself, in your relationships, and in your community?*

### Sustainability Planning Framework

Use this structure to guide the community partner conversation toward long-term sustainability:

3. **Feedback Loops:** How will peer supporters and community partners communicate about what is working and what is not? (Monthly check-ins, shared group chats, quarterly meetings.)
4. **Growth:** How might this network of peer supporters and community partners grow over time? (Training additional peer supporters, adding new partners, expanding to new settings.)

### Harrington Framework Connection

This module operationalizes Harrington's principle of reflective closure: participants consolidate learning through structured recall, articulate transfer intentions, and are positioned within a continuing ecosystem of support. The STAR reflection structure reinforces Harrington's emphasis on experiential learning integration.

### NAPS Alignment

- **NAPS Standard 4: Youth Development and Leadership** -- Closing reflection affirms participant growth and emerging leadership identity.
- **NAPS Standard 6: Community Engagement** -- Community partner presence at closing reinforces the ecosystem of ongoing support.
- **NAPS Standard 8: Program Sustainability** -- Evaluation data gathered supports continuous improvement cycles required under national quality standards.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Welcome + Grounding Circle (10 minutes)

**Setup:** Participants seated in a circle. Journey timeline (Modules 1-13) displayed prominently. Soft music playing as participants arrive.

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Welcome to our final session together. Take a look around this circle. These are the people who have walked this journey with you -- who have shared, listened, challenged, and grown alongside you. Today is about honoring that journey, celebrating your growth, and looking ahead to what comes next."

**Grounding Activity: "One Word"**

Go around the circle and ask each participant to share one word that describes how they feel arriving at this final session. The facilitator participates as well.

> "In one word, how are you feeling as we begin our last session together?"

After the round, acknowledge the range of emotions:

> "All of those feelings are welcome here today. Whether you're feeling proud, bittersweet, excited, or something else entirely -- that's part of what makes this moment real."

**Transition:** Briefly walk participants through the session flow so they know what to expect. Emphasize that this is their time.

---

### Activity 2: Full-Group Reflection -- "Our Collective Journey" (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Guide participants through a structured reflection that synthesizes insights from the entire training.

**Part A: Journey Mapping (8 minutes)**

Distribute two colors of sticky notes to each participant.

> "On the [first color] sticky notes, write down the things you are **taking with you** from this experience -- skills, insights, relationships, mindset shifts. One idea per sticky note. Write as many as you'd like."
>
> "On the [second color] sticky notes, write down the things you are **leaving behind** -- old habits, limiting beliefs, fears, or assumptions that no longer serve you. One idea per sticky note."

Give participants 5 minutes to write. Then invite them to place their sticky notes on the large poster paper on the wall, grouped by color.

**Part B: Gallery Reflection (4 minutes)**

Invite participants to stand and read what others have written. Encourage quiet reflection. No discussion yet.

**Part C: Group Discussion Using STAR Format (8 minutes)**

Bring participants back to the circle. Use the following guided prompts to facilitate a full-group conversation. Not everyone needs to answer every prompt -- invite willing voices.

> **Situation:** "Think back to your very first session. What was going on in your life at that time? What brought you here?"
>
> **Task:** "What did you hope to get out of this training? What were you working toward?"
>
> **Action:** "What is something specific you did during this training that pushed you outside your comfort zone or helped you grow?"
>
> **Result:** "How are you different now compared to when you started? What has changed in how you see yourself, your community, or your role in it?"

**Facilitator Note:** Validate every response. Use bridging language like "Thank you for sharing that" and "That connects to what [name] said about..." to weave a collective narrative.

---

### Activity 3: Letter to My Future Self (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Participants write a personal letter capturing their current commitments, hopes, and state of mind. The letter will be mailed to them 3-6 months later as a reminder of their intentions.

**Facilitator Script:**

> "You're going to write a letter to yourself -- the version of you who will open this letter a few months from now. This letter is private. No one else will read it. Be honest. Be kind to yourself. And be specific."

**Distribute:** Stationery and envelopes.

**Prompt Card (distribute to each participant):**

---

**Letter to My Future Self**

*Date: _______________*

*Dear Future Me,*

*Use the prompts below to guide your letter, or write freely.*

1. **Right now, I feel...** *(Describe your current emotional state as you complete this training.)*

2. **The most important thing I learned is...** *(Name the insight, skill, or realization that stands out most.)*

3. **I am committing to...** *(Be specific. What will you do differently in your daily life, your community, or your relationships?)*

4. **When things get hard, I want to remember...** *(Write a message of encouragement from your present self to your future self.)*

5. **The people I want to thank are...** *(Name them, and say why.)*

6. **Six months from now, I hope I am...** *(Describe the version of yourself you are working toward.)*

*With belief in you,*
*Me*

---

**After 12 minutes of writing time:**

> "When you're ready, seal your letter in the envelope and write your mailing address on the front. We will mail these to you in [3/6] months as a gift from your present self."

Collect sealed envelopes. Store securely.

### Activity 5: Certificate Ceremony (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Formally recognize each participant's completion of the training.

**Setup:** Certificates arranged in presentation order. If possible, have a program director, community partner representative, or respected community member assist with presentation.

**Facilitator Script:**

> "This certificate represents more than attendance. It represents the time you invested, the courage you showed, the growth you chose, and the commitment you've made to your community. When I call your name, please come forward."

**Ceremony Format:**

For each participant:
1. Call the participant's name.
2. Share one brief, specific affirmation about their participation (prepare these in advance -- reference a moment, contribution, or quality you observed).
3. Present the certificate with a handshake, hug, or culturally appropriate gesture.

### Activity 7: Closing Circle + Send-Off (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Create a meaningful final moment of connection and send participants off with energy and intention.

**Closing Round -- "My Commitment in One Sentence"**

> "We're going to go around the circle one final time. In one sentence, share one commitment you're making as you leave this space. What will you carry forward?"

Allow each participant to share. The facilitator goes last.

**Group Affirmation:**

> "I'd like to ask everyone to stand. Look around this circle one more time. These are the people who believed in this work, who showed up, who did the hard and beautiful thing of growing in community. You did this. And you carry each other's stories with you."

**Optional Closing Ritual (choose one based on group culture):**

- **Linked hands moment:** Participants hold hands in silence for 10 seconds, then release with a collective exhale or word of affirmation.
- **Call and response:** Facilitator says "We came as individuals," group responds "We leave as community."
- **Song or spoken word:** A participant or community partner leads a brief song, poem, or spoken-word piece.

**Group Photo**

> "Before we head to the refreshments -- let's get a group photo! Everyone gather in."

Take the photo. Share digitally with participants afterward (with appropriate permissions).

**Celebration and Refreshments**

Release participants to refreshments, informal conversation, and continued connection. Community partner representatives remain available for one-on-one conversations.

## STAR Reflection Prompts

This module's reflection prompts are designed to help participants synthesize their **entire training journey** across all 13 modules. Use these in the full-group reflection activity (Activity 2) and encourage participants to draw on them during the Letter to My Future Self (Activity 3).

### Prompt Set 1: The Full Arc

- **Situation:** "When you first walked into Module 1, what was happening in your life? What circumstances brought you to this training?"
- **Task:** "What did you hope to accomplish, learn, or change by participating? What goal -- spoken or unspoken -- were you carrying?"
- **Action:** "Name one specific thing you did during this training that you are proud of. It could be something you said, a risk you took, or a moment when you showed up differently than you would have before."
- **Result:** "What is different now? In yourself, in your relationships, in how you see your role in your community?"

### Prompt Set 2: Skills in Action

- **Situation:** "Think about a real situation in your life -- at home, at work, in your neighborhood -- where you recently faced a challenge."
- **Task:** "What did the situation require of you? What needed to happen?"
- **Action:** "Did you use any of the skills or strategies you learned in this training to handle it? What did you do?"
- **Result:** "How did it turn out? What would you do the same or differently next time?"

### Prompt Set 3: Community Impact

- **Situation:** "Consider your community right now -- the people, places, and systems around you."
- **Task:** "What does your community need from people who have been through an experience like this?"
- **Action:** "What is one step you can take in the next 30 days to contribute to your community based on what you've learned?"
- **Result:** "If you take that step, what do you hope the impact will be -- for you and for others?"

### Prompt Set 4: Relationships and Connection

- **Situation:** "Think about the relationships you've formed or deepened during this training."
- **Task:** "What was it about this environment that made it possible to connect with others?"
- **Action:** "What did you do to build trust, show support, or be a good participant in this group?"
- **Result:** "How will you sustain those connections after today? What will you carry from these relationships?"

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Post-Training Assessment Strategy

Module 13 serves as the final assessment point. Assessment in this session is **holistic and reflective** rather than quiz-based. Data is gathered through three channels:

**1. Observation-Based Facilitator Assessment**

During the full-group reflection and closing circle, the facilitator assesses the following indicators:

| Indicator | Evidence to Look For |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Synthesis of learning** | Participant references concepts or skills from multiple modules, not just the most recent sessions |
| **Self-awareness and growth** | Participant articulates specific ways they have changed, with concrete examples |
| **Community orientation** | Participant connects their learning to community impact, not just personal benefit |
| **Commitment to action** | Participant names specific, realistic next steps rather than vague aspirations |
| **Relational connection** | Participant acknowledges the contributions of peers and the value of the group experience |

Complete the facilitator observation form after the session, not during (to remain present with the group).

**2. Participant Self-Assessment (Embedded in Evaluation)**

The pre/post confidence ratings in the evaluation form (Section 2) provide quantitative self-assessment data. Compare with any pre-training baseline data collected during Module 1.

**3. Letter to My Future Self (Delayed Assessment)**

The sealed letter serves as a delayed self-assessment tool. When mailed back to participants in 3-6 months, it creates an opportunity for self-comparison between intentions and actions. Consider pairing the mailing with a brief follow-up survey.

## Community Partner Integration

### Purpose

The closing session is a high-visibility opportunity for community partners to reinforce the message that the participant's journey does not end with the training. Partners present ongoing pathways, resources, and support structures that sustain growth beyond the program.

### Pre-Session Coordination

- Invite community partner representatives at least **two weeks** in advance. Provide a brief overview of the session flow and their role.
- Ask each partner to prepare:
  - A 2-minute overview of their organization and how participants can stay connected.
  - Printed resource cards, flyers, or information packets for each participant.
  - At least one specific, upcoming opportunity participants can engage with immediately (e.g., a volunteer day, a workshop, a mentoring program, a job fair).
- Confirm partner attendance **48 hours** before the session.

### During the Session (Activity 4: Community Partner Moment -- 10 minutes)

**Facilitator Introduction:**

> "One of the things that makes this program powerful is that you're not doing this alone. You're connected to a network of people and organizations that believe in you and want to see you succeed. I'd like to invite our community partners to share a few words about how you can stay connected after today."

**Partner Presentations (2-3 minutes each, maximum of 3 partners):**

Each partner briefly shares:
1. Who they are and what they do.
2. One specific way participants can engage with them in the next 30 days.
3. How to contact them (distribute resource cards).

**Facilitator Bridge:**

> "Thank you to our partners for being here today and for being part of your journey. Their information is in the resource packet you'll receive. I encourage you to reach out -- these connections are a real extension of the work you've done here."

### Resource Packet Contents (Prepared in Advance)

Each participant receives a packet or folder containing:

- [ ] Community partner contact information cards
- [ ] List of upcoming community events and opportunities
- [ ] Local resource directory (mental health services, employment support, educational opportunities, civic engagement)
- [ ] Facilitator and/or program coordinator contact information for continued support
- [ ] Alumni network information (if applicable) -- online group, quarterly meetups, mentoring opportunities
- [ ] Any relevant referral forms or sign-up sheets for partner programs

### Post-Session Partner Follow-Up

Within one week of the closing session:

- Send a thank-you message to each attending community partner.
- Share aggregate (non-identifying) feedback from evaluations related to community partnership.
- Discuss potential referrals or warm handoffs for participants who expressed interest.
- Begin planning for alumni engagement touchpoints (30-day check-in, 90-day reunion, letter mailing).
