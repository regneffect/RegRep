# Module 9: Setting Boundaries

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify and describe at least three types of personal and professional boundaries relevant to peer support roles.
2. Recognize signs that a boundary has been crossed or is at risk of being violated in a support relationship.
3. Demonstrate at least two assertive communication techniques for setting and maintaining boundaries.
4. Distinguish between being supportive and overextending oneself in a peer supporter role.
5. Develop a personal boundary plan that protects both the supporter and the person being supported.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL USE ONLY -- NOT FOR PARTICIPANT DISTRIBUTION**

### Curriculum Context

This module is a cornerstone of peer supporter safety and sustainability within the SCBF 2.0 framework. Research consistently shows that peer supporters who lack boundary-setting skills are at significantly higher risk of compassion fatigue, burnout, and secondary traumatic stress. This session must be treated with the same seriousness as skill-building modules; it is not optional enrichment.

### Alignment to Standards

This module addresses the following NAPS (National AfterSchool Partnership Standards) domains:

- **NAPS Domain 3: Health and Safety** -- Ensuring the emotional and psychological safety of youth in support roles.
- **NAPS Domain 4: Youth Development** -- Building self-awareness and interpersonal competence as developmental assets.
- **NAPS Domain 6: Family, School, and Community Partnerships** -- Establishing appropriate relational boundaries across contexts.

The Harrington model of peer support competency identifies boundary-setting as a Tier 2 skill (applied competency), requiring both conceptual understanding and repeated guided practice before field deployment.

### Facilitator Mindset

- **Normalize boundary-setting.** Many young people have been socialized to view boundary-setting as selfish or unkind. Actively counter this narrative throughout the session.
- **Watch for disclosures.** Boundary discussions can surface personal experiences of boundary violations, including abuse. Have your referral protocol ready and a co-facilitator or support person available.
- **Model your own boundaries.** Share (at an appropriate level) a time you set a boundary in a professional or volunteer role. Participants learn from what you do, not just what you say.
- **Pacing note.** The source content for this module covers approximately 45 minutes. The 90-minute session expands practice time significantly. Use the extra time for deeper scenario work and role-play rehearsal -- do not rush through activities to fill time with lecture.

### Room Setup

Arrange seating in a circle or U-shape for the opening and closing segments. Ensure space for small groups of 3-4 to work together during scenario activities. Post chart paper on walls for the Boundary Types brainstorm.

### Pre-Session Checklist

- [ ] Print all handouts (one per participant)
- [ ] Cut scenario cards (Handout 9B) into individual cards
- [ ] Prepare chart paper with headers: "Physical," "Emotional," "Time/Energy," "Role," "Digital"
- [ ] Review scenario cards and adapt any that do not reflect the cultural context of your participant group
- [ ] Confirm referral resources are current and accessible
- [ ] Brief co-facilitator on disclosure protocol

## Activities

### Activity 1: Boundary Types Brainstorm (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build shared vocabulary around different types of boundaries.

**Setup:** Post five sheets of chart paper around the room, each labeled with one boundary type: **Physical**, **Emotional**, **Time/Energy**, **Role**, **Digital**.

**Instructions for Participants:**

1. You will rotate through five stations around the room. At each station, you will see a type of boundary.
2. With your marker, write one or two examples of what that type of boundary looks like in real life -- especially in situations where you are helping or supporting someone.
3. Spend about 2 minutes at each station, then rotate when signaled.
4. After the rotation, we will do a gallery walk and discuss what we notice.

**Facilitation Notes:**

- After the gallery walk, highlight patterns. Ask: "Which type of boundary had the most examples? Which had the fewest? Why do you think that is?"
- Introduce any boundary types that participants did not generate organically. Pay special attention to **Role Boundaries** (the limits of what a peer supporter should and should not do) and **Digital Boundaries** (social media, texting availability, sharing personal accounts).

**Definitions to Share (post or distribute):**

| Boundary Type | What It Means |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Physical** | Limits around personal space, touch, and physical proximity |
| **Emotional** | Limits around how much of others' emotional weight you take on; protecting your own feelings |
| **Time/Energy** | Limits on when, how long, and how often you are available to support someone |
| **Role** | Limits on what you are qualified and expected to do as a peer supporter versus what requires a trained professional |
| **Digital** | Limits on online availability, sharing personal accounts, and communication platforms |

---

### Activity 2: "Where's the Line?" Scenario Discussion (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Apply boundary concepts to realistic peer support situations and practice recognizing when boundaries are at risk.

**Setup:** Divide participants into small groups of 3-4. Distribute one set of scenario cards (Handout 9B) to each group.

**Instructions for Participants:**

> **Handout 9B: "Where's the Line?" Scenario Cards**

Read each scenario aloud in your small group. For each one, discuss:

1. **What type(s) of boundary is involved?** (Physical, Emotional, Time/Energy, Role, Digital)
2. **Is the boundary being respected, tested, or crossed?**
3. **What would you do in this situation?**

---

**Scenario 1: The Late-Night Texter**
A classmate you have been supporting starts texting you every night after 11 PM. The messages are not emergencies -- they want to vent about their day. You have school in the morning and you are losing sleep.

---

**Scenario 2: The Secret Keeper**
Someone you are supporting tells you they have been hurting themselves. They beg you not to tell anyone. "You are the only person I trust," they say.

---

**Scenario 3: The Social Media Request**
A younger participant you mentor asks to follow your personal Instagram account. Your account has photos of your family and personal life.

---

**Scenario 4: The Emotional Sponge**
After every support session, you feel drained and sad. You have started dreaming about the problems the people you support are dealing with. A friend asks if you are okay, and you snap at them.

---

**Scenario 5: The Gift Giver**
A family you have been supporting brings you an expensive gift card as a thank-you. They seem genuinely grateful and you do not want to hurt their feelings.

---

**Scenario 6: The Expanded Role**
Your program coordinator asks you to drive a participant home after a session because their ride fell through. You have your license and it is on your way home.

---

**Debrief Questions (Whole Group):**

After small-group discussion, bring the full group back together and ask:

- Which scenario was hardest to decide on? Why?
- Did anyone disagree within their group? What was the disagreement about?
- In which scenarios would you need to involve an adult, supervisor, or professional? How would you do that?

**Facilitation Notes:**

- Scenario 2 is intentionally high-stakes. Use it to reinforce that **role boundaries** mean knowing when something is beyond your scope and requires adult/professional intervention. Affirm that telling a trusted adult is not a betrayal -- it is the most supportive action.
- Scenario 6 addresses liability and organizational policy. Reinforce that even well-intentioned actions can create risk. Peer supporters should always check with their supervisor before taking on tasks outside their defined role.

### Activity 3: Practice Saying No / Setting Limits (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build muscle memory for assertive boundary-setting language through guided role-play.

**Setup:** Pair participants. Distribute Handout 9C: Assertive Language Stems. One partner will play the person requesting something; the other will practice setting a boundary. They will switch roles after each round.

**Instructions for Participants:**

> **Handout 9C: Assertive Language Stems**

Setting a boundary is a skill. Like any skill, it gets easier with practice. Use the sentence stems below as starting points. You do not have to use these exact words -- adapt them to sound like you.

**Saying No / Declining:**
- "I care about you, and I am not able to do that."
- "That is outside what I can help with. Let me connect you with someone who can."
- "I want to support you, and I also need to take care of myself. I cannot do this right now."
- "I am not the right person for this. Let us find someone who is."

**Setting a Limit:**
- "I am available to talk between [time] and [time]. Outside of those hours, I will not be able to respond until the next day."
- "I can help with [specific thing], but [other thing] is something you will need to talk to [appropriate person] about."
- "I need to take a step back for my own well-being. That does not mean I do not care."

**Redirecting:**
- "It sounds like this is really important. I think [name/resource] would be the best person to help with this."
- "I hear you. This is bigger than what I can handle on my own. Can we talk to [supervisor/adult] together?"
- "I appreciate you trusting me. The best thing I can do for you right now is help you get connected to the right support."

**Practice Rounds:**

Complete three rounds. In each round, one partner reads the prompt and the other practices responding using the language stems. Switch roles after each round.

**Round 1:** "Can you stay late after the session today? I really need to talk to someone."
*(Practice: Setting a time/energy boundary)*

**Round 2:** "Please do not tell anyone what I told you. Promise me."
*(Practice: Setting a role boundary -- when you have a duty to involve a professional)*

**Round 3:** "You are the only one who gets me. Can I have your phone number so I can call you whenever?"
*(Practice: Setting a digital/availability boundary)*

**Debrief:**

- How did it feel to say no or set a limit?
- What made it hard? What made it easier?
- Did using a sentence stem help? Would you change the wording to fit your style?

**Facilitation Notes:**

- Circulate during practice and offer encouragement. Many participants will laugh nervously or break character -- this is normal. Gently redirect them to try again.
- After the debrief, affirm: "Setting boundaries is not mean. It is one of the most professional and caring things you can do as a supporter. It keeps you healthy so you can keep showing up."

---

### Activity 4: My Boundary Plan (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Translate learning into a personalized, actionable plan.

**Setup:** Distribute Handout 9D: My Boundary Plan Worksheet. Participants complete this individually.

**Instructions for Participants:**

> **Handout 9D: My Boundary Plan Worksheet**

Think about your role as a peer supporter. Complete each section honestly -- this worksheet is for you, not for grading.

**1. My Availability Boundaries**
- Days and times I am available to support others: ___________________________
- How people can reach me (and how they cannot): ___________________________
- My response time expectation (e.g., "I will respond within 24 hours during the week"): ___________________________

**2. My Role Boundaries**
- Things I am comfortable helping with: ___________________________
- Things that are outside my role (I will refer to a professional): ___________________________
- The person I will go to when something is beyond my scope: ___________________________

**3. My Self-Care Boundaries**
- Signs that I am getting overwhelmed or burned out: ___________________________
- What I will do when I notice those signs: ___________________________
- One person I trust to check in on me: ___________________________

**4. My Boundary Script**
Write one sentence in your own words that you could use to set a boundary with someone you are supporting:

> "_______________________________________________________________"

**Facilitation Notes:**

- Reassure participants that their plans can (and should) evolve over time.
- Encourage participants to share their plan with their supervisor or a trusted adult, but do not require it.
- Collect plans only if your program requires documentation. Otherwise, emphasize that this is a personal tool.

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use the following prompts to guide the closing reflection. Participants may journal individually, share with a partner, or contribute to a whole-group discussion. Select one or two prompts based on remaining time.

### Prompt 1: Recognizing a Boundary Moment

- **Situation:** Think about a time when someone asked you for help, and you felt uncomfortable, overwhelmed, or unsure about whether to say yes.
- **Task:** What was being asked of you? What boundary was being tested?
- **Action:** What did you do? Did you set a boundary, go along with the request, or do something else?
- **Result:** What happened as a result? If you could go back, would you handle it differently? How?

### Prompt 2: The Boundary You Need Most

- **Situation:** Consider your current life -- school, home, friendships, any volunteer or support roles you play.
- **Task:** Identify one area where you need a stronger boundary.
- **Action:** What specific boundary would you set? What would you say or do to put it in place?
- **Result:** How do you think setting that boundary would affect your well-being and your relationships?

### Prompt 3: Supporting Someone Else's Boundary

- **Situation:** Think about a time when someone else set a boundary with you -- a friend, family member, teacher, or mentor.
- **Task:** What was the boundary they set? How was it communicated to you?
- **Action:** How did you respond? Did you respect the boundary, push back, or feel hurt?
- **Result:** What did that experience teach you about how boundaries work in relationships? How does it shape how you will communicate your own boundaries?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Formative Assessment (During Session)

Facilitators should observe and note the following throughout the session:

- [ ] **Activity 1:** Can participants generate examples across all five boundary types, or do they cluster around one or two types?
- [ ] **Activity 2:** Do small groups correctly identify the boundary type in each scenario? Do they recognize when professional referral is needed (especially Scenario 2)?
- [ ] **Activity 3:** Are participants able to use assertive language stems with increasing confidence across the three rounds? Do they adapt the language to sound natural?
- [ ] **Activity 4:** Are boundary plans specific and realistic, or vague and generic?

### Exit Ticket (Final 2 Minutes)

On a sticky note, each participant answers:

**Green sticky note:** Write one boundary you are committed to setting or maintaining as a peer supporter.

**Red sticky note:** Write one question you still have about boundaries, or one situation where you are still unsure what to do.

Collect both. Use the red sticky notes to inform follow-up sessions or individual check-ins.

### Mastery Indicators

A participant has met the objectives for this module when they can:

1. Name at least three types of boundaries and give an example of each.
2. Correctly identify when a scenario requires professional referral rather than peer support.
3. Demonstrate assertive boundary-setting language in a role-play context.
4. Articulate why boundary-setting is a form of care, not a form of rejection.

## Community Partner Integration

### Pre-Session Partnerships

- **Mental health or counseling organizations:** Invite a licensed counselor or social worker to co-facilitate the scenario discussion (Activity 2). Their expertise adds credibility to the message that referral is a strength, not a failure. Brief them on the session format and ensure they understand their role is to support, not lecture.
- **Local youth-serving organizations:** If your participants volunteer or work at partner organizations, request their boundary policies or codes of conduct. Reference these during the Role Boundaries discussion to ground the learning in real-world expectations.

### During-Session Integration

- **Guest speaker (10-15 minutes during Activity 2 debrief):** A community partner professional (e.g., social worker, youth program coordinator, crisis line volunteer) shares a brief story about a time they had to set a boundary in their work. This normalizes boundary-setting as a professional skill used by adults in helping roles.
- **Resource sharing:** Provide participants with printed or digital resource cards from community partners (crisis hotlines, counseling centers, youth support services). This reinforces Activity 3's emphasis on knowing where to refer.

### Post-Session Engagement

- **Boundary policy review:** Partner with the organizations where participants will serve. Have participants compare their personal Boundary Plans (Handout 9D) with the organization's policies. Where do they align? Where might there be tension?
- **Peer supporter supervision:** Establish a regular check-in schedule with community partner sites so that participants have a designated adult to consult when boundary questions arise in the field.
- **Follow-up workshop:** Invite a community partner to lead a follow-up session (2-4 weeks later) on compassion fatigue and self-care, building directly on the foundation laid in this module.

### Partner Communication Template

> Dear [Partner Name],
>
> Our peer supporters recently completed a session on Setting Boundaries. They learned about types of boundaries, practiced assertive communication, and created personal boundary plans. As they begin or continue their work at your site, we ask that you:
>
> 1. Review your organization's boundary expectations with them during their first shift/session.
> 2. Designate a point person they can approach with boundary questions or concerns.
> 3. Let us know if you observe any situations where additional support or coaching would be helpful.
>
> Thank you for partnering with us to keep our young people safe and effective in their roles.
