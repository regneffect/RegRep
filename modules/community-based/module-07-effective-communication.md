# Module 7: Effective Communication

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes
**Module Sequence:** Module 7 of 13 | **Builds on:** Module 6 (Communication Styles)

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify at least five nonverbal cues that influence communication and describe how body language can support or undermine a spoken message.
2. Explain three de-escalation techniques and demonstrate their use in a simulated conversation.
3. Practice giving and receiving constructive feedback during paired communication exercises.
4. Recognize the connection between effective communication and supporting peers through difficult moments.
5. Apply assertive communication strategies (introduced in Module 6) within conflict-oriented scenarios of increasing complexity.

- **Harrington Framework:** The de-escalation content aligns with Harrington's relational safety principles. Emphasize that de-escalation is about creating conditions for connection, not controlling another person's behavior.

### Facilitation Considerations
- **Builds on Module 6:** Participants should arrive with a working understanding of assertive, passive, aggressive, and passive-aggressive communication styles, as well as "I" statement construction. Briefly review these concepts in the opening if needed, but do not re-teach them.
- **Expanded to 90 minutes:** The source material runs approximately 60 minutes. The additional 30 minutes are allocated to:
  - A deeper nonverbal communication exploration activity (Activity 1 expanded with observation rounds)
  - Extended practice conversation time with structured feedback (Activity 3 gets a third round and written feedback)
  - A community partner integration discussion segment
- **Conflict scenarios:** This module introduces conflict-oriented practice for the first time. Some participants may have strong emotional responses. Normalize discomfort, remind participants of group agreements, and be prepared to pause and process if needed.
- **Trauma-informed lens:** Some participants may have personal experience with escalated conflict. Avoid requiring disclosure. Frame all scenarios as hypothetical and allow participants to opt out of any role-play that feels activating. Offer an observer/note-taker role as an alternative.

## Activities

### Activity 1: The Unspoken Message
**Duration:** 20 minutes | **Format:** Paired exercise with full-group debrief

#### Purpose
Participants explore how nonverbal communication -- body language, facial expressions, tone of voice, physical distance, and eye contact -- shapes the meaning of any interaction. This activity builds awareness that *how* we communicate is often more powerful than *what* we say.

#### Setup
- Arrange participants in pairs. If there is an odd number, create one triad.
- Distribute **Handout 7A: Nonverbal Communication Observation Sheet** to each participant.
- Post chart paper labeled: "What We See," "What We Hear," "What We Feel."

#### Instructions

**Round 1: Mismatch (5 minutes)**

Say to participants:
> "In this first round, one person in each pair will be the Speaker and the other will be the Observer. Speakers, I'm going to ask you to tell your partner about something you genuinely enjoy -- a hobby, a favorite food, a place you like to go. But here's the twist: while you're talking about something positive, I want you to use body language that says the opposite. Cross your arms, look away, frown, speak in a flat tone. Observers, use your Observation Sheet to note what you see and what it feels like to receive that message."

Allow 2 minutes for the exercise, then have partners switch roles and repeat.

**Round 2: Alignment (5 minutes)**

> "Now repeat the same exercise, but this time, let your body language match your words. Lean in, make comfortable eye contact, smile if it's natural, use an open posture. Observers, note the difference on your sheet."

Allow 2 minutes, then switch roles and repeat.

**Debrief (10 minutes)**

Bring the full group together. Facilitate discussion using these questions:
- "Observers, what did you notice in Round 1 versus Round 2?"
- "Speakers, how did it feel to deliberately mismatch your words and body language?"
- "When have you experienced someone saying one thing but their body language saying something else? What impact did that have?"

Record key observations on the chart paper under the three headers. Highlight that research suggests nonverbal cues carry significant weight in how messages are received -- often more than the words themselves.

#### Key Takeaways to Emphasize
- Nonverbal communication includes: facial expressions, eye contact, posture, gestures, tone of voice, physical distance, and touch.
- When verbal and nonverbal messages conflict, people tend to trust the nonverbal message.
- As peer supporters, aligning our body language with our words builds trust and safety.

### Activity 2: Cooling the Temperature
**Duration:** 20 minutes | **Format:** Facilitator-led introduction with small-group application

#### Purpose
Participants learn foundational de-escalation techniques -- practical strategies for responding when a conversation becomes heated, tense, or emotionally charged. The emphasis is on creating safety, not on controlling the other person.

#### Setup
- Distribute **Handout 7B: De-Escalation Techniques Quick Reference** to each participant.
- Remain in the full-group circle for the introduction; transition to groups of 3-4 for the application portion.

#### Part 1: Introduction (8 minutes)

Introduce the concept:
> "In Module 6, we practiced communicating assertively and using 'I' statements. Those skills are powerful. But what happens when a conversation starts to escalate -- when emotions are running high and someone is upset, frustrated, or angry? Today we're going to learn some techniques for 'cooling the temperature' so we can stay connected even when things get hard."

Walk through the five de-escalation techniques on Handout 7B, demonstrating each briefly:

1. **Regulate Your Own Response First.** Before trying to calm someone else, notice your own body. Take a slow breath. Unclench your jaw. Drop your shoulders. You cannot de-escalate someone else if you are escalated yourself.

2. **Lower and Slow Your Voice.** When tension rises, voices tend to get louder and faster. Deliberately speak in a calm, steady, slightly slower pace. This signals safety.

3. **Validate the Emotion, Not Necessarily the Behavior.** Name what you observe without judgment. "I can see you're really frustrated right now" acknowledges their experience without agreeing or disagreeing with their actions.

4. **Use Open Body Language.** Uncross your arms. Keep your hands visible. Turn your body toward the person. Maintain a comfortable distance -- not too close, not too far. Avoid standing over someone who is sitting.

5. **Offer Choice and Control.** When people feel out of control, offering small choices helps restore agency. "Would you like to take a walk, or would you rather sit here for a minute?" "Do you want to talk about it now, or come back to it later?"

#### Part 2: Spot the Technique (12 minutes)

Divide participants into groups of 3-4. Give each group two scenario cards from the de-escalation set in **Handout 7C**. Instructions:

> "Read each scenario together. Then discuss: Which de-escalation techniques would be most helpful here? Why? Be ready to share one scenario with the full group and explain your thinking."

Allow 7 minutes for small-group discussion, then have 2-3 groups share their scenarios and reasoning (5 minutes).

#### Key Takeaways to Emphasize
- De-escalation is about creating conditions for connection, not about winning an argument or controlling someone.
- Your own calm is the most powerful de-escalation tool you have.
- These techniques work in everyday life -- not just in crisis moments.

---

### Handout 7B: De-Escalation Techniques Quick Reference

**Five Ways to Cool the Temperature**

| # | Technique | What It Sounds / Looks Like | Why It Works |
|---|-----------|----------------------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Regulate Your Own Response First** | Pause. Breathe slowly. Relax your body. Notice your own emotions without acting on them. | You cannot help someone else calm down if you are escalated. Your calm is contagious. |
| 2 | **Lower and Slow Your Voice** | Speak at a steady, unhurried pace. Keep your volume at or below a normal conversational level. | A slower, quieter voice signals safety and reduces the sense of threat. |
| 3 | **Validate the Emotion** | "I can see this is really upsetting." / "It makes sense that you'd feel that way." | People need to feel heard before they can hear you. Validation is not agreement -- it is acknowledgment. |
| 4 | **Use Open Body Language** | Uncrossed arms. Hands visible. Comfortable distance. Face the person. Get on their physical level. | Open posture communicates respect and reduces defensiveness. |
| 5 | **Offer Choice and Control** | "Would you like to step outside for a minute?" / "Do you want to talk now or take a break first?" | When people feel powerless, small choices restore agency and reduce the need to fight for control. |

**Remember:** De-escalation is not about making someone's feelings go away. It is about creating enough safety that a real conversation can happen.

### Activity 3: Real Talk -- Practice Conversations
**Duration:** 20 minutes | **Format:** Paired practice with rotating roles and structured feedback

#### Purpose
Participants apply communication skills -- assertive communication, "I" statements, nonverbal awareness, and de-escalation techniques -- in realistic practice conversations. Each round increases in complexity. Structured feedback helps participants identify their strengths and growth areas.

#### Setup
- Arrange participants in pairs (different from Activity 1 if possible).
- Distribute **Handout 7C: Practice Conversation Scenario Cards** -- give each pair one card per round.
- Distribute **Handout 7D: Feedback Frame Worksheet** to each participant.

#### Instructions

Explain the structure:
> "We're going to practice three short conversations. In each round, one person plays Role A and the other plays Role B -- the roles are described on each card. After each conversation, you'll give each other feedback using the Feedback Frame on your worksheet. Then you'll switch roles for the next round."

**Round 1: Low Stakes (5 minutes)**
- Distribute Scenario Card Set 1 (everyday disagreement).
- 2 minutes for the conversation; 2 minutes for feedback using the Feedback Frame; 1 minute to reset.

**Round 2: Medium Stakes (5 minutes)**
- Distribute Scenario Card Set 2 (emotional conversation with a peer).
- Same timing: 2 minutes conversation, 2 minutes feedback, 1 minute reset. Switch who plays Role A.

**Round 3: Higher Stakes (7 minutes)**
- Distribute Scenario Card Set 3 (conflict scenario requiring de-escalation).
- 3 minutes for the conversation; 3 minutes for feedback; 1 minute for personal reflection.

**Brief Debrief (3 minutes)**

Ask the full group:
- "What got easier as we practiced? What stayed challenging?"
- "Which techniques felt most natural to you? Which felt like a stretch?"

**SCENARIO CARD SET 1 -- LOW STAKES**

**Situation:** Two friends are planning an event together. They disagree about the format.

**Role A:** You want the event to be outdoors at a park. You feel strongly about this because you think people need fresh air and open space.

**Role B:** You want the event to be indoors at a community center. You're worried about weather and accessibility for people with mobility challenges.

**Your goal:** Reach a plan you can both feel good about. Use "I" statements and assertive communication. Pay attention to your nonverbal cues.

---

**SCENARIO CARD SET 2 -- MEDIUM STAKES**

**Situation:** A peer comes to you visibly upset about something that happened at home.

**Role A (Peer Supporter):** Your friend/peer seems really stressed. You want to be supportive. Practice active listening, empathy, and open body language. Avoid jumping to advice or problem-solving.

**Role B (Person Seeking Support):** You are upset because a family member said something hurtful. You don't want someone to fix it -- you just want to feel heard. Share as much or as little as feels comfortable (this is practice, so you may use a fictional situation).

**Your goal:** Practice being fully present with someone who is hurting. Focus on listening and validating, not fixing.

---

**SCENARIO CARD SET 3 -- HIGHER STAKES**

**Situation:** A peer is upset and their frustration is escalating. They are speaking loudly and seem like they might shut down or walk away.

**Role A (Peer Supporter):** Your peer is upset about being excluded from a group project at school/work/a community program. Their voice is raised and they're pacing. Use de-escalation techniques: regulate yourself first, lower and slow your voice, validate their emotion, use open body language, and offer choices.

**Role B (Upset Peer):** You feel angry and dismissed. Start the conversation at a high emotional level. If your partner uses de-escalation techniques effectively, gradually allow yourself to feel heard and begin to calm down. If they don't, stay escalated (respectfully -- this is practice).

**Your goal:** Practice de-escalation in a realistic scenario. Notice what works and what doesn't.

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use these prompts to guide the closing reflection. Participants may respond verbally in pairs, in a full-group share, or in writing -- choose the format that best fits your group's energy and comfort level.

---

### Prompt 1: Nonverbal Awareness

- **Situation:** Think about a recent interaction where you noticed someone's body language sending a different message than their words.
- **Task:** What were you trying to understand or communicate in that moment?
- **Action:** What did you do in response to the mismatch? Did you address it, adjust your own approach, or let it go?
- **Result:** What happened as a result? Looking back, would you do anything differently now that you've practiced today?

---

### Prompt 2: De-Escalation in Your Life

- **Situation:** Recall a time when a conversation you were part of started to escalate -- voices got louder, tension increased, or someone shut down.
- **Task:** What was your role in that moment? Were you a participant in the conflict, a bystander, or a supporter?
- **Action:** What did you do? Looking at today's five de-escalation techniques, did you use any of them naturally -- or did you respond in a way you'd like to change?
- **Result:** How did the situation end? What might have been different if you had used one or more of today's techniques?

---

### Prompt 3: Practice Conversation Reflection

- **Situation:** During the practice conversations today, you played both the supporter role and the person seeking support (or the escalated person).
- **Task:** Which role felt more challenging for you, and why?
- **Action:** What specific technique or skill did you try during the practice that felt new or uncomfortable?
- **Result:** What did you learn about yourself as a communicator from that experience? What will you carry forward?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Informal Assessment (During Session)
Facilitators should observe and note the following during activities:

- [ ] **Activity 1:** Can participants identify specific nonverbal cues and articulate their impact? Do observation sheets show detailed, thoughtful responses?
- [ ] **Activity 2:** In small-group discussion, can participants correctly identify which de-escalation techniques apply to given scenarios and explain why?
- [ ] **Activity 3:** During practice conversations, are participants attempting to use "I" statements, active listening, empathy, open body language, and/or de-escalation techniques? Is quality improving across rounds?

### Exit Ticket (Final 2 minutes of reflection time)
Ask participants to write brief answers to the following on an index card or the back of any handout. Collect these as participants leave.

1. **Name two nonverbal cues** that you will pay more attention to in your own communication this week.
2. **Which de-escalation technique** do you think will be most useful to you, and in what situation?
3. **One thing I'm still working on** when it comes to effective communication is: ___________

### Mastery Indicators
Participants demonstrate mastery of this module when they can:
- Accurately describe how nonverbal communication influences message reception
- Name and explain at least three de-escalation techniques
- Demonstrate assertive communication with aligned verbal and nonverbal messages in a practice scenario
- Provide specific, constructive peer feedback using the Feedback Frame

## Community Partner Integration

### Connection Points for This Module

Effective communication and de-escalation are skills valued across community settings. Use these integration points to connect the session content to participants' real-world communities.

#### Before the Session
- **Guest Contributor Option:** Invite a community partner (e.g., a community mediator, youth program coordinator, peer support specialist, restorative justice facilitator, or crisis line volunteer) to co-facilitate the de-escalation segment or share a brief (5-minute) story about how communication skills show up in their work.
- **Coordination:** Provide the guest with Handout 7B in advance. Ask them to share one real example (appropriately anonymized) of de-escalation in practice.

#### During the Session
- **Community Connection Discussion (built into the session outline at 1:20):**
  Facilitate a brief discussion with the following prompts:
  - "Where in your community do you see effective communication making a difference? Where do you see it breaking down?"
  - "What community spaces or programs could benefit from the skills we practiced today?"
  - "If you could bring one technique from today into a community setting you're part of, which would it be and where would you use it?"

#### After the Session
- **Practice Challenge:** Encourage participants to practice one de-escalation technique in a real (low-stakes) interaction before the next session and come prepared to share their experience using a STAR reflection.
- **Community Mapping Bridge:** This module's communication focus connects to Module 12 (Building Community), where participants will map community resources. Invite participants to start noticing communication strengths and challenges in their community settings.
- **Partner Follow-Up:** If a community partner contributed, send a thank-you note and share (with participant permission) one piece of anonymous positive feedback from the group.
