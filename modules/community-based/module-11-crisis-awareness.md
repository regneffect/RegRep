# Module 11: Crisis Awareness

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify at least five warning signs that a peer may be experiencing a crisis.
2. Distinguish between the role of a peer supporter and the role of a professional crisis counselor.
3. Describe when and how to connect a peer to professional support resources.
4. Explain the basics of mandated reporting and why it exists.
5. Recognize the components of a safety plan and understand its purpose.
6. Practice language and steps for making a warm referral to a trusted adult or crisis service.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- FOR FACILITATOR USE ONLY**

### Content Warning and Emotional Safety

This is the heaviest content module in the curriculum. The material addresses crisis recognition, suicidal ideation, self-harm, abuse, and mandated reporting. Facilitators must prepare with the following protocols:

**Before the Session:**

- Review Harrington's framework on trauma-informed peer education delivery, particularly the scaffolded disclosure model and the "contain, connect, convey" facilitation sequence (Harrington, 2021, Chapter 7).
- Confirm that at least one licensed counselor, social worker, or mental health professional is on-site or on-call during this session. Do not facilitate this module without backup clinical support.
- Pre-populate Handout 11-E with local crisis resources: crisis hotline numbers, local mental health agencies, school counselor contacts (if applicable), and community partner referral pathways.
- Review your organization's mandated reporting policy and know your state/local reporting obligations.
- Prepare a "quiet space" near the session room where a participant can step out with a support person if content becomes triggering.

**Emotional Safety Protocols:**

1. **Opening Frame:** Begin the session by normalizing that this content is heavy. Explicitly state that participants may step out at any time without explanation and that a support person is available.
2. **Grounding Anchors:** Place fidget tools, stress balls, and tissues on every table. Briefly teach a 5-4-3-2-1 grounding exercise at the start (see Activity 1).
3. **Disclosure Protocol:** If a participant discloses active crisis, abuse, or harm during the session:
   - Remain calm. Thank them for trusting the group.
   - Do not promise confidentiality for safety concerns.
   - Gently transition the group activity and privately connect the participant with the on-site support person.
   - Document the disclosure per your organization's incident reporting policy.
   - Follow mandated reporting requirements as applicable.
4. **Facilitator Self-Care:** Debrief with a co-facilitator or supervisor after the session. This content affects facilitators too.

**Key Facilitation Reminders (per Harrington):**

- Harrington emphasizes that peer supporters must understand their role boundaries before engaging with crisis content. The "helper's boundary" concept (Harrington, 2021, pp. 134-138) should undergird every activity. Peer supporters are bridges to professional help, never replacements for it.
- Use Harrington's "three-gate check" before each activity: (1) Is the environment safe? (2) Are participants emotionally regulated enough to proceed? (3) Is professional backup accessible? If any gate fails, pause and address before continuing.
- Align to NAPS Standard 4 (Safe and Supportive Environments) and Standard 6 (Community and Family Engagement) throughout the session, particularly during the referral pathway and community partner segments.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Recognizing the Signs (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build participants' ability to notice when a peer may be in crisis.

**Setup:** Post four sheets of chart paper around the room, each labeled with one category: *Emotional Signs*, *Behavioral Signs*, *Physical Signs*, *Situational Signs*.

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min)** Facilitator introduces the activity:
   > "One of the most important skills you can develop is the ability to notice when someone around you might be struggling in a serious way. Today we are going to build a shared understanding of what crisis warning signs can look like. Remember -- noticing is the first step. What you do with what you notice is what we will work on for the rest of this session."

2. **(6 min)** Divide participants into four groups. Each group starts at one chart paper station with markers. Groups have 90 seconds at each station to write warning signs they can think of for that category, then rotate. By the end, every group has contributed to every category.

3. **(5 min)** Facilitator walks the room and reviews what was written, adding any critical signs that were missed. Use the reference list below to supplement.

4. **(2 min)** Distribute **Handout 11-A: Crisis Warning Signs Reference Card**. Ask participants to circle any signs they had not previously considered.

**Facilitator Supplement -- Warning Signs to Ensure Coverage:**

- **Emotional:** Hopelessness, extreme mood swings, rage, persistent sadness, expressions of feeling trapped, loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities, emotional numbness
- **Behavioral:** Withdrawal from friends and activities, giving away possessions, increased substance use, reckless behavior, sudden calm after a period of distress, searching online for methods of self-harm, saying goodbye in unusual ways
- **Physical:** Changes in sleep patterns, changes in appetite or weight, neglect of personal hygiene, unexplained injuries, fatigue or low energy
- **Situational:** Recent loss (death, breakup, housing, job), bullying or harassment, family conflict or instability, legal trouble, anniversary of a traumatic event, exposure to another person's suicide or crisis

#### Handout 11-A: Crisis Warning Signs Reference Card

**PARTICIPANT HANDOUT**

**Crisis Warning Signs -- What to Watch For**

People in crisis do not always ask for help directly. Learning to recognize warning signs can help you be a better supporter. These signs do not automatically mean someone is in crisis, but they are reasons to check in and show care.

| Category | Warning Signs |
|----------|--------------|
| **Emotional** | Hopelessness or feeling trapped; extreme mood swings; persistent sadness; rage or agitation; emotional numbness; loss of interest in things they used to enjoy |
| **Behavioral** | Pulling away from friends and activities; giving away meaningful possessions; increased use of alcohol or drugs; reckless or risky behavior; sudden calm after a period of depression; talking about being a burden; saying goodbye in unusual ways |
| **Physical** | Major changes in sleep (too much or too little); changes in appetite or weight; neglecting personal care; unexplained injuries; constant fatigue |
| **Situational** | Recent loss (a person, relationship, home, or job); experiencing bullying, harassment, or abuse; major family conflict; legal problems; anniversary of a painful event |

**Remember:** One sign alone may not indicate a crisis. Look for clusters of signs, sudden changes, or escalating patterns. When in doubt, check in.

### Activity 2: My Role, Their Role (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Establish clear boundaries between what a peer supporter does and what a professional crisis counselor does.

**Setup:** Distribute **Handout 11-B**. Prepare a T-chart on the whiteboard labeled "Peer Supporter" and "Professional Counselor."

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min)** Facilitator sets the frame:
   > "This is one of the most important things we will talk about today. You are learning to be a peer supporter -- someone who listens, cares, and connects people to help. You are NOT being trained to be a crisis counselor, therapist, or mental health professional. Knowing the difference is not a limitation. It is what keeps both you and the people you support safe."

2. **(5 min)** In pairs, participants review Handout 11-B and discuss: *"Which of these responsibilities surprised you? Which ones feel hard to accept?"*

3. **(5 min)** Full group share-out. Facilitator records key points on the T-chart and reinforces boundaries. Address common pushback:
   - *"But what if there is no counselor available?"* -- You still refer. You contact a hotline together. You tell a trusted adult. You do not become the counselor.
   - *"What if they made me promise not to tell?"* -- You cannot keep a secret that puts someone's life at risk. You can say: "I care about you too much to keep this to myself. Let us get help together."
   - *"What if I have been through the same thing?"* -- Your experience gives you empathy, but it does not give you clinical training. Share that you understand, and still help them connect to a professional.

4. **(3 min)** Facilitator summarizes with the key message:
   > "Your job is to be the bridge, not the destination. You walk with someone to the door of professional help. You do not have to have all the answers behind that door."

---

#### Handout 11-B: "My Role, Their Role" Comparison Sheet

**PARTICIPANT HANDOUT**

**Understanding Your Role as a Peer Supporter**

You matter. Your willingness to show up for your peers is powerful. Part of showing up responsibly is knowing what falls within your role and what belongs to trained professionals.

| **What a Peer Supporter Does** | **What a Professional Counselor Does** |
|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| Listens without judgment | Conducts clinical assessments |
| Notices warning signs | Diagnoses mental health conditions |
| Checks in on a peer who seems to be struggling | Develops treatment plans |
| Offers empathy and presence | Provides therapy or clinical interventions |
| Encourages the peer to seek professional help | Manages medication referrals (with prescribers) |
| Walks with a peer to a counselor, trusted adult, or resource | Conducts risk assessments and creates clinical safety plans |
| Contacts a crisis hotline with or on behalf of a peer | Manages ongoing cases and follow-up care |
| Tells a trusted adult when someone is in danger | Fulfills mandated reporting obligations (as a licensed professional) |
| Takes care of their own emotional well-being | Supervises peer support programs and provides debriefing |

**The Bottom Line:**

You are a **bridge to help**, not a replacement for professional support. Knowing your limits is a strength, not a weakness. The most effective peer supporters are the ones who know when to say, "I care about you, and this is bigger than what I can help with. Let us find someone who can."

### Activity 3: Making the Connection (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Practice the language and steps for connecting a peer to professional support through warm referrals.

**Setup:** Distribute **Handout 11-C**. Arrange participants in pairs for role-play.

**Instructions:**

1. **(3 min)** Facilitator introduces the concept of a "warm referral":
   > "A warm referral is when you do not just tell someone to get help -- you actively help them connect. It might mean walking with them to a counselor's office, sitting with them while they call a hotline, or introducing them to a trusted adult. It is the difference between pointing at a door and walking someone through it."

2. **(2 min)** Review Handout 11-C together. Walk through the conversation starters and the step-by-step referral process.

3. **(7 min)** Role-play in pairs. Each pair practices two scenarios (switch roles after the first):
   - **Scenario A:** A friend tells you they have been feeling hopeless and do not see the point in anything anymore. Practice using a conversation starter from the handout and walking through the referral steps.
   - **Scenario B:** You notice a peer has been withdrawing from the group, giving away personal items, and seems unusually calm after weeks of being upset. Practice initiating a check-in and suggesting professional support.

4. **(3 min)** Debrief as a full group:
   - What felt natural? What felt awkward?
   - What would make it easier to have these conversations in real life?
   - What barriers might get in the way, and how can you prepare for them?

#### Handout 11-C: Referral Conversation Starters

**PARTICIPANT HANDOUT**

**How to Start the Conversation**

Starting a conversation about crisis can feel intimidating. Here are some phrases you can adapt to your own voice:

**Checking In:**
- "I have noticed you seem different lately, and I want you to know I care. How are you really doing?"
- "You do not have to talk about it if you are not ready, but I am here whenever you are."
- "I am asking because I care about you, not because I want to be nosy."

**When They Share Something Serious:**
- "Thank you for trusting me with this. That took a lot of courage."
- "I hear you, and I want to make sure you get the best support possible."
- "I do not have all the answers, but I know people who can help. Can I help you connect with them?"

**Suggesting Professional Help:**
- "Would it be okay if we talked to [trusted adult / counselor name] together? I will be right there with you."
- "I know a really good resource we could call together right now. You do not have to do this alone."
- "This feels bigger than what I can help with on my own, and that is okay. Let us find someone with the right training."

**If They Resist:**
- "I understand you might not be ready. I will keep checking in because you matter to me."
- "I respect your decision, but I need you to know that if I believe you are in danger, I am going to tell someone who can help. That is what caring looks like."

**Steps for a Warm Referral:**

1. **Listen first.** Let them share. Do not rush to solutions.
2. **Acknowledge.** Validate their feelings without minimizing or exaggerating.
3. **Name your concern.** Be honest: "I am worried about your safety."
4. **Offer to go together.** "Can I walk with you to talk to [resource]?"
5. **Follow through.** Check in afterward. Let them know you still care.
6. **Get support for yourself.** Talk to a trusted adult about how you are feeling after the conversation.

### Activity 4: Mandated Reporting -- What You Need to Know (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Provide a basic, age-appropriate understanding of mandated reporting so participants understand why adults in their lives may be required to act on certain disclosures.

**Setup:** No additional handout needed. This is a facilitator-led informational segment. Use the whiteboard for key points.

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min)** Facilitator introduces the concept:
   > "You may have heard the term 'mandated reporter' before. Today we are going to talk about what that means, because understanding it helps you understand what happens when you or someone you know shares information about certain kinds of harm."

2. **(5 min)** Facilitator covers the following key points (write main ideas on the board):

   **What is mandated reporting?**
   - Certain adults -- like teachers, counselors, coaches, social workers, and medical professionals -- are required by law to report when they have a reasonable suspicion that a young person is being abused, neglected, or is at risk of serious harm.
   - They do not need proof. They report the concern, and trained investigators follow up.

   **Why does it exist?**
   - It exists to protect young people. It is not about getting anyone in trouble. It is about making sure people who need help get it.

   **What gets reported?**
   - Physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, and situations where someone is at imminent risk of harming themselves or others.

   **What does NOT happen:**
   - The reporter does not investigate. They pass the concern to the appropriate agency.
   - A report does not automatically mean someone is removed from their home. Investigators assess the situation and determine the best course of action, which often includes supportive services.

   **What this means for you as a peer supporter:**
   - If someone tells you something that involves abuse, neglect, or danger, and you share that with a trusted adult, that adult may be required to report it. This is not a betrayal. It is part of a system designed to help.
   - You should never promise total confidentiality when safety is at stake.

3. **(3 min)** Open the floor for questions. Address common concerns:
   - *"Will the person know I told?"* -- You can ask the trusted adult about how the process works. Your role is to connect, not to manage the outcome.
   - *"What if reporting makes things worse?"* -- This is a real concern, and it is taken seriously by professionals who handle reports. Not reporting when someone is in danger can also make things worse.

### Activity 5: Safety Planning Overview (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Introduce the concept of a safety plan so participants understand what it is, why it exists, and how it is different from what a peer supporter does.

**Setup:** Distribute **Handout 11-D**. This is an informational overview, not a clinical exercise. Participants will NOT create their own safety plans during this session.

**Important Note to Facilitator:** Safety planning is a clinical tool. This activity introduces the concept at an awareness level only. Participants should understand what a safety plan contains so they can recognize and support the process, but they are NOT being trained to create or manage safety plans.

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min)** Facilitator introduces the concept:
   > "A safety plan is a tool that mental health professionals use with someone who is experiencing a crisis. Think of it like an emergency exit plan -- it maps out what someone can do and who they can contact when they are in a dangerous moment. We are looking at this today so you understand what it is, not so you can create one. That is a professional's job."

2. **(4 min)** Walk through Handout 11-D together. Review each component briefly:
   - Warning signs the person recognizes in themselves
   - Coping strategies they can use on their own
   - People and social settings that provide distraction
   - People they can contact for help
   - Professionals and agencies they can contact in a crisis
   - Making the environment safe

3. **(4 min)** Discussion questions:
   - "How is a safety plan different from advice a friend might give?"
   - "If a friend told you they had a safety plan, how might you support them in using it?"
   - "Why is it important that a professional helps create a safety plan rather than a peer?"

#### Handout 11-D: Safety Planning Overview

**PARTICIPANT HANDOUT**

**What is a Safety Plan?**

A safety plan is a written document created by a person in partnership with a mental health professional. It is a personalized guide for what to do during moments of crisis. It is NOT a contract or a promise. It is a practical, step-by-step tool.

**Components of a Safety Plan:**

| Step | What It Includes |
|------|-----------------|
| **1. Warning Signs** | Personal signs that a crisis may be developing (thoughts, feelings, behaviors, situations) |
| **2. Internal Coping Strategies** | Things the person can do on their own to manage the moment (deep breathing, journaling, physical activity, grounding exercises) |
| **3. Social Distractions** | People and places that can provide healthy distraction and a sense of normalcy |
| **4. People to Contact for Help** | Trusted friends, family members, or mentors the person can reach out to when coping alone is not enough |
| **5. Professional and Crisis Resources** | Therapists, counselors, crisis hotlines, and emergency services |
| **6. Making the Environment Safe** | Steps to reduce access to means of harm during a crisis |

**Your Role as a Peer Supporter:**

- You do NOT create safety plans. That is a professional's role.
- You CAN encourage a peer to work with a professional on a safety plan.
- If a peer shares their safety plan with you, you can support them by gently reminding them of the steps they identified.
- You can be one of the "People to Contact for Help" on someone's safety plan. That means your job is to listen, be present, and help them move to the next step on their plan.

## STAR Reflection Prompts

**Instructions to Facilitator:** Distribute index cards. Ask participants to respond in writing to one of the following prompts. Remind them that the STAR format helps organize their thinking. Collected cards serve as both a reflection tool and an informal assessment.

> **Prompt Option 1:**
>
> Think about a time when you noticed that someone around you might have been struggling.
>
> - **Situation:** Describe the context. Where were you? What was happening?
> - **Task:** What did you feel was needed in that moment?
> - **Action:** What did you do (or what do you wish you had done)?
> - **Result:** What happened as a result? If you could go back, what would you do differently based on what you learned today?

> **Prompt Option 2:**
>
> Imagine a peer comes to you and shares something that concerns you about their safety.
>
> - **Situation:** Describe the scenario you are imagining.
> - **Task:** Based on what we discussed today, what is your role in that moment?
> - **Action:** Walk through the specific steps you would take, including what you would say and who you would contact.
> - **Result:** What outcome are you hoping for? What would success look like in that situation?

> **Prompt Option 3:**
>
> Reflect on today's session and your own readiness to support a peer in crisis.
>
> - **Situation:** Describe where you are right now in your comfort level with this topic.
> - **Task:** What do you feel you still need in order to be prepared?
> - **Action:** What is one specific step you will take this week to build your readiness (review resources, talk to a trusted adult, practice a conversation)?
> - **Result:** How will taking that step change your ability to show up for your peers?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Index Card Check (completed during STAR Reflection above)

Review collected index cards after the session to assess whether participants demonstrate:

- [ ] Ability to identify at least three crisis warning signs
- [ ] Clear understanding that their role is to connect, not counsel
- [ ] Knowledge of at least two crisis resources (one local, one national)
- [ ] Awareness of what mandated reporting is and why it exists

### Verbal Check -- "Fist to Five"

Before closing, ask participants to rate their confidence on a 0-5 scale (fist = 0, five fingers = 5) for each statement:

1. "I can recognize warning signs that a peer may be in crisis."
2. "I understand what a peer supporter does and does NOT do in a crisis."
3. "I know how to connect someone to professional help."
4. "I know where to find crisis resources for myself or a peer."

Facilitator notes any areas where the group averages below 3 and plans follow-up reinforcement in subsequent sessions.

## Community Partner Integration

### Why This Module Requires Community Partners

Module 11 is the most critical point for community partner involvement in the curriculum. Crisis awareness cannot exist in a vacuum -- participants need to know that real, local support systems exist and are accessible to them. Abstract knowledge of crisis resources is insufficient; participants need tangible connections.

### Recommended Community Partner Roles for This Module

| Partner Type | Role in This Module |
|-------------|-------------------|
| **Local Mental Health Agency** | Guest speaker or on-site support during session; provide referral pathway information for Handout 11-E; serve as follow-up resource for participants who need support after the session |
| **Crisis Hotline Representative** | Brief presentation on how hotlines work; demystify the process; answer participant questions about confidentiality and what to expect when calling |
| **School Counselor or Social Worker** | On-site emotional support during session; available for immediate follow-up if disclosures occur; help facilitate the mandated reporting segment with real-world context |
| **Youth-Serving Nonprofit (e.g., YMCA, Boys & Girls Club)** | Provide ongoing safe spaces for peer support outside of session; connect participants to mentoring and wellness programs |
| **Community Health Worker** | Bridge between program and family/community resources; support culturally responsive referral pathways |

### Integration Points During the Session

1. **Activity 1 (Crisis Warning Signs):** A mental health professional can co-facilitate, adding clinical context to participant-generated lists while keeping language accessible.
2. **Activity 3 (Making the Connection):** A crisis hotline representative can walk participants through what actually happens when you call a crisis line, reducing fear and stigma.
3. **Activity 4 (Mandated Reporting):** A school counselor or social worker can share (in age-appropriate terms) what the reporting process looks like from the professional's perspective.
4. **Activity 5 (Safety Planning):** A licensed counselor can briefly explain how safety plans are developed and answer questions.
5. **Closing and Resource Distribution:** Community partners distribute their own resource cards and make themselves available for one-on-one conversations after the session.

### Post-Session Follow-Up

- Schedule a follow-up check-in with community partners within one week of this session to review any concerns, disclosures, or referrals that emerged.
- Ensure that all participants have at least one direct point of contact (name and number) for a local crisis resource -- not just a generic hotline number.
- Community partners should be informed in advance about the session content so they can prepare appropriately for potential disclosures or emotional responses.
