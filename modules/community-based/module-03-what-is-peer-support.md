# Module 3: What is Peer Support?

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Define peer support in their own words and identify at least three core principles that guide it.
2. Distinguish the role of a peer supporter from other helping roles (e.g., counselor, therapist, friend, mentor) by describing at least two differences for each.
3. Identify appropriate and inappropriate examples of peer support when presented with real-world scenarios.
4. Articulate at least two personal strengths or experiences that equip them to offer peer support.
5. Recognize initial boundary considerations that shape the peer support relationship.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- NOT FOR PARTICIPANT DISTRIBUTION**

### Conceptual Foundation

This module is the conceptual cornerstone of the curriculum. It establishes the working definition of peer support that all subsequent modules build upon. The session design draws on the **Harrington framework** for peer support, which emphasizes mutuality, shared lived experience, self-determination, and a non-hierarchical relationship between supporter and supported. Facilitators should internalize these four pillars because they recur throughout later modules (especially Modules 4, 5, 9, and 11).

### Standards Alignment

This module aligns with the following **National AfterSchool Partnership Standards (NAPS)**:

- **Standard 2: Relationships** -- Creating environments where young people experience supportive relationships with peers and adults.
- **Standard 4: Youth Participation** -- Engaging young people as active contributors rather than passive recipients.
- **Standard 6: Community and Family Engagement** -- Connecting program content to the broader community context in which participants live.

The Harrington framework's emphasis on mutuality and non-hierarchy directly supports NAPS Standard 4 by positioning participants as co-creators of knowledge rather than students receiving instruction.

### Key Facilitation Considerations

- **Language matters.** When participants attempt definitions, affirm contributions and gently redirect language that drifts toward clinical or hierarchical framing. Peer support is distinct because it is rooted in shared experience, not professional credentials.
- **Boundary introduction is light here.** Module 9 (Setting Boundaries) covers boundaries in depth. In this module, introduce the concept just enough so participants understand that peer support has limits, without going deep into boundary-setting skills.
- **Scenario calibration.** Review the "Is This Peer Support?" scenarios before the session. Adjust any that do not resonate with the specific community context of your participant group. Scenarios should feel plausible and relevant to participants' daily lives.
- **Watch for over-identification.** Some participants may recognize themselves in scenarios depicting someone who needs support. Be prepared to gently hold space if emotional reactions surface, and have referral information available.

## Activities

### Activity 1: Defining Peer Support Together

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Full group brainstorm followed by structured synthesis
**Materials:** Easel pad, markers (3-4 colors), sticky notes (one color), pens, Participant Handout 3A

**Instructions:**

1. **(3 minutes)** Open with the prompt written on the easel pad: *"What does support from a peer look like?"* Ask participants to think silently for 60 seconds, then write one idea per sticky note (2-3 notes each).

2. **(5 minutes)** Invite participants to post their sticky notes on the easel pad. As they post, ask a few volunteers to read theirs aloud. Facilitator clusters similar ideas together in real time, narrating the emerging themes: *"I'm noticing several of you mentioned listening. And these over here are about not judging someone. Let's keep going."*

3. **(5 minutes)** Facilitate a brief discussion drawing out core principles:
   - *"What do these clusters have in common?"*
   - *"What makes support from a peer different from support from an adult professional?"*
   - *"Does the person giving support need to have gone through the same thing? Why or why not?"*

4. **(4 minutes)** Using the group's language, co-construct a working definition on the easel pad. Write it in a color that stands out. A sample scaffold if the group needs help:

   > "Peer support is when someone who has been through similar experiences offers understanding, encouragement, and practical help to someone else -- as an equal, not an expert."

5. **(3 minutes)** Distribute **Handout 3A** and ask participants to write the group's definition in the space provided at the top. Then review the four core principles listed on the handout, connecting each one back to the sticky notes already on the wall.

**Facilitator Tip:** Resist the urge to provide the "correct" definition first. The learning lives in the co-construction. If critical principles are missing from the group's version, weave them in through follow-up questions rather than direct correction.

### Activity 2: Role Comparison -- Who Does What?

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Think-pair-share followed by full-group chart completion
**Materials:** Participant Handout 3B, pens, easel pad with pre-drawn comparison chart

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 minutes)** Distribute **Handout 3B**. Introduce the activity: *"We've defined peer support. Now let's get clearer on what makes a peer supporter different from other people in a helping role. We're going to compare three roles: a peer supporter, a professional counselor or therapist, and a friend."*

2. **(5 minutes -- Think-Pair)** Ask participants to work in pairs. Each pair should discuss and fill in at least two characteristics for each column on Handout 3B. Circulate and listen for strong examples to lift up during the full-group share.

3. **(8 minutes -- Share)** Bring the group back together. Using the easel pad chart, fill in each column by soliciting contributions from pairs. Probe for clarity:
   - *"Can a friend also be a peer supporter? What would have to change?"*
   - *"What can a counselor do that a peer supporter should not do?"*
   - *"What can a peer supporter do that a counselor cannot?"*

4. **(5 minutes)** Facilitate a closing discussion about what sits in the overlapping space and what is unique to each role. Key takeaways to surface:
   - A peer supporter shares experience; a counselor provides clinical expertise. Both matter.
   - A friend has personal investment in the relationship; a peer supporter maintains a different kind of care that includes intentional boundaries.
   - Peer support is a *role*, not just a personality trait. It can be learned and practiced.

**Facilitator Tip:** Participants sometimes worry that peer support sounds like "being a therapist without a license." Name this concern directly: *"Peer support is not therapy. It is its own thing, and it is powerful precisely because it is different."*

### Activity 3: "Is This Peer Support?" Scenarios

**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Format:** Small groups (4-5 per group) with scenario cards, followed by full-group debrief
**Materials:** Participant Handout 3C (pre-cut scenario cards, one set per group), sticky notes (second color), pens

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 minutes)** Organize participants into small groups of 4-5. Distribute one set of scenario cards (Handout 3C) and a stack of sticky notes (second color) to each group.

2. **(10 minutes -- Small Group Work)** Each group reads through their six scenario cards. For each scenario, the group must:
   - Decide together: **Yes, this is peer support** / **No, this is not peer support** / **It depends**
   - Write their reasoning on a sticky note and attach it to the card
   - If they say "It depends," they must identify what would need to change to make it peer support

3. **(8 minutes -- Debrief)** Bring all groups back. Select 3-4 scenarios that generated the most discussion or disagreement. For each:
   - Ask the group to share their verdict and reasoning
   - Invite other groups to agree, disagree, or add nuance
   - Highlight where boundaries show up: *"Notice how in this scenario, the peer supporter is doing something that really belongs to a counselor. That's a boundary we'll explore much more in a later session."*

**Facilitator Tip:** The "It depends" option is intentionally included. Real-world peer support is rarely black and white. Celebrate groups that engage with the gray areas -- that is where the deepest learning happens.

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use the following prompts for the guided reflection segment (10 minutes). Participants may respond in writing on the back of any handout, in a journal, or through a brief pair-share.

**Prompt 1 -- Receiving Peer Support:**

- **Situation:** Think of a time when you were going through something difficult and a peer (not a professional, not a parent -- someone your age or close to it) helped you feel supported.
- **Task:** What did you need in that moment? What kind of support were you looking for?
- **Action:** What did that peer actually do or say that made a difference?
- **Result:** How did their support affect how you felt or what you did next? What made it work?

**Prompt 2 -- Offering Peer Support:**

- **Situation:** Think of a time when you noticed someone around you was struggling and you stepped in to help, even in a small way.
- **Task:** What did the situation call for? What did you think the person needed?
- **Action:** What did you do? How did you approach them? What did you say?
- **Result:** What happened afterward? Looking back, what would you keep the same, and what might you do differently knowing what you now know about peer support?

**Prompt 3 -- Looking Ahead (optional, for groups with extra time):**

- **Situation:** Imagine you are six months from now and you have been practicing peer support in your community.
- **Task:** Someone you know comes to you because they trust you. They are dealing with a challenge.
- **Action:** Based on what you learned today, how do you show up for them? What principles guide your response?
- **Result:** What do you hope the outcome looks like -- for them and for you?

### Facilitator Review Guidance

After collecting exit tickets, look for:

- **Definitions** that include at least two of the four core principles (shared experience, mutuality, respect/equality, self-determination)
- **Role distinction** responses that show clear differentiation rather than overlapping descriptions
- **Confidence ratings** below 3 as signals to revisit foundational concepts in Module 4 opening
- **Questions** that can be addressed in the opening of the next session or posted on a "Parking Lot" board

## Community Partner Integration

### Option A: Guest Introduction (Recommended)

Invite a representative from a local peer support organization, youth mentoring program, or community wellness initiative to join the final 5 minutes of the session. The guest:

- Briefly introduces themselves and their organization (1-2 minutes)
- Shares one example of peer support in action in the local community (1-2 minutes)
- Lets participants know how to connect with them or their organization (1 minute)

**Suggested partners:** community mental health centers that employ peer support specialists, campus or school-based wellness programs, youth-led advocacy organizations, faith-based mentoring programs, community health worker networks.

### Option B: Community Resource Spotlight

If a guest cannot attend in person, the facilitator:

- Displays or distributes a one-page community resource card listing 3-5 local organizations where peer support is practiced
- Briefly describes one organization and how its model reflects the principles discussed in the session
- Asks participants: *"Where else in your community do you see peer support happening, even if it is not called that?"*

### Option C: Community Mapping Preview

As a bridge to Module 12 (Building Community):

- Post a large sheet of paper or whiteboard labeled "Peer Support in Our Community"
- Invite participants to add sticky notes with places, people, or groups in their community where peer support happens informally or formally
- Leave the map posted for future sessions to build upon

### Integration Notes for Facilitators

- Coordinate with community partners at least two weeks before the session
- Provide partners with a brief overview of the session content and the community-based setting guidelines
- Ask partners to use accessible language and avoid clinical jargon
- Ensure any materials from partners are reviewed for consistency with program values
