# Module 10: Self-Care

**Edition:** Community-Based | **Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. Identify the multiple dimensions of self-care (physical, emotional, social, spiritual, professional, and psychological).
2. Recognize early warning signs of burnout in themselves and their peers.
3. Distinguish between reactive coping and proactive self-care practices.
4. Create a personalized, actionable self-care plan that addresses at least three dimensions.
5. Articulate why consistent self-care is a professional responsibility rather than an indulgence, especially for those in peer support roles.

## Facilitator Preparation Notes

> **INTERNAL -- NOT FOR PARTICIPANT DISTRIBUTION**

### Framework Alignment

This module aligns with the **SCBF 2.0** framework's emphasis on sustaining the well-being of peer supporters as a precondition for effective community engagement. The session draws on the **Harrington Model** of holistic youth development, particularly the principle that helpers must maintain their own capacity in order to serve others authentically. **NAPS Standard 4 (Health and Safety)** and **NAPS Standard 6 (Staffing and Professional Development)** are directly addressed through the self-care planning components.

### Pedagogical Notes

- This module is designed as a companion to **Module 9: Boundaries**. If delivered in sequence, open by briefly referencing boundary-setting as one form of self-care before expanding the concept.
- Self-care content can surface difficult emotions. Be prepared for disclosures related to stress, anxiety, family obligations, or trauma. Have referral resources available and know your local crisis protocols.
- Normalize vulnerability. Model openness by sharing (at an appropriate level) your own self-care challenges as a facilitator.
- The session is intentionally expanded from a 45-minute core to 90 minutes. The additional time allows for deeper individual reflection, peer sharing, and thorough plan development. Do not rush the planning activity; it is the anchor of the session.
- Emphasize throughout: self-care is not selfish. For peer supporters, it is a professional and ethical responsibility. A depleted helper cannot hold space for others.

### Key Terminology

| Term | Working Definition |
|------|--------------------|
| Self-care | Intentional actions taken to maintain or improve one's physical, emotional, and mental health. |
| Burnout | A state of chronic stress leading to physical and emotional exhaustion, cynicism, and feelings of ineffectiveness. |
| Compassion fatigue | The gradual erosion of empathy and hope that can affect those in helping roles. |
| Proactive self-care | Planned, routine practices that build resilience before stress becomes overwhelming. |
| Reactive coping | Strategies used in response to a crisis or after stress has already accumulated. |

### Room Setup

Arrange seating in a circle or U-shape to promote open dialogue. Place handouts face-down at each seat before participants arrive. Have the Self-Care Wheel diagram drawn on the whiteboard or projected before the session begins.

## Activities

### Opening & Check-In (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Establish a safe, reflective tone for the session.

1. Welcome participants. Briefly review group agreements (confidentiality, respect, right to pass).
2. **One-Word Check-In:** Go around the circle. Ask each participant to share one word that describes how they are feeling right now. No elaboration needed -- just the word.
3. Transition: *"Today we are going to talk about something that is easy to overlook but absolutely essential -- especially for people who show up for others the way you do. We are talking about self-care. And I want to be clear from the start: self-care is not a luxury. It is a responsibility. You cannot pour from an empty cup."*
4. If Module 9 (Boundaries) was delivered previously, bridge: *"Last session we talked about setting boundaries to protect our energy. Today we go deeper: how do we actively refill that energy? How do we take care of ourselves on purpose, not just when we are already running on empty?"*

---

### Exploring Self-Care Dimensions (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build a shared understanding of self-care as multi-dimensional.

1. Ask the group: *"When you hear 'self-care,' what comes to mind?"* Capture responses on the whiteboard. Expect answers like bubble baths, sleep, exercise, etc.
2. Affirm their answers, then expand: *"All of those count. But self-care is bigger than any single activity. It has multiple dimensions -- areas of your life that each need attention."*
3. Introduce the **six dimensions of self-care** by drawing or revealing them on the whiteboard:

| Dimension | Examples |
|-----------|----------|
| **Physical** | Sleep, nutrition, exercise, medical check-ups |
| **Emotional** | Journaling, therapy, crying, creative expression |
| **Social** | Quality time with friends, setting social boundaries, community involvement |
| **Spiritual** | Meditation, prayer, time in nature, reflection on purpose and values |
| **Professional / Academic** | Time management, saying no to overcommitment, mentorship, skill-building |
| **Psychological** | Learning new things, mindfulness, reducing screen time, self-reflection |

4. Briefly discuss: *"Which of these dimensions do people tend to forget about?"* (Common answers: spiritual, psychological, professional.)
5. Transition to Activity 1: *"Let's take an honest look at how you are doing across all six of these areas right now."*

### Activity 2: Burnout Warning Signs Checklist (15 minutes)

**Purpose:** Help participants identify early warning signs of burnout so they can intervene before reaching a crisis point.

**Instructions for Facilitator:**
1. Transition: *"Now that we have a picture of where our self-care stands, let's talk about what happens when self-care breaks down over time. The word for that is burnout."*
2. Ask: *"Has anyone ever felt completely drained -- not just tired from one day, but exhausted in a way that sleep doesn't fix?"* Validate responses.
3. Explain: *"Burnout doesn't happen overnight. It builds up slowly, and it sends warning signs along the way. The tricky part is that we often don't notice them until we're already deep in it. This checklist is designed to help you catch those signs early."*
4. Distribute **Handout 10-B: Burnout Warning Signs Checklist**.
5. Allow 5-6 minutes for individual completion.
6. Form groups of 3-4. Give groups 5 minutes to discuss using the group discussion prompts on the handout.
7. Bring the full group back together. Ask each small group to share one key takeaway. Reinforce: *"Recognizing burnout early is itself an act of self-care."*

---

#### Handout 10-B: Burnout Warning Signs Checklist

**Instructions:** Read each statement below. Check the box if you have experienced this in the **past two weeks**. Be honest -- this is for your eyes only.

**Physical Warning Signs**
- [ ] I feel tired even after a full night of sleep.
- [ ] I have frequent headaches, stomachaches, or muscle tension.
- [ ] My eating habits have changed significantly (eating much more or much less).
- [ ] I am getting sick more often than usual.
- [ ] I have trouble falling asleep or staying asleep.

**Emotional Warning Signs**
- [ ] I feel overwhelmed by tasks that used to feel manageable.
- [ ] I am more irritable, impatient, or short-tempered than usual.
- [ ] I feel numb, detached, or "going through the motions."
- [ ] I feel like nothing I do makes a difference.
- [ ] I dread activities or responsibilities I used to enjoy.

**Social Warning Signs**
- [ ] I am withdrawing from friends, family, or my community.
- [ ] I avoid answering messages or phone calls.
- [ ] I feel resentful when people ask me for help.
- [ ] I compare myself negatively to others more often.
- [ ] I feel isolated even when I am around people.

**Behavioral Warning Signs**
- [ ] I am procrastinating more than usual.
- [ ] I have stopped doing hobbies or activities I used to enjoy.
- [ ] I am relying more on unhealthy coping (junk food, excessive screen time, substances).
- [ ] I find it hard to concentrate or make decisions.
- [ ] I have been skipping commitments or responsibilities.

**Scoring Guide:**
- **0-4 checks:** You are managing well. Keep building your self-care practices.
- **5-9 checks:** Caution zone. Some warning signs are present. Review your self-care wheel for gaps.
- **10-14 checks:** Alert zone. Burnout is building. Take action this week -- identify one change you can make immediately.
- **15+ checks:** Urgent. Please talk to a trusted adult, mentor, counselor, or support person. You deserve help.

**My total checks:** ______

**Group Discussion Prompts:**
1. Without sharing your score, what is one warning sign you were surprised to see on the list?
2. What makes it hard to notice burnout when it is happening to you?
3. What is one thing a friend could say or do that would help you recognize you are burning out?

### Movement Break (5 minutes)

**Purpose:** Model self-care in real time by pausing for physical and mental reset.

Lead participants through one of the following (choose based on group energy):

**Option A -- Box Breathing:**
- Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts, exhale for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts.
- Repeat 4 cycles.

**Option B -- Guided Stretch:**
- Stand up. Reach arms overhead. Stretch to the left, hold 10 seconds. Stretch to the right, hold 10 seconds.
- Roll shoulders forward 5 times, backward 5 times.
- Shake out hands and arms vigorously for 10 seconds.

**Option C -- Grounding Exercise (5-4-3-2-1):**
- Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste.

After the break: *"That right there -- that was self-care. It took three minutes. Self-care doesn't have to be expensive or time-consuming. It can be small, intentional pauses like that one."*

### Activity 3: Personal Self-Care Plan (20 minutes)

**Purpose:** Translate awareness into action by building a concrete, personalized self-care plan.

**Instructions for Facilitator:**
1. Transition: *"You've mapped your self-care wheel. You've checked for burnout warning signs. Now it's time to build a plan -- something realistic, something you'll actually do."*
2. Distribute **Handout 10-C: My Personal Self-Care Plan**.
3. Emphasize these planning principles before they begin:
   - **Specific beats vague.** "Exercise more" is vague. "Walk for 20 minutes on Tuesdays and Thursdays after school" is specific.
   - **Small beats big.** A plan you'll actually follow is better than an ambitious plan you'll abandon.
   - **At least three dimensions.** Reference your wheel -- target the areas where you scored lowest.
   - **Name your barriers.** If you know what will get in the way, you can plan around it.
4. Allow 12-15 minutes for individual work. Circulate and offer encouragement. Help participants who are stuck by asking: *"What's one small thing that makes you feel better when you're stressed?"*
5. Reserve the final 5 minutes for 2-3 volunteers to share one item from their plan with the group.

### Peer Accountability Partners (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Build mutual support structures that extend self-care beyond the session.

**Instructions for Facilitator:**
1. Ask participants to pair up with someone they feel comfortable checking in with.
2. Each partner shares:
   - One item from their self-care plan they are committing to this week.
   - One burnout warning sign they will watch for in themselves.
   - How they would like their partner to check in (text, call, in-person, etc.).
3. Partners exchange contact information or agree on a check-in method.
4. Bring the group back together. Ask: *"How does it feel to know someone is going to check on you this week?"*
5. Reinforce: *"Accountability is not about pressure. It is about care. You are giving someone permission to care about your well-being."*

## STAR Reflection Prompts

Use the following prompts during the closing reflection. Participants may respond verbally in the circle, in writing on the back of their handout, or silently to themselves.

### Prompt 1: Reflecting on Today's Session

- **Situation:** Think about arriving at today's session and your state of mind at the beginning.
- **Task:** You were asked to honestly assess your self-care and identify areas that need attention.
- **Action:** What did you do during this session -- what stood out to you? What did you write, discuss, or realize?
- **Result:** What is one thing you are walking away with today that you did not have when you walked in?

### Prompt 2: Applying Self-Care as a Peer Supporter

- **Situation:** Imagine a week where several people in your community come to you for support, and you begin to feel drained.
- **Task:** You need to continue showing up for others while also taking care of yourself.
- **Action:** Using what you learned today, what specific steps would you take to care for yourself during that week?
- **Result:** How would taking those steps affect your ability to support others? How would it affect how you feel about yourself?

### Prompt 3: Recognizing Burnout in a Peer

- **Situation:** A close friend who is also a peer supporter has been canceling plans, seems irritable, and told you they feel like nothing they do matters.
- **Task:** You want to check in with them without being pushy or judgmental.
- **Action:** What would you say or do? How would you bring up what you have noticed?
- **Result:** What outcome would you hope for? What would make the conversation successful?

## Assessment / Check for Understanding

### Formative Assessment (During Session)

- **Self-Care Wheel Completion:** Verify that participants rated all six dimensions and answered all reflection questions. Look for thoughtful, specific responses rather than single-word answers.
- **Burnout Checklist Engagement:** During small-group discussion, listen for evidence that participants can distinguish between normal tiredness and early burnout warning signs.
- **Plan Specificity:** While circulating during Activity 3, check that self-care plan entries are specific and actionable (e.g., "Walk for 15 minutes Monday, Wednesday, Friday" rather than "exercise more").

### Summative Assessment

**Exit Ticket -- Three Quick Questions** (collected at the door or completed verbally in the closing circle):

1. **Name two dimensions of self-care** and give one example of each.
   - *Proficient response:* Correctly names two of the six dimensions with relevant, specific examples.

2. **What is one early warning sign of burnout** you will now watch for in yourself?
   - *Proficient response:* Identifies a specific, personal warning sign (not a generic answer).

3. **What is the difference between proactive self-care and reactive coping?**
   - *Proficient response:* Demonstrates understanding that proactive self-care is planned and ongoing, while reactive coping happens after stress has already built up.

### Mastery Indicators

| Indicator | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| Identifies self-care dimensions | Accurately names and gives examples for at least 4 of 6 dimensions |
| Recognizes burnout warning signs | Identifies at least 3 warning signs and explains why early recognition matters |
| Creates actionable self-care plan | Plan addresses at least 3 dimensions with specific, time-bound actions and identified barriers |
| Understands self-care as professional responsibility | Articulates why peer supporters must prioritize self-care to be effective in their roles |

## Community Partner Integration

### Pre-Session Integration

- **Local wellness organizations** (yoga studios, community gardens, recreation centers): Invite a representative to attend the session and share free or low-cost self-care resources available to participants. Provide them with the session outline in advance so they can tailor their contribution to the self-care dimensions.
- **School counselors or community mental health providers:** Coordinate before the session to have referral cards or resource lists available in case participants disclose high levels of burnout or distress.

### During-Session Integration

- **Guest speaker (10 minutes, can be embedded in the "Exploring Self-Care Dimensions" segment):** Invite a community health worker, counselor, or wellness practitioner to speak briefly about one dimension of self-care. Ask them to share a personal story about a time self-care made a difference in their professional life.
- **Resource table:** If space allows, set up a resource table with brochures, flyers, and contact information from community partners offering free wellness services (counseling hotlines, peer support groups, fitness programs, meditation apps with free tiers).

### Post-Session Integration

- **Community self-care challenge:** Partner with a local organization (library, recreation center, faith-based organization) to host a one-week self-care challenge. Participants log their daily self-care actions and check in at the partner site at the end of the week.
- **Follow-up workshops:** Connect with community mental health providers to offer an optional deeper-dive workshop on stress management, mindfulness, or compassion fatigue for participants who want to continue building their skills.
- **Accountability partner check-ins:** Encourage accountability partners to meet at a community partner site (coffee shop, park, library) for their check-ins, reinforcing the connection between self-care and community.
