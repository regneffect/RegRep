# Braiding + Crowdsourcing Architecture: CMU/CCRB Compound Advantage

**Layer**: Sits on top of SUD Lane Dominance Report + Audit Findings
**Core Argument**: Braiding creates the structural advantage. Crowdsourcing creates the adaptive advantage. Together they compound — so that after 24 months, no competitor can replicate what CMU has built without rebuilding the entire infrastructure from scratch.

---

## Part One: Five Dimensions of Braiding

### Dimension 1: Funding Braids

Every grant amplifies every other grant. The architecture is designed so no single funding stream stands alone — each one becomes the load-bearing wall for the next.

| Entry Point | What It Unlocks | Amplification Chain |
|---|---|---|
| CASE for Kids ($5,000) | School-based screening presence at CUPs sites; establishes CMU as embedded prevention partner in HISD-adjacent juvenile programming | Creates the referral pipeline that feeds OAFC enrollment targets (50 youth/year); generates literacy screening data (WRAT-5/TABE) that justifies education-linked recovery narrative for OJJDP |
| OAFC CCRB ($749,565 over 3 years) | 90-day stabilization + 12-month YRC continuing care; 4.0 FTE direct service infrastructure; naloxone distribution; MOUD linkage pathway | Funds the operational spine that Hogg capacity-building wraps around; produces the outcome data UTHealth needs for evaluation; creates the workforce pipeline (RSPS apprenticeships at 18+) that becomes a sustainability argument for every future funder |
| Hogg Foundation [NEEDED: specific initiative] | Supervision system build; training infrastructure; QA/fidelity scaffolding; data system architecture | Builds the durable capacity layer that makes OAFC delivery replicable and auditable; creates the SOPs, manuals, and practice standards that become intellectual property |
| OJJDP SCA Youth Offender Reentry Program, Category 2 [NEEDED: FY/solicitation number] | Federal evaluation rigor; prerelease case planning (90+ days pre-release); justice-outcome measurement (recidivism, reentry success); post-release case management expansion; JustGrants reporting infrastructure; MOA/MOU with HCJPD for facility access and shared data | Adds federal-grade accountability that elevates the entire program's credibility; SCA Category 2 requirements (community-based reentry partnerships, comprehensive pre/post-release case management, validated risk/needs assessment, evidence-based programming) align directly with CCRB's existing consortium model, UTHealth evaluation partnership, and 90-day stabilization structure |
| UTHealth Evaluation Partnership | Independent evaluation pipeline; quasi-experimental design capacity; IRB infrastructure | Produces the publishable outcomes that attract future funding; satisfies OJJDP's requirement for "rigorous quasi-experimental studies with matched comparison groups" |

**Amplification Logic**: The $5,000 CASE for Kids grant is not a $5,000 program. It is the screening entry point for a system backed by $749,565 in OAFC funding, a UTHealth evaluation pipeline, and (once secured) federal and philanthropic capacity layers. Each dollar spent at the entry point is leveraged by every dollar behind it.

### Dimension 2: Credential Braids

Each of the three Credible Messenger layers maps to a specific funder, billing category, evidence-based framework, and site deployment. One practitioner, one interaction, three funded functions.

| CM Layer | Practitioner | Funder / Billing Category | Evidence-Based Framework | Site Deployment | Documentation Artifact |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Layer 1: Recovery Coaching + Stabilization** | RSPS (CMU) | OAFC / CO03 (90-Day Stabilization Delivery) | MET/CBT + Contingency Management | CUPs-based; court accompaniment; school navigation | Session logs; fidelity checklist; contact logs coded to CO03 |
| **Layer 2: MOUD Navigation + A-CRA Delivery** | RSPS (SRS) | OAFC / CO02 (MOUD Linkage) + CO03 | A-CRA-informed supports + telehealth MOUD assessment pathway | Telehealth-enabled; SRS clinical network; ages 16+ | Referral log; appointment confirmation; MOUD adherence monitoring; coded to CO02/CO03 |
| **Layer 3: Family Stabilization + Harm Reduction** | Certified Family Partner (SRS) | OAFC / CO04 (CFP Supports) + CO05 (Naloxone) | Fentanyl/naloxone education; home-based family navigation | Home visits; parent groups; benefits/housing/school advocacy | Home visit logs; education sign-ins; distribution logs; coded to CO04/CO05 |

**Credential Braid Logic**: When a CMU Recovery Specialist sits with a youth at a CUPs site, that single interaction simultaneously: (a) delivers OAFC-funded stabilization coaching (CO03), (b) screens for MOUD eligibility that activates the SRS clinical pathway (CO02), and (c) triggers Certified Family Partner engagement (CO04). One touch, three funded functions, three documentation streams, zero duplication — because each function maps to a distinct cost objective with distinct proof artifacts.

### Dimension 3: Framework Braids

Four evidence-based frameworks weave into a single six-phase continuum logic model. No framework operates in isolation.

| Phase | SBIRT | CDC ENGAGE | RCAM | Restorative Justice | Integrated Output |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Screening & Identification** (Days 1-7) | Universal SUD screening; OUD risk stratification; literacy screen (WRAT-5/TABE) | Community engagement principles guide how screening is introduced to youth/family | Risk-informed assessment identifies recovery capital baseline | — | Comprehensive intake that captures clinical, educational, relational, and community dimensions in one event |
| **2. Brief Intervention & Stabilization** (Days 8-30) | Brief intervention for substance use; motivational enhancement | Engagement strategies for building trust with justice-involved youth | Recovery capital development begins (housing, education, family, social supports) | Restorative circles for accountability and relationship repair with family/community | Stabilization plan that addresses substance use, relationships, and community belonging simultaneously |
| **3. Referral to Treatment & Navigation** (Days 31-60) | Referral to MOUD assessment (16+); treatment initiation tracking | Partner coordination across systems (court, school, clinical, community) | Active navigation to build recovery capital across all domains | Community conferencing to establish prosocial support network | Active recovery navigation where clinical treatment and community reintegration proceed in parallel |
| **4. Transition & Warm Handoff** (Days 61-90) | Treatment adherence monitoring; step-down assessment | Systems-level coordination for long-term placement | Recovery capital sufficiency assessment; gap analysis | Reentry planning with restorative accountability framework | 90-day completion that documents both clinical stabilization and community reintegration readiness |
| **5. Continuing Care** (Months 4-15) | Ongoing screening for relapse indicators | Sustained engagement through decreasing-intensity contacts | Recovery capital maintenance and growth tracking | Ongoing restorative community participation | YRC continuing care that maintains clinical gains while deepening community integration |
| **6. Workforce & Sustainability** (Months 12+) | — | Community-informed workforce development | Recovery capital as employment readiness framework | Restorative justice facilitation as a peer workforce skill | RSPS apprenticeship pathway (18+) where lived experience becomes professional credential |

**Framework Braid Logic**: A single youth encounter at Day 45 simultaneously advances SBIRT (treatment adherence check), CDC ENGAGE (trust-building contact), RCAM (recovery capital assessment update), and restorative justice (community accountability check-in). The frameworks are not layered sequentially — they are woven into every interaction.

### Dimension 4: Data Braids

Four-layer data architecture where staff enter data once and it serves every reporting obligation.

| Layer | System | What It Captures | Who Uses It | Reporting Destination |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Layer 1: Justice System** | JIMS2 (Harris County Juvenile Information Management System) | Court events; probation status; disposition; supervision compliance | HCJPD; court officers; CCRB intake staff | Court reporting; OJJDP justice outcome measures |
| **Layer 2: Program Operations** | CMU Recovery Tracker | Enrollment; screening results; contact logs; session notes; milestone completions; naloxone distribution; family engagement; YRC attendance | RSPS; Certified Family Partner; Program Director (LCDC); supervisors | OAFC quarterly reports; OAFC KPIs (Table 8/11); internal QA |
| **Layer 3: Evaluation** | UTHealth Evaluation Database | De-identified outcomes; matched comparison group data; pre/post measures; follow-up data | UTHealth evaluation team; Program Director for review | Published outcomes; OJJDP performance measurement; funder reports; sustainability narratives |
| **Layer 4: Grant Reporting Crosswalk** | Reporting Crosswalk Matrix (maintained by LCDC/Program Director) | Maps each data element to its funder-specific reporting field; tracks submission deadlines; flags duplicative reporting risks | Program Director; fiscal staff; compliance oversight | OAFC GMS submissions; OJJDP JustGrants; Hogg deliverable reports; SF-425 financial reports |

**Data Braid Logic**: When an RSPS logs a contact in the CMU Recovery Tracker, that single entry: (a) updates the youth's OAFC milestone progress (Layer 2 -> Layer 4 -> OAFC quarterly report), (b) feeds the UTHealth evaluation dataset (Layer 2 -> Layer 3), (c) connects to the youth's JIMS2 record for justice outcome tracking (Layer 1 -> Layer 3 -> OJJDP performance measures), and (d) populates the grant reporting crosswalk so the Program Director can verify no duplicative billing across funders. One entry, four layers, zero re-entry.

### Dimension 5: Site Braids

How a youth flows through the entire system — one continuum, one data record, multiple funded touchpoints.

```
School-Based Screening (CASE for Kids / CUPs site)
    |
    v
Court / Probation Referral (HCJPD / Harris County juvenile court)
    |
    v
CCRB Intake & Consent (CUPs-based; RSPS conducts screening battery)
    |
    v
[Age 16+ & suspected OUD?]
    |--- Yes ---> MOUD Clinical Assessment Linkage (SRS telehealth; 14-day target)
    |--- No ----> Non-MOUD stabilization track
    |
    v
90-Day Stabilization (CUPs-based delivery)
    |-- RSPS: MET/CBT + CM + recovery coaching (CUPs site)
    |-- Certified Family Partner: home visits + education (home-based)
    |-- Naloxone distribution (CUPs + home-based)
    |-- SCBF curriculum delivery (CUPs / juvenile probation)
    |
    v
Day 90 Completion + Transition Plan
    |
    v
YRC Continuing Care (community-based; up to 12 months)
    |
    v
Workforce Pathway (RSPS apprenticeship at 18+; JI-RPS training)
    |
    v
Cottage 6 Community Reintegration (long-term community recovery supports)
```

**Site Braid Logic**: The youth's journey from school-based screening to Cottage 6 community reintegration is one continuous data record. At each site transition, the data follows — JIMS2 for justice status, CMU Recovery Tracker for program milestones, UTHealth for evaluation. No site handoff creates a data gap. No site transition requires re-intake. The youth is in one system, even as they move through multiple physical locations and multiple funded touchpoints.

## Part Two: Six Channels of Crowdsourcing

### Channel 1: Youth Design Labs

**Purpose**: Co-design of SCBF 2.0, screening experience, and recovery coaching engagement protocols.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Participants** | Current and former CCRB participants (ages 14-24); recruited through YRC continuing care and RSPS relationships |
| **Cadence** | Quarterly 2-hour design sessions; ongoing feedback loops through YRC meetings |
| **Outputs** | SCBF 2.0 curriculum revisions; screening experience redesign recommendations; peer engagement strategy updates; youth-validated outcome definitions |
| **Documentation** | Session notes; design artifacts (journey maps, prototypes); participant consent for contribution attribution; version-controlled curriculum updates |
| **Funder Alignment** | OAFC: youth engagement as program quality improvement; OJJDP: youth participatory action research for evidence-based program development; Hogg: capacity-building through community voice |

### Channel 2: Credible Messenger Practice Intelligence Network (CM-PIN)

**Purpose**: Formalizing POGA (Practice Observations, Guidance, and Adaptations) capture and Monthly Practice Rounds so practitioner wisdom becomes institutional knowledge.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Participants** | All RSPS and Certified Family Partner staff; LCDC as facilitator; Peer Supervisor as curator |
| **Cadence** | Weekly POGA submissions (5-minute structured capture); Monthly Practice Rounds (90-minute facilitated session) |
| **Outputs** | POGA Library (searchable practice knowledge base); practice pattern reports; fidelity adaptation recommendations; training curriculum updates |
| **Documentation** | Structured POGA forms (situation, action, outcome, adaptation rationale); Monthly Practice Rounds minutes; practice pattern analysis; version-controlled updates to SOPs and training materials |
| **Funder Alignment** | Hogg: supervision and QA system build (CO08); OAFC: practice improvement feeding KPI attainment; OJJDP: evidence of continuous quality improvement for performance reporting |

### Channel 3: Family Recovery Advisory Circle (FRAC)

**Purpose**: Co-designing repair conferencing protocols and caregiver support programming.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Participants** | Family members of current/former CCRB participants; Certified Family Partner as liaison; recruited through home visits and parent groups |
| **Cadence** | Bi-monthly 90-minute sessions; ongoing input through Certified Family Partner home visits |
| **Outputs** | Repair conferencing protocol refinements; caregiver support resource guides; family engagement strategy updates; family-validated definitions of recovery progress |
| **Documentation** | Session notes; protocol revision drafts; family feedback summaries; consent for contribution attribution |
| **Funder Alignment** | OAFC: family engagement KPI (80% target) quality improvement; Hogg: family-support protocol development (capacity); OJJDP: family engagement as reentry best practice |

### Channel 4: Community Recovery Ecosystem Mapping (CREM)

**Purpose**: Asset walks and community conversations that map recovery-supporting resources across the 9-county benefit region.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Participants** | CMU staff; community members; recovery community organizations; local service providers; youth ambassadors |
| **Cadence** | Semi-annual asset walks (2 per year); quarterly community conversations; ongoing digital asset map updates |
| **Outputs** | Living asset map (physical + digital); referral pathway updates; gap analysis reports; community partnership leads; site-specific resource guides for RSPS navigation |
| **Documentation** | Asset walk reports with GPS/photo documentation; community conversation transcripts; asset map version history; referral pathway updates logged in CMU Recovery Tracker |
| **Funder Alignment** | OAFC: strengthening referral networks for stabilization/YRC; Hogg: community capacity assessment; OJJDP: community-based reentry resource mapping (Second Chance Act Category 1 requirement) |

### Channel 5: Systems Partner Feedback Loop (SPFL)

**Purpose**: Structured feedback from justice, education, health, and community partners.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Participants** | HCJPD staff; Harris County court officers; HISD liaisons; SRS clinical partners; community behavioral health providers; housing/employment partners |
| **Cadence** | Quarterly partner surveys (15-minute digital instrument); annual Systems Partner Roundtable (half-day) |
| **Outputs** | Partner satisfaction and coordination quality metrics; referral pathway efficiency data; system-level barrier identification; MOA/MOU effectiveness assessment; partner-recommended improvements |
| **Documentation** | Survey results with trend analysis; roundtable minutes and action items; partner feedback integrated into QA reports; MOA/MOU revision recommendations |
| **Funder Alignment** | OAFC: court coordination and systems navigation quality; OJJDP: evidence of collaboration with corrections, law enforcement, education, and health agencies (Second Chance Act Category 1 requirement); Hogg: partnership infrastructure development |

### Channel 6: Open-Source Curriculum Refinement (OSCR)

**Purpose**: Facilitator debriefs and participant experience surveys feeding the Claude Code versioning system for continuous curriculum improvement.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Participants** | SCBF facilitators; RSPS delivering MET/CBT/CM elements; A-CRA facilitators (SRS); participants providing experience feedback |
| **Cadence** | Post-session facilitator debriefs (structured 10-minute form after each delivery); quarterly participant experience surveys; monthly curriculum version reviews |
| **Outputs** | Facilitator debrief database; participant experience trends; curriculum version updates (tracked in Claude Code versioning system); adaptation rationale documentation; fidelity-adaptation balance reports |
| **Documentation** | Debrief forms (what worked, what adapted, why, outcome); participant survey results; curriculum change logs with version numbers; fidelity monitoring integration |
| **Funder Alignment** | OAFC: evidence-based treatment fidelity and quality improvement; Hogg: training system and curriculum development (CO08); OJJDP: evidence-based methodology documentation |

## Part Three: Convergence Map — Where Braiding Meets Crowdsourcing

Each crowdsourcing channel feeds a specific braiding dimension. This is where structural advantage and adaptive advantage compound.

| Crowdsourcing Channel | Primary Braiding Dimension Fed | Convergence Mechanism | Compound Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Youth Design Labs** | Framework Braids | Youth co-design validates and refines the six-phase continuum logic model; youth experience data tests whether SBIRT/ENGAGE/RCAM/RJ integration actually works as designed | Frameworks that are evidence-based AND youth-validated — a combination no competitor can claim without building both the clinical infrastructure and the participatory research pipeline |
| **CM Practice Intelligence Network** | Credential Braids | POGA capture and Practice Rounds surface which credential combinations (RSPS + framework + site) produce best outcomes; practitioner wisdom refines the three-layer CM deployment model | Credential deployment that improves with every interaction — institutional knowledge that compounds monthly and cannot be purchased or copied |
| **Family Recovery Advisory Circle** | Funding Braids | Family input shapes how Certified Family Partner services (CO04) are delivered, which directly affects the 80% family engagement KPI that justifies OAFC funding; family-validated repair conferencing protocols strengthen OJJDP reentry narratives | Funding justification that comes from the community, not just from grant writers — making renewal and expansion arguments backed by participant testimony |
| **Community Recovery Ecosystem Mapping** | Site Braids | Asset maps and community conversations identify where the school-to-CUPs-to-Cottage 6 continuum has gaps; new community resources get integrated into the referral pathway | Site continuum that adapts to community reality in real time — referral pathways that update semi-annually based on what actually exists, not what was assumed at grant writing |
| **Systems Partner Feedback Loop** | Data Braids | Partner surveys reveal where the four-layer data architecture (JIMS2/CMU Tracker/UTHealth/Crosswalk) has friction; partner feedback identifies reporting gaps before auditors do | Data architecture that self-corrects — system partners flag integration problems before they become compliance findings |
| **Open-Source Curriculum Refinement** | Framework Braids + Credential Braids | Facilitator debriefs and participant surveys test whether MET/CBT/CM/A-CRA delivery matches fidelity standards while remaining responsive to youth needs; version-controlled updates create an auditable improvement trail | Curricula that get better with every delivery cycle — and the improvement trail is documented, version-controlled, and attributable to specific practitioner and participant input |

**Convergence Logic**: Braiding without crowdsourcing creates a static system — well-funded but brittle. Crowdsourcing without braiding creates adaptive programs without structural support. The convergence creates a system that is both structurally sound (every dollar allocated, every credential mapped, every framework integrated, every data point routed, every site connected) and continuously adaptive (every youth, practitioner, family member, community asset, and systems partner contributing to improvement). After 24 months of compound operation, a competitor would need to simultaneously: (a) secure the same multi-stream funding architecture, (b) recruit and credential the same three-layer CM workforce, (c) integrate the same four evidence-based frameworks, (d) build the same four-layer data architecture, (e) establish the same multi-site continuum, AND (f) develop the same six participatory feedback channels with the same depth of institutional knowledge. That is not a 24-month task for a new entrant. It is a 48-month task, if achievable at all.

## Compound Advantage Summary

After 24 months, a competitor attempting to replicate CMU's position would need to simultaneously build:

| Dimension | What CMU Has After 24 Months | What a Competitor Would Need to Build |
|---|---|---|
| **Funding Braids** | 3-4 active, non-duplicative funding streams with documented cost allocation across 9 cost objectives | Secure multiple grants, build chart of accounts, establish audit-safe documentation — minimum 12-18 months |
| **Credential Braids** | Three CM layers with 24 months of documented delivery across distinct cost objectives and evidence-based frameworks | Recruit, credential, and deploy three specialized CM layers — minimum 12 months to operational |
| **Framework Braids** | Four integrated frameworks with 24 months of documented six-phase delivery and fidelity monitoring | Integrate SBIRT + ENGAGE + RCAM + RJ into operational delivery — minimum 6-12 months to pilot |
| **Data Braids** | Four-layer data architecture with 24 months of connected data across justice, program, evaluation, and reporting systems | Build JIMS2 integration, program tracker, evaluation pipeline, and reporting crosswalk — minimum 12 months |
| **Site Braids** | School-to-community continuum with established referral pathways, data continuity, and community partnerships | Establish presence at schools, courts, CUPs, and community sites with data connectivity — minimum 12-18 months |
| **Youth Design Labs** | 8+ co-design sessions; SCBF 2.0 youth-validated; screening redesigned | Cannot be accelerated — requires trust and relationship |
| **CM-PIN** | 150+ POGA entries; 24 Monthly Practice Rounds; practice guides published | Cannot be purchased — requires practitioner experience over time |
| **Family Advisory Circle** | Repair conferencing v2.0; caregiver protocols family-validated | Cannot be shortcut — requires family trust built through service |
| **Community Mapping** | Ecosystem Map v2.0 with 4 asset walks and 8 community conversations across 9 counties | Can be partially accelerated but lacks longitudinal depth |
| **Partner Feedback** | 2 annual roundtables; 8 quarterly surveys; MOA/MOU revisions driven by data | Requires established partnerships that take 12+ months to build |
| **Curriculum Refinement** | 3+ curriculum versions; facilitator debrief database; participant-validated adaptations | Cannot be replicated without the delivery volume and feedback loops |

**Total estimated replication time for a new entrant: 36-48 months minimum, assuming simultaneous pursuit of all dimensions. CMU's 24-month head start is effectively a permanent structural advantage in the Harris County juvenile recovery ecosystem.**
