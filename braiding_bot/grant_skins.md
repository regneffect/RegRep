### Recidivism Definition
Return to a residential placement facility, jail, or prison with a new juvenile adjudication and/or criminal conviction or as the result of a violation of the terms of supervision within **24 months** of initial release.

### Unallowable Uses (from NOFO)
- Programs violating federal immigration law (8 U.S.C. § 1373)
- Programs violating federal civil rights or nondiscrimination law
- Legal services for undocumented individuals (with limited exceptions)

### OJJDP Skin Paragraph

OJJDP funding under the FY25 Second Chance Act Youth Reentry Program (Category 2) will expand GSI's court-to-community reentry continuum by implementing evidence-based programming that bridges juvenile confinement to community recovery for moderate-to-high-risk youth in Harris County. Through a consortium model — GSI as fiscal agent, Credible Messengers United (CMU) for peer recovery delivery and Credible Messenger mentoring, and Strategic Recovery Solutions (SRS) for MOUD navigation and telehealth-enabled behavioral health services — this project will provide comprehensive pre- and post-release case management beginning at least 90 days prior to youth's release from HCJPD facilities. The project directly addresses the protection of American children by reducing recidivism through the Smart Choices Bright Futures (SCBF 2.0) evidence-based curriculum, validated risk/needs assessment tools, and an independent evaluation partnership with UTHealth Houston employing quasi-experimental design with random assignment to the maximum extent feasible.

### Compliance Notes

- Timekeeping: 2 CFR 200.430 (compensation — personal services)
- Reporting: Semi-annual performance reports via JustGrants (Jan-Jun due July 30; Jul-Dec due Jan 30); SF-425 financial reports

- OJP civil rights certification required (FY2025): recipient certifies no programs violate federal civil rights or nondiscrimination laws
- Youth includes emerging adults ages 18-25 if under juvenile justice jurisdiction

### OAFC Service Model (from CCRB Narrative v4)
- Dual-county design: Harris County (urban) + Austin County (rural)
- Juvenile Drug Treatment Court enhancement
- Core components: MAT via telehealth (SRS), Credible Messenger mentorship (CMU), Certified Family Partner navigation (CMU), Contingency Management
- OJJDP JDTC Guidelines framework (7 objectives, 28 implementation guidelines)
- Budget: $749,565 with personnel, fringe, travel, supplies, voluntary 10% indirect rate
- Target: justice-involved youth with SUD, screened via CRAFFT/validated tools
- Target volume: 50 youth/year (150 over 3 years)

### OAFC Skin Paragraph

OAFC funding operates the Court-Connected Recovery Bridge (CCRB) opioid abatement service lane for justice-involved transitional age youth ages 12-24 in Harris and Austin Counties. This project implements a 90-day stabilization workflow followed by 12 months of Youth Recovery Community (YRC) continuing care, integrating MAT via telehealth for eligible youth ages 16+, peer-delivered recovery coaching using MET/CBT and contingency management, and family-level stabilization through Certified Family Partner education, navigation, and home-based supports. CCRB maintains distinct cost objectives and reimbursement documentation ensuring OAFC funds are not used for any cost reimbursed by another funding source, consistent with OAFC non-duplication requirements.

### Compliance Notes
- OAFC non-duplication: grant funds may not be used for costs reimbursed by another funding source (NOFA Para 140)
- TxGMS quarterly reporting
- Strategy alignment documentation required
- 34 TAC 16.205 eligibility requirements
- Reimbursement documentation per NOFA Para 531-537

### Hogg Skin Paragraph

Hogg Foundation TCBRI funding will build the non-clinical organizational capacity that enables CMU's braided service delivery across multiple grant-funded programs serving justice-involved youth. This investment funds a Training Coordinator position anchoring multi-site SCBF 2.0 curriculum delivery, restorative practice facilitation, and the credential stacking pipeline that develops Credible Messengers from community mentors into certified recovery specialists. Hogg dollars will produce durable capacity assets — supervision protocols, training manuals, quality assurance systems, and data infrastructure — that strengthen community mental health and well-being by ensuring the people closest to the problem have the organizational tools to sustain their work. All Hogg-funded activities are explicitly non-clinical and coded to capacity-only cost objectives, with auditable separation from OAFC-funded direct services and OJJDP-funded reentry programming.
